A relative pronoun lets you fuse two sentences into one by turning the second into a description that hangs off a noun in the first — instead of *Tengo un libro. El libro es interesante*, you say *Tengo un libro que es interesante*. English does this job with "that," "which," "who," and "whose," switching between them mostly by feel. Spanish spreads the same job across a smaller, more systematic set of words, and picking the right one depends on three things at once: what you're describing (a person or a thing), whether the description is essential to identifying it, and — in careful writing especially — what preposition, if any, sits directly in front of it. This chapter builds that system from the one word that covers most cases up through the register-driven choices that still trip up confident intermediate speakers.

## Core Pattern: Que — the default relative pronoun
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Que* is the workhorse relative pronoun: it refers to people or things equally, it never changes for gender or number, and it can be the subject or the direct object of the clause it introduces. When in doubt, *que* is the right first guess.

<ExampleSentence es="El hombre que llegó tarde es mi jefe." en="The man who arrived late is my boss." note="que is the subject of llegó — and refers to a person, not just a thing." />
<ExampleSentence es="El libro que compré es muy interesante." en="The book that I bought is very interesting." note="Here que is the direct object of compré." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
As a relative pronoun, *que* never carries a written accent. That matters because *qué* — with an accent — is a completely different word, the interrogative "what," covered in [Chapter 18](../lesson-18). They sound identical but are never interchangeable in writing.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Quien and Donde

*Que* covers the default case, but two more relative pronouns split off to handle narrower jobs: *quien(es)* restricts itself to people, and *donde* restricts itself to place — both are still built on the same basic logic of standing in for an already-mentioned noun.

### Quien / quienes — people only, and required after a preposition
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Quien* (plural *quienes*) refers exclusively to people. It agrees in number but never in gender. Its most important job is standing in for *que* directly after a preposition when the antecedent is a person — Spanish doesn't accept a bare *que* there:

<ExampleSentence es="La persona con quien hablé ayer es mi vecina." en="The person I spoke with yesterday is my neighbor." note="con que would sound wrong here; a preposition + person requires quien." />
<ExampleSentence es="Los estudiantes a quienes llamé no contestaron." en="The students I called didn't answer." note="quienes agrees in number (plural) with estudiantes, but not gender." />

Notice that Spanish also can't do what English does in casual speech — strand the preposition at the end ("the person I spoke *with*"). The preposition and *quien* move together, in front of the verb:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "con", role: "connector" },
    { text: "quien", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "hablé", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>

*Quien* can also open a non-restrictive clause about a person without any preposition at all (an alternative to *que* there), and it can stand completely alone, with no antecedent, meaning "whoever" — common in proverbs and set expressions:

<ExampleSentence es="Mi hermano, quien vive en Madrid, viene a visitarnos." en="My brother, who lives in Madrid, is coming to visit us." />
<ExampleSentence es="Quien mal anda, mal acaba." en="Whoever lives badly, ends badly (roughly: as you sow, so shall you reap)." note="No antecedent at all — quien here means 'whoever,' a common structure in sayings." />

### Donde — the relative pronoun of place
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Donde* replaces *que* (plus a preposition like *en*) when the antecedent is a place:

<ExampleSentence es="La ciudad donde nací es muy pequeña." en="The city where I was born is very small." note="Equivalent to la ciudad en que nací — donde simply packages the idea of 'in which' into one word for places." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Adonde* (or *a donde*) does the same job when the verb involves motion toward the place: *el pueblo adonde vamos* ("the town we're going to"). In everyday usage the distinction is soft — *donde* alone is also heard with motion verbs — but *adonde* is the more precise choice when "to" is part of the meaning.
</GrammarNote>

### Restrictive vs. non-restrictive clauses: when a comma changes the meaning
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

A relative clause either narrows down which noun you mean (*restrictive*, no commas) or adds extra, non-essential information about a noun that's already fully identified (*non-restrictive*, set off by commas). In Spanish, exactly as in English, the comma isn't decorative — leaving it out or putting it in changes what the sentence actually claims:

<ExampleSentence es="Los estudiantes que aprobaron el examen recibieron un premio." en="The students who passed the exam received a prize." note="Restrictive, no commas: only some students passed, and only those got a prize." />
<ExampleSentence es="Los estudiantes, que aprobaron el examen, recibieron un premio." en="The students, who passed the exam, received a prize." note="Non-restrictive, commas: all the students (already a known, fixed group) passed, and all of them got a prize — que here just adds a side fact." />

Both versions are grammatically fine sentences; they simply describe different situations. *Que* works in both. *Quien* can also open a non-restrictive clause about people (as shown above), but it's a poor fit for restrictive clauses about people in careful usage — *que* is the natural restrictive choice even for people (*los estudiantes que aprobaron...*, not *quienes aprobaron* as the default).

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### El que / el cual: the family that agrees, and shows up after prepositions
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

*El que, la que, los que, las que* — and their more formal cousins *el cual, la cual, los cuales, las cuales* — are the only relative pronouns in this chapter that agree in both gender and number with their antecedent. That agreement makes them useful in three situations where invariable *que* falls short:

**After a preposition, especially a longer one.** A single-syllable preposition (*en, con, de*) tolerates *que* or *el que* fairly interchangeably in speech, but longer prepositions push toward *el que* or *el cual*:

<ExampleSentence es="El bolígrafo con el que escribo era de mi abuelo." en="The pen I'm writing with belonged to my grandfather." />
<ExampleSentence es="La razón por la cual llegué tarde es complicada." en="The reason I arrived late is complicated." note="por la cual agrees feminine singular with razón." />

**To disambiguate between two possible antecedents of different genders.** Invariable *que* can't tell you which noun it points back to; a gendered form can:

<ExampleSentence es="La hermana de mi amigo, la cual vive en Sevilla, nos visitará." en="My friend's sister, who lives in Seville, will visit us." note="la cual is feminine, so it unambiguously points to hermana, not to amigo — que alone would leave that open." />

**Standing alone as "the one(s) who/which."** *El que* (unlike *el cual*) works comfortably with no explicit antecedent at all, functioning as its own noun phrase:

<ExampleSentence es="El que llega primero gana." en="Whoever/the one who arrives first wins." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Don't reach for *el cual* by default just because it sounds more sophisticated. In everyday spoken and written Castellano, *que* alone is correct and far more natural after short prepositions and in most restrictive clauses. Overusing *el cual* reads as stiff, even bureaucratic, outside formal writing.
</GrammarNote>

### Cuyo: the possessive relative — and a genuine trap for English speakers
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

*Cuyo/cuya/cuyos/cuyas* means "whose." Its behavior catches English speakers off guard: it agrees with the noun that comes right after it — the thing *possessed* — never with the possessor that comes before it.

<ExampleSentence es="La mujer cuyo perro ladra toda la noche vive al lado." en="The woman whose dog barks all night lives next door." note="cuyo agrees with perro (masculine), even though mujer (the possessor) is feminine." />
<ExampleSentence es="El escritor cuyas novelas ganaron el premio es argentino." en="The writer whose novels won the prize is Argentine." note="cuyas agrees with novelas (feminine plural), regardless of escritor's gender." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
English "whose" never changes form, so learners instinctively want *cuyo* to agree with the possessor instead — writing *cuya perro* to match a feminine *mujer*. Resist that instinct: check the noun immediately after *cuyo*, not the noun before it.
</GrammarNote>

*Cuyo* is also a strongly formal, mostly written word. Everyday spoken Castellano tends to route around it entirely, either restructuring the sentence or leaning on a colloquial (prescriptively frowned-on, but extremely common) *que...su* pattern: *la mujer que su perro ladra toda la noche*. Recognize this construction when you hear it, but *cuyo* remains the correct form for anything you write.

### Choosing between que, quien, and el que/el cual: a working framework
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Faced with a relative clause, work through it in this order: default to *que*. If the antecedent is a person and a preposition comes right before the pronoun, switch to *quien(es)*. If the sentence needs gender agreement — because a preposition (especially a long one) makes bare *que* awkward, or because two possible antecedents need disambiguating — reach for *el que* (everyday register) or *el cual* (formal register). Reserve *cuyo* for possession, agreeing it with what's possessed, and expect it mainly in writing. Use *donde* whenever the antecedent is a place.

### C1: *El cual*'s stronghold in formal written register
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

In academic, legal, and other high-formality written Spanish, *el cual* becomes close to obligatory — not just preferred — directly after compound or multisyllable prepositions and prepositional phrases: *mediante el cual, a través del cual, en virtud de lo cual, según lo cual*. In these contexts *que* or even *el que* would read as noticeably too informal for the register, even though either would be perfectly natural in speech.

<ExampleSentence es="El acuerdo, mediante el cual se resolvió el conflicto, fue firmado ayer." en="The agreement, through which the conflict was resolved, was signed yesterday." note="Formal written register — mediante el que would be unusual here, and mediante que ungrammatical." />

## Bringing It Together

Given a sentence to build, ask first whether the antecedent is a person, a thing, or a place — that picks between *quien*, *que*, and *donde*. Then check whether a preposition sits directly in front of the pronoun: no preposition, stay with *que* (or *quien* for people); a short preposition, *que* or *el que* both work in speech; a long preposition or formal writing, move to *el que* or *el cual*. Save *cuyo* for possession, and remember it agrees with what's owned, not the owner.

<ExampleSentence es="El vecino cuya hija, quien estudia en la universidad donde trabajo, me prestó el libro que estoy leyendo." en="The neighbor whose daughter, who studies at the university where I work, lent me the book that I'm reading." note="A sentence deliberately stacking cuya (possession), quien (non-restrictive, person), donde (place), and que (restrictive, thing) in one breath." />
