Spanish has two everyday ways to talk about the past, and English speakers often assume they're interchangeable: the preterite (*comí*) and the present perfect (*he comido*). They aren't quite the same tool. The pretérito perfecto — *haber* conjugated in the present tense plus a past participle — frames a past action as connected to *now*: something that happened within a stretch of time that hasn't closed yet (today, this week, this year, "ever" in your life), or whose result is still felt in the present moment. It's also one of the most important tenses to get a feel for if you're learning Castellano specifically, because Spain reaches for it far more often, and in more everyday contexts, than most of Latin America does — a genuine regional split this chapter covers in depth below.

## Core Pattern: Haber (Present Tense) + Past Participle

### Haber as auxiliary — and only as auxiliary, here
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

You already met *haber's* present-tense forms in [Chapter 23](../lesson-23) as one of Spanish's four fully irregular verbs. This chapter is where they earn their keep: in everyday Spanish, *he, has, ha, hemos, habéis, han* function almost exclusively as the **auxiliary verb** that builds the present perfect — they don't mean "to have" in the sense of owning something (that job belongs entirely to *tener*), and they don't mean "there is/are" either (see the next section).

<BootTable
  label="haber"
  caption="present tense — used here only as the perfect-tense auxiliary"
  forms={{
    "yo": "he",
    "tú": "has",
    "él/ella/usted": "ha",
    "nosotros": "hemos",
    "vosotros": "habéis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "han"
  }}
/>

Look at the shape of that irregularity: *he, has, ha,* and *han* are worn down almost past recognition — nothing in them obviously points back to an infinitive "haber." *Hemos* and *habéis*, by contrast, still carry the recognizable *hab-* stem. That's a genuine boot outline, the same logic as [Chapter 23's](../lesson-23) stem-changing verbs: the four cells around the outside erode down to something unpredictable, while nosotros/vosotros stay closer to the base form.

<ExampleSentence es="He comido paella dos veces esta semana." en="I've eaten paella twice this week." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Habéis visto la nueva película?" en="Have you all seen the new movie?" />

### Two unrelated jobs, one shared ancestor: he/has/ha vs. hay
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Don't let *haber's* two jobs blur together. The six personal forms above (*he, has, ha, hemos, habéis, han*) are exclusively the perfect-tense auxiliary — "I have," "you have," etc., only ever glued to a participle. The invariant word *hay* ("there is / there are") is a completely separate, frozen existential form: it never changes for person or number, and it never combines with a participle to build a tense. Historically both come from the same Latin verb, but in modern Spanish they've split into two jobs that never overlap.
</GrammarNote>

<ExampleSentence es="Hay dos cartas en la mesa." en="There are two letters on the table." note="Existential hay — no person, no participle, completely invariant." />
<ExampleSentence es="He escrito dos cartas." en="I've written two letters." note="Auxiliary he — agrees with yo, and requires a participle to mean anything." />

*Hay* gets its own fuller treatment alongside Spanish's other impersonal constructions in [Chapter 47](../lesson-47); for this chapter, the only thing to hold onto is that it is never a stand-in for *he/has/ha...*, and vice versa.

### Forming the regular past participle: -ado / -ido
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

For any regular verb, drop the infinitive ending and add *-ado* (for *-ar* verbs) or *-ido* (for *-er*/*-ir* verbs). This participle never changes shape once it's built — more on that below.

| Infinitive | Past Participle |
|---|---|
| hablar | hablado |
| comprar | comprado |
| comer | comido |
| vivir | vivido |

<ExampleSentence es="Todavía no he terminado el informe." en="I still haven't finished the report." note="No sits before he, not between he and terminado — nothing can wedge itself inside the haber + participle unit." />

## Building Out: The Irregular Participles

### The core irregular-participle list
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

A small set of extremely common verbs refuse the regular *-ado/-ido* pattern entirely. There are only ten of them at the core, and because they're so frequent, they're worth memorizing outright rather than trying to pattern-match your way to them:

| Infinitive | Irregular Participle | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| escribir | escrito | written |
| hacer | hecho | done / made |
| decir | dicho | said |
| ver | visto | seen |
| poner | puesto | put / placed |
| romper | roto | broken |
| volver | vuelto | returned |
| morir | muerto | died |
| abrir | abierto | opened |
| cubrir | cubierto | covered |

<ExampleSentence es="¿Quién ha roto el jarrón?" en="Who broke the vase?" />
<ExampleSentence es="Todavía no hemos abierto los regalos." en="We still haven't opened the presents." />

### Compounds inherit the irregularity
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The same logic extends past the ten base verbs: any verb built on top of one of them as a prefix keeps the irregular participle underneath. You don't need to memorize these separately — just recognize the base verb inside the compound.

- *descubrir* (des- + *cubrir*) → **descubierto**
- *devolver* (de- + *volver*) → **devuelto**
- *deshacer* (des- + *hacer*) → **deshecho**
- *describir* (de- + *escribir*) → **descrito**
- *componer* (com- + *poner*) → **compuesto**

<ExampleSentence es="La policía ha descubierto la verdad." en="The police have discovered the truth." note="descubrir inherits cubrir's irregular participle: descubierto." />

### Participles that need a written accent: leído, traído, oído, creído, caído
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A handful of *-er*/*-ir* verbs whose stem ends in a vowel (*leer, traer, oír, creer, caer*) are technically **regular** — they still take *-ido* — but the resulting *-ído* needs a written accent to keep the *i* and the preceding vowel from collapsing into a diphthong. This is an orthographic adjustment, not a true irregularity, in the same spirit as *coger → cojo* from [Chapter 23](../lesson-23).

<ExampleSentence es="No he leído ese libro todavía." en="I haven't read that book yet." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### The regional split: Spain's present perfect vs. Latin America's preterite
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
This is one of the most practically important regional differences for a Castellano-focused learner. For an event that happened today, or within a time frame the speaker still considers "open" (this week, this year, this morning), Peninsular Spanish — especially the Madrid-centered and northern norm — reaches for the present perfect by default: **"Hoy he comido paella."** Most of Latin American Spanish, by contrast, treats the same event with the preterite: **"Hoy comí paella."** Both sentences are completely grammatical everywhere in the Spanish-speaking world — this is a difference of default and frequency, not correctness. Spain's usage also extends further than just "today": in casual Peninsular speech, the present perfect often shows up for anything felt as recent, even without an explicit "today"-type time word, where most of Latin America would still default to preterite. Interestingly, the split isn't strictly "Spain vs. Latin America" either — the Canary Islands and parts of Andalusia lean toward the Latin American preterite-preference pattern too, so "Castellano usage" here really describes the central and northern Peninsular norm most associated with Madrid.
</GrammarNote>

<ExampleSentence es="Hoy he comido paella." en="I ate paella today." note="Natural default in Madrid and northern/central Spain." />
<ExampleSentence es="Hoy comí paella." en="I ate paella today." note="Natural default across most of Latin America — equally grammatical, just a different everyday choice." />

### Participle agreement: invariant with haber, but agrees as an adjective
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Inside a present-perfect (or any *haber* + participle compound tense), the participle is **completely frozen** — it never agrees with the subject or a direct object, no matter their gender or number. This trips up learners who over-apply the adjective-agreement instinct from [Chapter 4](../lesson-04):

<ExampleSentence es="Hemos escrito las cartas." en="We have written the letters." note="Escrito never becomes escritas here, even though las cartas is feminine plural — the participle inside a compound tense doesn't agree with anything." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
"He escritas" or "hemos escritos" are always wrong, in any person, with any object. The invariant *-o* form is the only form a participle takes when it's glued to *haber*.
</GrammarNote>

The moment that same participle steps outside a *haber* construction and works as a plain adjective, the ordinary agreement rules snap back on — this is a genuinely different grammatical job wearing the same word:

<ExampleSentence es="Las cartas escritas están en el cajón." en="The written letters are in the drawer." note="Here escritas modifies cartas directly, like any adjective — full agreement applies, because there's no haber involved at all." />

This same split shows up with *estar* + participle, which describes a resulting state (and agrees, just like an adjective) rather than building a compound tense at all — a contrast previewed back in [Chapter 21](../lesson-21). [Chapter 44](../lesson-44) is the participle's true home as its own grammatical category — this chapter's list is complete for present-perfect purposes, but Chapter 44 covers the participle-as-adjective and absolute-construction uses (*terminada la reunión...*) in more depth.

### Clitic pronouns always precede the whole haber + participle unit
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

Object and reflexive pronouns attach *before* the entire *haber* + participle block — never between the two pieces, and never after the participle the way English tacks an object on at the end.

<ExampleSentence es="Lo he hecho ya." en="I've already done it." note="Lo attaches before he; nothing goes between he and hecho, and nothing follows hecho either. English 'I have done it' places the object after the participle — Spanish never does." />

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Esta semana he escrito tres cartas, he roto dos borradores y todavía no he abierto el correo de hoy." en="This week I've written three letters, torn up two drafts, and still haven't opened today's mail." note="Three different irregular participles (escrito, roto, abierto), all frozen in form, all built on the auxiliary he — a today's-events framing typical of Peninsular Spanish." />

Faced with a past action, ask: does it belong to a stretch of time that's still open, or whose result still matters right now? If so, and especially if you're aiming for a Spain-flavored register, the present perfect is very often the natural choice — *haber* in the present, plus a participle that never bends to agree with anything once it's inside that construction.
