Every preposition needs something after it — a noun, or a pronoun standing in for one. English reuses the same object pronouns everywhere ("for me," "with me," "than me"), but Spanish reserves a dedicated set specifically for the slot after a preposition, and that set isn't quite identical to any pronoun group you've already learned. Most of it is a near-copy of the subject pronouns from [Chapter 11](../lesson-11), with two small but essential changes in the first and second person singular — plus one genuinely irregular fusion, born centuries ago and never smoothed out, that native speakers use every day without a second thought: *conmigo*, "with me."

## Core Pattern: The Six Prepositional Pronoun Forms

### Meet the set
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Prepositional pronouns fill the familiar six-way grid. Compare them side by side with the subject pronouns from Chapter 11 and notice that only *yo* and *tú* actually change shape:

<BootTable
  label="Prepositional Pronouns"
  forms={{
    "yo": "mí",
    "tú": "ti",
    "él/ella/usted": "él / ella / usted",
    "nosotros": "nosotros / nosotras",
    "vosotros": "vosotros / vosotras",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "ellos / ellas / ustedes"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Este regalo es para mí." en="This gift is for me." />
<ExampleSentence es="No puedo salir sin ti." en="I can't go out without you." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Mí* carries a written accent to distinguish it from the possessive *mi* (*mi hermano* vs. *es para mí*) — the two words are pronounced identically but mean completely different things. *Ti* never takes an accent; there's no unaccented *ti* it could ever be confused with, so the accent mark simply isn't needed.
</GrammarNote>

### Use after common prepositions
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Almost any preposition that governs a noun governs one of these pronouns the same way: *de, en, para, por, sin, sobre, contra, hacia, desde, ante, tras...*

<ExampleSentence es="No sé nada de él." en="I don't know anything about him." />
<ExampleSentence es="El regalo es para nosotros, no para ellos." en="The gift is for us, not for them." />
<ExampleSentence es="Confío en ti más que en nadie." en="I trust you more than anyone." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
These forms only ever appear after a preposition — never as a subject, and never standing alone as an answer. "Who's there?" gets answered *Yo* ("me," using the subject pronoun), never *Mí*. If there's no preposition directly in front of it, *mí/ti* is the wrong choice.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: The Conmigo/Contigo/Consigo Fusion

### The irregular fusion: conmigo, contigo, consigo
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Con* ("with") is the one preposition that refuses to combine with *mí* and *ti* the ordinary way. Instead of *con mí* and *con ti*, Spanish fuses them into single irregular words — a fossil left over from Latin, where "with me" and "with you" were built by tacking *-cum* ("with") onto the end of the pronoun (*mecum, tecum*). Centuries later, speakers stopped recognizing *-cum* as meaning "with" on its own, so they added a second, redundant *con* to the front — and the doubled-up result is what stuck:

| Person | Fused form |
|---|---|
| yo | conmigo |
| tú | contigo |
| él/ella/usted, ellos/ellas/ustedes (reflexive only — see below) | consigo |
| él/ella/usted, nosotros, vosotros, ellos/ellas/ustedes (non-reflexive) | con él / con nosotros / con vosotros / con ellos... |

<ExampleSentence es="¿Quieres venir conmigo al cine?" en="Do you want to come with me to the movies?" />
<ExampleSentence es="Siempre trae su perro contigo — perdón, contigo no, ¡conmigo!" en="He always brings his dog with — sorry, not with you, with me!" note="A reminder that conmigo/contigo are single fused words, not con + a separate pronoun you could swap pieces of." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Only *yo* and *tú* fuse this way with *con*. Every other person just uses *con* plus the ordinary prepositional pronoun: *con él, con nosotros, con vosotras, con ellos* — no fusion, no irregularity.
</GrammarNote>

### Consigo: reflexive only
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

*Consigo* is the third fused form, but it has a narrower job than *conmigo/contigo*: it's used only when "with him/her/you/them" refers back to the *same* person who is the subject of the sentence — a reflexive relationship, in the same spirit as [Chapter 15](../lesson-15)'s reflexive pronouns. When the object of *con* is a *different* third person from the subject, Spanish uses the ordinary, non-fused *con él/con ella/con ellos* instead:

<ExampleSentence es="Lleva las llaves consigo a todas partes." en="She carries the keys with her everywhere." note="Consigo — the keys go with the same person who's carrying them (the subject herself)." />
<ExampleSentence es="María fue al cine con él." en="María went to the movies with him." note="Con él, not consigo — 'him' is someone other than María, the subject." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
*Consigo* leans formal and literary in everyday conversation; colloquial Castellano often reaches for *con él mismo/ella misma* instead to make the reflexive sense unmistakable. You'll still see and hear plain *consigo* constantly, especially in writing.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Prepositional pronouns vs. subject pronouns after que
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

[Chapter 5](../lesson-05) flagged this contrast and depends on it, so it's worth stating cleanly here: a true preposition — *para, sin, de, con, contra*, and the rest — always takes a prepositional pronoun (*mí, ti*). But *que*, in a comparison like *más alto que yo*, isn't a true preposition at all; it introduces an elliptical clause (*más alto que yo [soy alto]*, "taller than I am tall"), and clauses take subject pronouns, not prepositional ones:

<ExampleSentence es="Corre más rápido que yo." en="He runs faster than I do." note="Que yo, never que mí — que is a conjunction here, not a preposition." />
<ExampleSentence es="Este regalo es para mí, no para ti." en="This gift is for me, not for you." note="Contrast: para is a true preposition, so it takes mí/ti exactly as expected." />

The test is mechanical, not a matter of feel: if you could restate the phrase as a clause with an implied verb (*que yo [corro]*), use the subject pronoun; if the word genuinely governs the pronoun the way a preposition governs a noun, use *mí/ti*.

### A few words that look like prepositions but take subject pronouns
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

*Que* isn't alone. A small set of prepositions — *entre* (between), *según* (according to), and the near-conjunctions *excepto/salvo/menos* (except) — also break the *mí/ti* pattern and take subject pronouns instead, for reasons rooted in their individual histories rather than one shared rule:

<ExampleSentence es="Entre tú y yo, no confío en su plan." en="Between you and me, I don't trust his plan." note="Entre tú y yo, never entre ti y mí — one of the most common phrases in this exception group." />
<ExampleSentence es="Según tú, el examen fue fácil." en="According to you, the exam was easy." />
<ExampleSentence es="Todos vinieron excepto yo." en="Everyone came except me." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This is a short, closed list — don't extend the pattern to prepositions not named here. *Para, sin, de, con, contra, sobre, hacia,* and the large majority of Spanish prepositions all take *mí/ti* exactly as the core pattern predicts; *entre, según,* and *excepto/salvo/menos* are the memorable exceptions, not the rule.
</GrammarNote>

### Redundant, emphatic doubling: a mí, a ti...
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Some verbs grammatically require an indirect object pronoun even in an ordinary, unemphatic sentence — *gustar* and its whole family are the most common case, covered fully in [Chapter 24](../lesson-24), but the same mechanic shows up with *doler, interesar,* and any verb that uses an IO pronoun (see [Chapters 13](../lesson-13)–[14](../lesson-14)). When you want to add emphasis, contrast, or clarification, Spanish doesn't replace that IO pronoun — it adds *a* plus the matching prepositional pronoun alongside it:

<ExampleSentence es="A mí me gusta el chocolate, pero a él no le gusta." en="I like chocolate, but he doesn't." note="A mí and a él add contrast between two people — me and le are still both required." />
<ExampleSentence es="A ti te toca ahora." en="It's your turn now." note="A ti clarifies/emphasizes who te already refers to." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*A mí* never stands in for *me* — you can't say *A mí gusta el chocolate* and drop *me*. The prepositional phrase only ever doubles the pronoun that's already doing the grammatical work; it's decoration for emphasis, not a substitute. [Chapter 24](../lesson-24) covers why *gustar*-type verbs need that IO pronoun there in the first place.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="A mí me encanta viajar, así que le pregunté a mi hermana si quería venir conmigo. Entre tú y yo, no esperaba que dijera que sí — pero al final decidió que, según ella, un viaje así solo se hace una vez, y no quería perdérselo por quedarse en casa sin mí." en="I love traveling, so I asked my sister if she wanted to come with me. Between you and me, I didn't expect her to say yes — but in the end she decided that, according to her, a trip like this only happens once, and she didn't want to miss it by staying home without me." note="Emphatic a mí, the conmigo fusion, the entre tú y yo and según ella exceptions, and an ordinary sin mí, all in one passage." />

Prepositional pronouns are a small, closed system, and one question resolves almost every choice: is a true preposition governing this pronoun (use *mí/ti*, or the fused *conmigo/contigo/consigo* after *con*), or is *que/entre/según/excepto* involved instead (use the subject pronoun)?
