Spanish has a dedicated future tense — one word, not a helper verb plus an infinitive — for talking about what will happen: hablaré, comerás, vivirá. English speakers often reach first for a Spanish equivalent of "going to," and that construction exists too (more on it below), but the futuro simple isn't a fallback or an afterthought: it's the tense Spanish reaches for in predictions, promises, formal writing, and news headlines. It also has a second job that has nothing to do with future time at all — guessing what's probably true right now — and that job is used so constantly in everyday speech that this chapter gives it real weight, not a footnote.

## Core Pattern: The Whole Infinitive as the Stem

### Regular verbs: infinitive + one shared ending set
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

For a regular verb in any of the three conjugations, the future is built by taking the entire infinitive — unchanged, not trimmed down to a stem the way the present tense is — and attaching one of six endings. This is a genuine simplification: there's no stem to sort out, and unlike the present tense, the same six endings attach identically whether the verb is -ar, -er, or -ir.

<BootTable
  label="hablar"
  caption="future — fully regular, no boot outline"
  forms={{
    "yo": "hablaré",
    "tú": "hablarás",
    "él/ella/usted": "hablará",
    "nosotros": "hablaremos",
    "vosotros": "hablaréis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "hablarán"
  }}
/>

The same six endings — -é, -ás, -á, -emos, -éis, -án — attach to *comer* and *vivir* exactly as they do to *hablar*, with no ending swap between conjugations:

| | hablar | comer | vivir |
|---|---|---|---|
| yo | hablaré | comeré | viviré |
| tú | hablarás | comerás | vivirás |
| él/ella/usted | hablará | comerá | vivirá |
| nosotros | hablaremos | comeremos | viviremos |
| vosotros | hablaréis | comeréis | viviréis |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | hablarán | comerán | vivirán |

<ExampleSentence es="Mañana hablaré con el jefe sobre el proyecto." en="Tomorrow I'll talk to the boss about the project." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿A qué hora volveréis de la excursión?" en="What time will you all get back from the trip?" />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Every future-tense form carries a written accent on the ending itself — hablaré, hablarás, hablará, hablaréis, hablarán — except nosotros. That's not an inconsistency: hablaremos already places the natural stress exactly where Spanish's regular stress rules would put it anyway, so it's the one form in the set that never needs the accent mark to redirect anything.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: The 12 Irregular Stems, Grouped by Pattern

The endings above never change — not even for the verbs in this section. What changes is the stem they attach to: twelve common verbs replace all or part of the infinitive with an irregular stem before taking the exact same six endings. Twelve looks like a lot to memorize at first glance, but they sort cleanly into three small families, and recognizing the family tells you the pattern.

### The d-insertion group: tener, poner, salir, venir, valer
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Five verbs drop the infinitive's final vowel and squeeze in a *-d-* before the endings attach:

| Infinitive | Stem | yo | nosotros |
|---|---|---|---|
| tener (to have) | tendr- | tendré | tendremos |
| poner (to put) | pondr- | pondré | pondremos |
| salir (to leave) | saldr- | saldré | saldremos |
| venir (to come) | vendr- | vendré | vendremos |
| valer (to be worth) | valdr- | valdré | valdremos |

<BootTable
  label="tener"
  caption="future — same irregular stem in all six forms"
  forms={{
    "yo": "tendré",
    "tú": "tendrás",
    "él/ella/usted": "tendrá",
    "nosotros": "tendremos",
    "vosotros": "tendréis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "tendrán"
  }}
/>

As with the preterite's irregular stems ([Chapter 27](../lesson-27)) and *haber's* present-tense forms as the perfect-tense auxiliary ([Chapter 30](../lesson-30)), there's no boot outline to draw here: the irregularity is the stem itself, and every one of the six forms is built on that same irregular *tendr-*. This is now a pattern worth recognizing on sight across chapters — whenever an irregularity replaces the whole stem rather than shifting a vowel in just four of six cells, expect a plain grid, not a boot.

<ExampleSentence es="¿Vendréis a la boda el sábado?" en="Will you all come to the wedding on Saturday?" />
<ExampleSentence es="No cabremos todos en el coche — tendremos que pedir un taxi." en="We won't all fit in the car — we'll have to call a taxi." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Notice the family: tener, poner, salir, and venir are exactly the four verbs whose present-tense yo-form you already learned adds an irregular -g- — tengo, pongo, salgo, vengo ([Chapter 23](../lesson-23)). Different consonant, same instinct: Spanish keeps resisting a bare vowel-final stem in front of certain endings. If you already have the g-insertion yo-forms down, you have a head start on these d-insertion future stems.
</GrammarNote>

### The vowel-drop group: poder, saber, caber, haber, querer
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Five more verbs simply drop the infinitive's -e- before attaching the endings, with no consonant inserted:

| Infinitive | Stem | yo | nosotros |
|---|---|---|---|
| poder (to be able to) | podr- | podré | podremos |
| saber (to know) | sabr- | sabré | sabremos |
| caber (to fit) | cabr- | cabré | cabremos |
| haber (auxiliary "to have") | habr- | habré | habremos |
| querer (to want) | querr- | querré | querremos |

<ExampleSentence es="No sabré la respuesta hasta mañana." en="I won't know the answer until tomorrow." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Querrás acompañarme al médico?" en="Will you want to come with me to the doctor?" />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Querer drops its e the same way as the rest of this group, but keeps a double r — querré, not "queré" — because a single r sitting between two vowels would soften to a light tap instead of the trilled r the verb needs. That's a spelling adjustment to protect the sound, not a separate irregularity to memorize on top of the vowel drop.
</GrammarNote>

Hold onto *habré* and its siblings — you'll meet this exact stem again as the auxiliary that builds the futuro compuesto in [Chapter 35](../lesson-35). For now, it behaves here just like any other verb in this group.

### The truncation group: decir and hacer
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The last two verbs cut further than either group above: *decir* loses everything but the letter d, and *hacer* loses everything but the letter h, before the endings attach.

| Infinitive | Stem | yo | nosotros |
|---|---|---|---|
| decir (to say/tell) | dir- | diré | diremos |
| hacer (to do/make) | har- | haré | haremos |

<ExampleSentence es="Te diré la verdad cuando llegue el momento." en="I'll tell you the truth when the moment comes." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Qué harás este fin de semana?" en="What will you do this weekend?" />

Only two verbs belong to this group, and their stems don't reduce to a shared rule the way the two groups above do — they're simplest to just memorize outright.

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Future for conjecture: what's probably true right now
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The futuro simple has a second job that has nothing to do with future time at all: expressing a guess about the present. English handles this with "must be," "probably," or "I bet" — Spanish just uses the future tense of the verb in question, with no extra word required.

<ExampleSentence es="Son las diez y María no ha llegado. Estará en el tráfico." en="It's ten o'clock and María hasn't arrived. She's probably stuck in traffic." note="Estará doesn't point to a future event at all — it's a guess about right now." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Quién llama a esta hora? Será tu hermano." en="Who's calling at this hour? It's probably your brother." />
<ExampleSentence es="Tendrá unos treinta años." en="He's probably around thirty." />

This use — sometimes called the "future of probability" — is extremely common in everyday speech, not a rare literary flourish. Any time you'd say "probably," "must be," or "I wonder if" about a present situation in English, try reaching for the futuro simple instead of a separate word for "probably."

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The form is identical to a genuine future statement — only meaning and context tell the two apart. "Estará en casa mañana" (he'll be home tomorrow) is a true future; "estará en casa ahora mismo" (he's probably home right now) is a guess about the present. A nearby time word, or the lack of one, is usually the clue.
</GrammarNote>

### Two ways to talk about the future: ir a + infinitivo
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Spanish also builds a future meaning without this chapter's tense at all, using a helper verb: *ir* (conjugated in the present — voy, vas, va, vamos, vais, van, [Chapter 23](../lesson-23)) + *a* + infinitive.

<ExampleSentence es="Voy a comer paella esta noche." en="I'm going to eat paella tonight." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Vais a venir a la fiesta?" en="Are you all going to come to the party?" />

In everyday speech, *ir a* + infinitive is often the more natural first choice for something already decided or close at hand — much like English "going to" versus "will." The futuro simple, by contrast, leans more toward predictions, promises, formal or written contexts, and — as just covered — conjecture. This is a distinction of register and nuance rather than a hard rule; native speakers use both constructions for plain future meaning constantly, and the line between them blurs in casual conversation.

<ExampleSentence es="Mañana va a llover." en="Tomorrow it's going to rain." note="A near-term, fairly confident prediction — ir a + inf. feels natural here." />
<ExampleSentence es="El año que viene lloverá menos, según los expertos." en="Next year it will rain less, according to experts." note="A more formal, longer-range prediction — futuro simple fits the register." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
In casual speech across the whole Spanish-speaking world, ir a + infinitivo has been steadily edging out the futuro simple for plain future meaning — but don't mistake that for the futuro simple being old-fashioned or purely literary in a Castellano context. Peninsular Spanish keeps it comfortably alive in both speech and writing (weather forecasts, news headlines, everyday promises), more so than its reputation as a "formal/written tense" might suggest.
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="tip">
What's above is only the short version. Ir a + infinitivo is one member of a whole family of verb + link + infinitive/gerund constructions called perífrasis verbales, and [Chapter 45](../lesson-45) is where it gets its full, dedicated treatment — including how it interacts with object pronouns and its own idiomatic extensions. Everything in this section is enough to use it correctly starting today.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="El próximo verano haremos un viaje largo. Saldremos el primero de julio y no volveremos hasta agosto. Todavía no sé cuánto costará, pero supongo que tendremos que ahorrar bastante. Voy a empezar a planear la ruta este fin de semana." en="Next summer we'll take a long trip. We'll leave on July first and won't come back until August. I still don't know how much it will cost, but I imagine we'll have to save quite a bit. I'm going to start planning the route this weekend." note="haremos (truncation group), saldremos and tendremos (d-insertion group), costará (a guess-flavored statement about an unknown amount), voy a empezar (periphrastic future for an already-decided near-term plan)." />

Faced with a verb you need to put in the future, check first whether it's one of the twelve irregular stems above — if not, the whole infinitive plus the ending is all you need. If what you're actually doing is guessing at something true right now rather than stating something that hasn't happened yet, the same form does that job too. And if the moment feels closer, more decided, or more conversational than a formal prediction, ir a + infinitive is usually the more natural pick.
