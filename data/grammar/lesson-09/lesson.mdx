By now you already know the Spanish number words themselves — *uno, dos, tres, primero, segundo* — the same way you know *mesa* or *comer*. What this chapter covers isn't the words but their grammar: the handful of places where a number behaves like an adjective and has to agree with the noun it counts, the places where it shortens before landing on a noun, and one genuinely surprising fact about how Spanish speakers handle ordinals once you get past tenth — a rule most textbooks bury or skip, but that you'll hear constantly the moment you listen to native speakers talk about centuries, kings, and anniversaries.

## Core Pattern: Cardinal Numbers That Agree

### Uno becomes un before a masculine noun
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Uno* is the only cardinal number from 1-99 that changes form at all — and it does so constantly, because "one" modifies a noun so often. Directly before a masculine singular noun, *uno* shortens to *un*; before a feminine noun, it becomes *una*. This is the exact same word as the indefinite article *un/una* (more on that overlap below), which is no coincidence — "a/an" and "one" trace back to the same root in Spanish just as in English.

<ExampleSentence es="Tengo un hermano y una hermana." en="I have one brother and one sister." note="un before hermano (m.), una before hermana (f.) — full agreement, just like an adjective." />

The same shortening applies inside compound numbers ending in *-uno*, at any size:

<ExampleSentence es="Compré treinta y un libros y treinta y una revistas." en="I bought thirty-one books and thirty-one magazines." note="treinta y un before the masculine noun, treinta y una before the feminine — the apocope travels with -uno no matter how large the number." />

### Twenty-one to twenty-nine: veintiuno becomes veintiún
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The numbers sixteen through twenty-nine are written as single fused words in Spanish (*dieciséis, veintidós*), and *veintiuno* follows the exact same un/una shortening as plain *uno*:

<ExampleSentence es="Tiene veintiún años." en="He/she is twenty-one years old." note="veintiuno → veintiún before años (m.); the accent shifts onto the ú to keep the stress in the right place." />
<ExampleSentence es="La empresa tiene veintiuna empleadas en esta oficina." en="The company has twenty-one female employees in this office." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
From thirty-one onward, the number is written as three separate words (*treinta y uno, cuarenta y uno*...) instead of one fused word, but the un/una apocope behaves identically either way — only the spelling convention changes at thirty.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Hundreds Agree Too

The agreement you just learned for *uno* extends — with its own separate mechanics — to the hundreds. *Cien/ciento* is a shortening rule of its own, and the hundred-words 200-900 agree in gender the way an adjective would, something English "two hundred" never does.

### Cien vs. ciento
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Cien* is the short form used directly before a noun, before *mil* (thousand), or before *millón*, and also when counting exactly to one hundred with nothing after it. *Ciento* is the long form used inside a compound number (101-199) where something else follows within the same hundred:

<ExampleSentence es="Hay cien estudiantes en el curso." en="There are a hundred students in the course." />
<ExampleSentence es="Cuento hasta cien: noventa y ocho, noventa y nueve, cien." en="I'll count to a hundred: ninety-eight, ninety-nine, a hundred." note="cien standing alone at the end of a count, with nothing following it." />
<ExampleSentence es="Hay ciento cincuenta estudiantes en el curso." en="There are a hundred and fifty students in the course." note="ciento, not cien, because another number follows it inside the same hundred." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Cien mil* and *cien millones* both use the short form, even though *mil*/*millón* immediately follow — *cien* only lengthens to *ciento* when a smaller number (not *mil*/*millón*) follows within the same hundred: *ciento tres*, but *cien mil*.
</GrammarNote>

### Two hundred, three hundred...: hundreds agree in gender
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Unlike English "two hundred" (fixed, no gender), the Spanish hundred-words from 200-900 change their ending to match the gender of the noun they count:

| Number | Masculine | Feminine |
|---|---|---|
| 200 | doscientos | doscientas |
| 300 | trescientos | trescientas |
| 500 | quinientos | quinientas |
| 700 | setecientos | setecientas |
| 900 | novecientos | novecientas |

<ExampleSentence es="Hay doscientos libros y trescientas revistas en la biblioteca." en="There are two hundred books and three hundred magazines in the library." note="doscientos agrees with libros (m.), trescientas agrees with revistas (f.) — the same counting sentence, two different endings." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Quinientos, setecientos, and novecientos look irregular because they don't build cleanly off cinco/siete/nueve the way doscientos builds off dos — there's no shortcut, they're simply worth memorizing as a set.
</GrammarNote>

### Stacking agreement: when uno and a hundred-word combine
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

In a number like 201 or 351, both the hundred-word and the final *uno* agree independently with the noun, since each piece is doing its own agreement job:

<ExampleSentence es="Doscientas una personas asistieron al evento." en="Two hundred and one people attended the event." note="doscientas agrees with personas, and the final una also agrees separately — both pieces mark gender, not just the first." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: Ordinals

### Ordinal formation and agreement
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Ordinals (*primero, segundo, tercero*...) behave like completely ordinary -o/-a adjectives, agreeing in gender and number with the noun they describe, and — unlike cardinal numbers — they normally come *before* the noun:

<ExampleSentence es="Es la tercera vez que te lo explico." en="It's the third time I'm explaining it to you." note="tercera agrees with vez (f.) and sits before the noun, like a typical descriptive adjective in this position." />

### Apocope: primero and tercero drop -o before a masculine singular noun
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Primero* and *tercero* shorten to *primer* and *tercer* directly before a masculine singular noun — the identical apocope mechanism covered for *bueno/grande* in [Chapter 4, Adjectives: Agreement & Position](../lesson-04) and for *alguno/ninguno* in [Chapter 8, Quantifiers & Indefinites](../lesson-08):

<ExampleSentence es="Vivo en el tercer piso." en="I live on the third floor." note="tercero → tercer before piso (m. sing.); the feminine tercera never shortens: la tercera puerta." />
<ExampleSentence es="Hoy es mi primer día de trabajo." en="Today is my first day of work." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
No other ordinal apocopates — *cuarto, quinto, sexto* and beyond keep their full form in every position (*el cuarto piso, la cuarta puerta*). Only *primero* and *tercero* shorten, and only directly before a masculine singular noun.
</GrammarNote>

### Past tenth, real speech quietly swaps ordinals for cardinals
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Formal ordinals do technically exist all the way up (*undécimo, duodécimo, decimotercero*...), but past around *décimo* (10th), everyday Spanish abandons them in favor of the plain cardinal number placed *after* the noun. This shows up constantly in three places: monarchs' names, centuries, and anniversaries.

<ExampleSentence es="Alfonso XIII" en="Alfonso the Thirteenth" note="Read aloud as 'Alfonso Trece' — the cardinal número trece placed after the name — never 'Alfonso Decimotercero,' which would sound stilted and overly formal even though it's technically correct." />
<ExampleSentence es="Vivimos en el siglo XXI." en="We live in the twenty-first century." note="Read as 'el siglo veintiuno' (cardinal), not 'el siglo vigésimo primero' (ordinal) — the reverse of English, which keeps the ordinal 'twenty-first' naturally." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This is a real, load-bearing contrast with English, not a stylistic option: English "Alfonso the Thirteenth" and "the twenty-first century" both keep the ordinal, so an English speaker's instinct is to reach for *decimotercero*/*vigésimo primero*. Native speakers essentially never do past 10th — the cardinal-after-noun pattern is the actual spoken norm, and the formal ordinals read as bookish or a little pompous outside very formal writing.
</GrammarNote>

Below tenth, ordinals stay completely normal and idiomatic — nobody says "Juan Carlos Uno" for Juan Carlos I; *el primero* (and *segundo* through *décimo*) is the everyday choice in that range.

### Un as a number and un as an article — the same word wearing two hats
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Because *uno/un* is simultaneously the cardinal number "one" and the indefinite article "a/an," a sentence like *tengo un hermano* is genuinely ambiguous out of context between "I have a brother" and "I have one brother." Spanish resolves this the same way English does — through context and, when the count actually matters, extra emphasis:

<ExampleSentence es="Tengo un solo hermano, no dos." en="I have only one brother, not two." note="Adding solo forces the 'one, not more' reading when the article/number ambiguity actually needs resolving." />

## Bringing It Together

Numbers touch a sentence in three separate ways, and it's worth keeping them straight: cardinal agreement (*uno/una, doscientos/-as*) is about matching the noun's gender; ordinal apocope (*primer, tercer*) is about position, triggered only directly before a masculine singular noun; and the ordinal-to-cardinal switch past tenth is about register — a different phenomenon that has nothing to do with agreement at all.

<ExampleSentence es="Compré doscientas una entradas para el tercer piso del museo, que celebra el siglo veintiuno." en="I bought two hundred and one tickets for the third floor of the museum, which celebrates the twenty-first century." note="A sentence deliberately stacking hundreds+uno agreement (doscientas una), ordinal apocope (tercer before piso), and the ordinal-to-cardinal switch (siglo veintiuno) in one breath." />
