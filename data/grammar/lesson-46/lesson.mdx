Passive voice turns a sentence around: instead of the subject performing an action, the subject receives it. English leans on this constantly — "The house was built," "The letter was sent" — and it's tempting to assume Spanish handles it the same way, with a direct word-for-word equivalent always on hand. Spanish does have a true passive, built almost exactly like English's, but it reaches for it far less often than English speakers expect, usually preferring a construction built around the small word *se* or simply restructuring the sentence as active. This chapter covers both real strategies for expressing passive meaning, how to tell them apart from constructions that only look similar, and — just as importantly — when native Spanish quietly avoids the literal, English-shaped translation altogether.

## Core Pattern: The True Passive — *Ser* + Participle + *Por*

### Ser + participle: the subject receives the action
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Spanish's true passive is built from *ser*, conjugated in whatever tense the sentence needs, plus a past participle that agrees in gender and number with the subject — the same participle-as-adjective agreement [Chapter 44](../lesson-44) covers in full. The agent, if named at all, follows *por*:

<ExampleSentence es="El puente fue construido por ingenieros franceses." en="The bridge was built by French engineers." note="The exact example previewed back in Chapter 21 — this chapter delivers the full mechanics it promised." />

The logic behind the sentence is a straightforward swap. In the active version, *ingenieros franceses* is the subject and *el puente* is the object. The passive version promotes the object into subject position, demotes the original subject into an optional *por*-phrase, and recasts the verb as *ser* + participle:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Ingenieros franceses", role: "subject" },
    { text: "construyeron", role: "verb" },
    { text: "el puente.", role: "object" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "El puente", role: "subject" },
    { text: "fue construido", role: "verb" },
    { text: "por ingenieros franceses.", role: "other" }
  ]}
/>

Notice that the grammatical subject changes (*el puente* now controls the verb), but *who is doing what* to *whom* stays identical — passive voice is a change in grammatical packaging, not in meaning.

### Ser in any tense, the participle always agreeing
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Because *ser* is doing the conjugating, the true passive can appear in any tense Spanish has — the participle itself never changes shape once its agreement is set:

<ExampleSentence es="La novela fue escrita por una autora catalana." en="The novel was written by a Catalan author." note="escrita agrees with the feminine singular novela — escribir's irregular participle, from Chapter 44's list." />
<ExampleSentence es="Los documentos serán revisados por el comité mañana." en="The documents will be reviewed by the committee tomorrow." note="revisados — masculine plural, future tense of ser." />
<ExampleSentence es="Estas casas han sido restauradas recientemente." en="These houses have been restored recently." note="restauradas — feminine plural, present perfect of ser." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is exactly the agreement Chapter 44 flagged as a preview: *ser* + participle behaves like *estar* + participle and plain adjective use — the participle tracks the subject's gender and number every time. Contrast this with *haber* + participle (the compound tenses, [Chapter 30](../lesson-30)), where the participle is frozen and never agrees with anything, no matter how many times you conjugate *haber* around it.
</GrammarNote>

### Passive without a stated agent
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The *por*-phrase is optional — Spanish, like English, often uses the passive precisely because the agent is unknown, obvious, or simply unimportant to the sentence:

<ExampleSentence es="El cuadro fue restaurado el año pasado." en="The painting was restored last year." note="No agent named — who restored it isn't the point of the sentence." />

## Building Out: The Passive/Impersonal *Se* — *Se Habla Español, Se Venden Casas*

### The se-passive: Spanish's everyday alternative
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Spanish has a second, far more common way to express the same basic idea — something is done to something, with no need to name who did it. It's built from *se* plus a third-person verb that agrees in number with whatever follows it, since that following noun is functioning as the sentence's grammatical subject:

<ExampleSentence es="Se habla español aquí." en="Spanish is spoken here." note="habla stays singular because español is singular." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se venden casas en esta zona." en="Houses are sold/for sale in this area." note="venden goes plural to agree with casas — exactly the same agreement logic as a normal verb and its subject." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The se-passive never takes a *por*-phrase. You cannot say *"Se venden casas por los agentes"* to mean "the houses are sold by the agents" — once you need to name an agent, you've left se-passive territory and need the true passive instead (*Las casas son vendidas por los agentes*, though even that would usually be re-phrased as active in practice — more on that below).
</GrammarNote>

### Se-passive vs. true passive: two roads to the same idea
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Both constructions can express "X is done" with no agent named, and in that overlap, the se-passive is overwhelmingly the more natural everyday choice — on signs, menus, recipes, and in ordinary conversation:

<ExampleSentence es="El español se habla en muchos países." en="Spanish is spoken in many countries." note="The natural, everyday phrasing." />
<ExampleSentence es="El español es hablado en muchos países." en="Spanish is spoken in many countries." note="Grammatically correct but stiff — a native speaker would reach for the se-passive above almost every time." />

The full story of when each construction actually gets used — and when Spanish prefers neither and goes active instead — is this chapter's job in the Nuance section below.

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Passive-se vs. reflexive-se: same spelling, unrelated jobs
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The *se* in *se venden casas* looks identical to the reflexive *se* [Chapter 15](../lesson-15) covers, but the two are doing completely different jobs. A true reflexive needs a subject capable of performing — and receiving — the action itself, almost always something animate:

<ExampleSentence es="Los niños se lavan las manos." en="The children wash their hands." note="Reflexive: los niños are both the ones washing and the ones being washed (via las manos)." />
<ExampleSentence es="Los platos se lavan todos los días." en="The dishes are washed every day." note="Passive-se: plates cannot wash themselves, so the only sensible reading is that someone (unnamed) washes them." />

That second example is the fastest practical test: if the subject genuinely could plausibly act on itself, you're likely looking at a reflexive; if the subject is an inanimate thing that cannot logically perform the action, *se* is almost certainly doing passive work instead. Context and plausibility disambiguate far more often than grammar alone.

### The three jobs of *se*: completing the picture
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Across the pronoun chapters, *se* has now shown up doing three genuinely unrelated jobs that happen to share one spelling. This is the recap [Chapter 14](../lesson-14) promised once all three were on the table:

| Job | Example | Full treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Reflexive / reciprocal / pronominal *se* | *Los niños **se** lavan las manos.* | [Chapter 15](../lesson-15) |
| *Le/les* → *se* before *lo/la/los/las* (pure spelling substitution, no meaning of its own) | *Le doy el libro. → **Se** lo doy.* | [Chapter 14](../lesson-14) |
| Passive / impersonal *se* | *Se* **venden** *casas.* | This chapter, plus [Chapter 47](../lesson-47) |

That third row is itself worth splitting further. The passive-se covered above agrees in number with the noun that follows it, because that noun is a genuine grammatical subject (*se vende casa* / *se venden casas*). A closely related but distinct construction — true impersonal *se*, which stays singular no matter what follows because there's no grammatical subject at all to agree with (*se vive bien aquí*, *se dice que...*) — gets its full treatment in [Chapter 47](../lesson-47). The two are close cousins, not the same rule twice.

### When Spanish avoids the true passive
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

This is the single most useful habit to build out of this chapter. English defaults to the passive constantly, and it's tempting to translate every English passive sentence literally with *ser* + participle. Spanish can almost always follow along grammatically — but it frequently sounds imported and stiff when it does, because native speakers reach for something else instead: an active sentence with a vague or indefinite subject, or the se-passive already covered above.

<ExampleSentence es="Me robaron el coche." en="My car was stolen." note="Literally 'they stole my car from me' — an active sentence with an unspecified they, the natural everyday phrasing." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mi coche fue robado." en="My car was stolen." note="Grammatically fine, but reads like a police report or news item rather than something you'd say to a friend." />

<ExampleSentence es="Aquí se hablaba español hace 500 años." en="Spanish was spoken here 500 years ago." note="The natural phrasing, using the se-passive with a past-tense verb." />

As a rough guide: true passive is at home in formal writing, journalism, historical narration, and legal or official language, where a named or implied institutional agent and a written register both feel natural —

<ExampleSentence es="El tratado fue firmado en 1898." en="The treaty was signed in 1898." note="A history-book sentence — exactly the register where ser-passive sounds completely native." />

— while everyday spoken and written Spanish about ordinary events reaches first for active voice with an indefinite subject, or for the se-passive. Treating *ser* + participle as your default translation for every English passive is the single most common way this construction gives away a non-native speaker; treating it as one formal-register tool among several is the more accurate mental model.

## Bringing It Together

Faced with an English passive sentence to render in Spanish, work through it in this order: first, could this just as naturally be said as an active sentence with *tú*, *ellos*, or an indefinite "someone" as the subject? If so, that's usually the most natural Spanish. If the sentence is about something being done in general — a service offered, a product sold, a rule in force — reach for the se-passive. Only when the register is genuinely formal or journalistic, and especially when you want to name the agent explicitly with *por*, does the true *ser*-passive become the right tool.

<ExampleSentence es="Se necesitan camareros. La entrevista será realizada por la gerente el viernes." en="Waiters are needed. The interview will be conducted by the manager on Friday." note="One sentence in the natural se-passive (no agent, everyday register) followed by one in the true passive (agent named with por, more formal register) — both correct, doing different jobs." />
