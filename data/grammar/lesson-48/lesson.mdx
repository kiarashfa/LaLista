English hands one small word, "for," an enormous amount of work — and then borrows "by," "through," and "in order to" to cover the rest of the same territory. Spanish splits that whole territory between two prepositions, *por* and *para*, and gives each a distinct job description. Neither one is hard to say, and neither has a paradigm to memorize — there's no conjugation here at all — so the entire task this chapter sets you is a judgment call: given a relationship you'd express with "for" (or "because of," or "through," or nothing at all) in English, which Spanish word does the underlying logic actually call for. Build the judgment below and the choice becomes close to automatic; skip it and por/para becomes one of the most persistent, most noticeable non-native tells in spoken and written Spanish.

## Core Pattern: Two Directions — Looking Back, Looking Forward

### The organizing logic: *por* looks backward, *para* looks forward
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The single most useful lens for approaching *por* and *para*, and the one this chapter leans on throughout, is direction. **Por** tends to point backward — to a cause, a reason, an origin, something that already exists and explains why an action happens. **Para** tends to point forward — to a purpose, a goal, a destination, an endpoint the action is aimed at but hasn't necessarily reached yet. Put another way: if you can answer "why" or "because of what," you're probably reaching for *por*; if you can answer "to what end" or "toward what," you're probably reaching for *para*.

<ExampleSentence es="No fui a la fiesta por la lluvia." en="I didn't go to the party because of the rain." note="Por — the rain is a cause that already existed and explains the (non-)action." />
<ExampleSentence es="Estudio para aprender español." en="I study in order to learn Spanish." note="Para — learning is the goal the studying is aimed at, not yet achieved." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Treat "backward vs. forward" as a genuinely useful starting compass, not an exceptionless test. A few of *por* and *para*'s most common uses — duration and exchange for *por*, comparison for *para* — don't map cleanly onto direction at all. Rather than stretch the metaphor to cover them, this chapter flags each one honestly as it comes up and treats it as its own entry to learn.
</GrammarNote>

### *Por's* home base: cause, reason, and motive
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

This is *por* at its most central — naming the reason something happened, the circumstance that explains an action after the fact:

<ExampleSentence es="Llegamos tarde por el tráfico." en="We arrived late because of the traffic." />
<ExampleSentence es="Por eso no te llamé anoche." en="That's why I didn't call you last night." note="Por eso — 'that's why,' pointing back to a reason already on the table." />

### *Para's* home base: purpose and goal
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

And this is *para* at its most central — naming what an action is aimed at achieving, usually followed directly by an infinitive:

<ExampleSentence es="Trabajo para vivir, no vivo para trabajar." en="I work to live, I don't live to work." note="Two goals, each marked with para + infinitive." />
<ExampleSentence es="Ahorro dinero para comprar un coche." en="I'm saving money to buy a car." />

## Building Out: The Rest of Each Preposition's Territory

### *Por*: duration, exchange, movement-through, means, and "on behalf of"
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Four more jobs belong to *por*, and they're best learned as their own entries rather than forced through the backward-looking lens above — two of them, duration and exchange, genuinely don't reduce to "cause" no matter how the logic gets stretched.

<ExampleSentence es="Vivimos en Madrid por dos años." en="We lived in Madrid for two years." note="Duration — measuring a stretch of time an action occupies. This doesn't fit the backward metaphor; learn it as its own use." />
<ExampleSentence es="Te doy diez euros por el libro." en="I'll give you ten euros for the book." note="Exchange — one thing traded for another. Also doesn't reduce cleanly to 'cause,' though you can loosely picture the item handed over as the basis for the price." />
<ExampleSentence es="Caminamos por el parque cada tarde." en="We walk through the park every afternoon." note="Movement through or along a space — the route itself, not an endpoint. Contrast with para for destination, below." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mandé el paquete por avión." en="I sent the package by plane." note="Means/manner — por avión, por correo, por teléfono: the medium something travels or happens through." />
<ExampleSentence es="No pude ir a la reunión, así que fue mi hermana por mí." en="I couldn't make the meeting, so my sister went for me / in my place." note="On behalf of, in place of — still loosely backward-facing, since the reason my sister went traces back to me." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A useful extension of "movement-through" that doesn't get its own headline: *por* also covers a loosely-defined stretch of time, especially parts of the day — *por la mañana* (in the morning), *por la tarde* (in the afternoon), *por la noche* (at night). Think of it as moving "through" a general span of time the same way *caminar por el parque* moves through a space, rather than naming an exact clock time (which always takes a different construction, covered in [Chapter 10](../lesson-10)).
</GrammarNote>

### *Para*: destination, deadline, and recipient
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Three more *para* uses, all genuinely forward-facing:

<ExampleSentence es="Salgo para Madrid mañana." en="I'm leaving for Madrid tomorrow." note="Destination — para marks the place you're headed, typically after salir or ir." />
<ExampleSentence es="Necesito el informe para el viernes." en="I need the report by Friday." note="Deadline — the point in time the action is aimed at completing by." />
<ExampleSentence es="Esto es para ti." en="This is for you." note="Recipient — who or what the thing is intended for." />

### *Para's* edge case: comparison and opinion
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

One more *para* use resists the forward-looking frame about as much as duration and exchange resist *por's* backward frame: using *para* to introduce a standard of comparison or a personal viewpoint. There's no real goal or destination here — just a reference point the rest of the sentence gets measured against.

<ExampleSentence es="Para mí, es difícil." en="For me / in my opinion, it's difficult." note="Introduces a personal viewpoint, not a goal — best learned as its own use rather than stretched to fit 'forward.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="Para ser principiante, lo hace muy bien." en="For a beginner, he does it very well." note="Comparison against an implied standard — 'given that he's a beginner, this is more skilled than expected.'" />

### Fixed expressions with *por*
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

A handful of everyday *por* phrases are worth learning as fixed units:

<ExampleSentence es="Por ejemplo, en España se cena muy tarde." en="For example, in Spain people eat dinner very late." note="Por ejemplo — for example." />
<ExampleSentence es="¡Por fin llegaste!" en="You finally arrived!" note="Por fin — finally, at last." />
<ExampleSentence es="Por supuesto que puedes venir." en="Of course you can come." note="Por supuesto — of course." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
One more, *por lo tanto* ("therefore," "consequently"), belongs on this list too, but it does double duty as a discourse connector linking whole sentences rather than a phrase inside one — its full treatment, alongside *sin embargo*, *es decir*, and the rest of that family, is [Chapter 56](../lesson-56)'s job.
</GrammarNote>

### Fixed expressions with *para*
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

<ExampleSentence es="Te querré para siempre." en="I'll love you forever." note="Para siempre — forever." />
<ExampleSentence es="No me gusta para nada." en="I don't like it at all." note="Para nada — not at all (as a standalone intensifier of negation)." />
<ExampleSentence es="Hoy, para variar, llegó puntual." en="Today, for a change, he arrived on time." note="Para variar — for a change, usually with a light note of irony." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### *Por* as the passive-voice agent marker
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Por* has one more job you've very likely already met: introducing the agent in Spanish's true passive voice, *ser* + participle + *por*.

<ExampleSentence es="El puente fue construido por ingenieros franceses." en="The bridge was built by French engineers." note="The same example first previewed in Chapter 21 and given full treatment in Chapter 46 — por here just names who performed the action." />

This isn't a new rule to learn — it's the same backward-facing *por* from the top of this chapter, just attached to a passive sentence instead of an active one: *por ingenieros franceses* answers "who caused this to happen," exactly the kind of question *por* always answers. The full mechanics of building the passive itself belong to [Chapter 21](../lesson-21) and [Chapter 46](../lesson-46) — this entry is only here so the use has a place in *por's* complete range.

### Side by side: same frame, different meaning
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A few sentence frames accept either preposition and change meaning sharply depending on which one shows up — the fastest way to feel the *por*/*para* difference is to see the same words split both ways:

| Frame | With *por* | With *para* |
|---|---|---|
| Trabajo ___ mi padre. | *Trabajo por mi padre.* — I'm covering for my father (he can't work today; I'm standing in for him). | *Trabajo para mi padre.* — My father is my employer. |
| Lo hice ___ ti. | *Lo hice por ti.* — I did it because of you / for your sake (you're the reason). | *Lo hice para ti.* — I made it for you (you're the intended recipient). |
| Caminamos ___ el parque. | *Caminamos por el parque.* — We walked through the park (the route). | *Caminamos para el parque.* — We walked toward the park (heading there as a destination). |

<GrammarNote type="tip">
That last row's *para* reading is grammatical but a little less common in casual speech than *hacia* ("toward") for describing motion aimed at a place — *para* as a destination marker shows up most naturally after verbs of departure (*salir para*, *partir para*), which is why the destination examples earlier in this chapter use *salgo para Madrid* rather than *camino para Madrid*. Full treatment of *hacia* and Spanish's other directional prepositions is [Chapter 50](../lesson-50)'s job.
</GrammarNote>

### C1 idiomatic exceptions: *estar por* vs. *estar para*
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

*Estar* combined with *por* or *para* plus an infinitive builds two genuinely idiomatic constructions that don't derive cleanly from anything taught above — at this point they're best learned as set patterns in their own right.

<ExampleSentence es="Estoy por dejarlo todo e irme de viaje." en="I'm tempted to drop everything and go traveling." note="Estar por + infinitive — inclined to, tempted to; a pull toward doing something, not yet decided." />
<ExampleSentence es="La cena todavía está por hacer." en="Dinner still needs to be made." note="Estar por + infinitive with a thing as the subject — 'yet to be done.' A very common everyday construction in Spain: la ropa está por planchar, los platos están por lavar..." />
<ExampleSentence es="No estoy para bromas hoy." en="I'm not in the mood for jokes today." note="Estar para + noun — in the mood for, up for." />
<ExampleSentence es="El tren está para salir." en="The train is about to leave." note="Estar para + infinitive — about to, on the verge of." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
In much of Latin America, *estar por* + infinitive commonly means straightforwardly "about to" (*está por llover* — "it's about to rain"). In Spain, *estar por* leans toward the "inclined to / tempted to" and "yet to be done" readings above instead, and *estar a punto de* is the go-to phrase whenever you specifically mean "about to." *Estar para* + infinitive for "about to," as in the *tren* example, exists in Castellano Spanish but is genuinely less frequent than reaching for *estar a punto de*.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

Faced with an English "for" — or a sentence that would use "because of," "through," "by," or nothing at all — work through it in this order. **Naming a cause, reason, or origin — something that already exists and explains why?** Reach for *por*. **Naming a purpose, goal, or destination — something the action is aimed at?** Reach for *para*. **Is it duration, exchange, movement-through, means, or "on behalf of"?** Those are *por's*, even though they don't all fit the backward metaphor cleanly. **Is it a deadline, a recipient, or a comparison/opinion?** Those are *para's*, even though comparison doesn't fit the forward metaphor either. **A fixed expression** — *por fin*, *por supuesto*, *para siempre*, *para nada*? Learn it as a set phrase. **Naming who performed a passive action?** That's *por* too, the same agent-marking job from Chapters 21 and 46.

<ExampleSentence es="Trabajo por la mañana para ganar dinero, y por la tarde estudio para mis exámenes, que son para el jueves." en="I work in the morning to earn money, and in the afternoon I study for my exams, which are on Thursday." note="Four uses in one sentence: por la mañana (a general span of time), para ganar dinero (purpose), para mis exámenes (the goal the studying is aimed at), and para el jueves (deadline)." />
