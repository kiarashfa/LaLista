English "I like the book" puts *I* in the driver's seat: I am the subject, doing the liking, and the book just sits there as the object. Spanish restructures the whole sentence around a different idea — the book is doing something *to* me, namely pleasing me — and that reversal, more than any single vocabulary word, is the entire grammatical content of this chapter. Once *me gusta el libro* stops sounding like a scrambled version of "I like the book" and starts sounding like its own complete, logical sentence, an entire family of everyday Spanish verbs — *encantar, importar, doler, faltar* — falls into place alongside it.

## Core Pattern: The Grammar Behind Me Gusta

### Subject and indirect object swap places
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

*Gustar* doesn't really mean "to like" — it means "to be pleasing (to someone)." The person doing the liking shows up as an **indirect object pronoun** ([Chapter 13](../lesson-13)), and the thing being liked is the **grammatical subject** — the part the verb actually agrees with:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "I", role: "subject" },
    { text: "like", role: "verb" },
    { text: "the book.", role: "object" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Me", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "gusta", role: "verb" },
    { text: "el libro.", role: "subject" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Me gusta el libro." en="I like the book." note="Literally closer to 'the book is pleasing to me' — el libro is the grammatical subject, me is the indirect object." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Te gusta el café?" en="Do you like coffee?" note="El café is still the subject; te is who's doing the liking." />

The indirect object pronoun set is the same six-way grid from Chapter 13, and it's worth seeing gustar paired against all six at once:

<BootTable
  label="gustar (with a singular thing liked)"
  caption="present tense"
  forms={{
    "yo": "me gusta",
    "tú": "te gusta",
    "él/ella/usted": "le gusta",
    "nosotros": "nos gusta",
    "vosotros": "os gusta",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "les gusta"
  }}
/>

Notice no boot outline appears here — unlike Chapter 23's stem-changing verbs, the verb form itself never changes across this grid; only the indirect object pronoun in front of it does. *Gusta* stays *gusta* in all six cells, because it's agreeing with an unchanging singular subject (whatever is being liked), not with the pronoun beside it. That's a genuinely different kind of six-way pattern than a conjugation table — this grid is really showing pronoun agreement, not verb agreement.

### The verb agrees with what's liked, not who's doing the liking
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Because the real subject of a gustar sentence is the thing being liked, the verb has exactly two present-tense forms that matter in ordinary use: *gusta* for a singular subject, *gustan* for a plural one.

<ExampleSentence es="Me gusta el libro." en="I like the book." note="El libro — singular subject, gusta." />
<ExampleSentence es="Me gustan los libros de misterio." en="I like mystery novels." note="Los libros — plural subject, gustan. Not 'me gusto los libros.'" />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The number that matters is the thing liked, never the person doing the liking. "We like the movies" is Nos gustan las películas (gustan agrees with películas, plural), not "nos gustamos las películas."
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: The Full Gustar Family

### More verbs, same pattern
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

A whole family of common verbs uses this exact indirect-object-subject structure. Learn the pattern once with gustar and every verb below works the same way, no new grammar required:

| Verb | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| encantar | to love, be delighted by | *Me encanta esta canción.* (I love this song.) |
| interesar | to interest | *Nos interesa la historia.* (History interests us.) |
| importar | to matter (to someone) | *No me importa la hora.* (The time doesn't matter to me.) |
| molestar | to bother | *Le molesta el ruido.* (Noise bothers her.) |
| faltar | to be lacking, missing | *Me falta un lápiz.* (I'm missing a pencil.) |
| quedar | to have left; to fit (clothing) | *Nos quedan diez minutos.* (We have ten minutes left.) |
| doler | to hurt | *Me duelen los pies.* (My feet hurt.) |

<ExampleSentence es="A ella le encanta el cine español." en="She loves Spanish cinema." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Te quedan bien esos zapatos?" en="Do those shoes fit you well?" note="quedar here means 'to fit,' a common extension of the same be-left-over sense." />

### Doler stacks a stem change on top of the gustar pattern
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Doler* follows the o→ue stem change from [Chapter 23](../lesson-23) (*duele, duelen*) on top of the gustar-style sentence structure — two systems layering, not competing:

<ExampleSentence es="Me duele la cabeza." en="I have a headache." note="Duele (singular, o→ue) because la cabeza is singular." />
<ExampleSentence es="Le duelen las piernas después de correr." en="His/her legs hurt after running." note="Duelen (plural, o→ue) because las piernas is plural." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### The classic error: forcing English "I like" onto Spanish
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The single most common mistake with this family is building the sentence around the person as if Spanish worked like English:

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Yo gusto el chocolate* is not simply bad grammar — it's a different, strange sentence. Dropped into that structure, gusto (a real conjugated form of gustar) would describe yourself as being pleasing/attractive to someone, not describe your own preference. The fix isn't to conjugate gustar for "yo" at all — it's to reach for me: Me gusta el chocolate.
</GrammarNote>

### When the "liked" thing is a person: yo le gusto a María
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

That "attractive/pleasing" sense of gustar isn't a trap to avoid — it's a real, useful sentence once you're using it on purpose. Compare these two, which look similar but say opposite things:

<ExampleSentence es="María me gusta." en="I like María." note="María is the subject (the thing/person being liked); me is who's doing the liking." />
<ExampleSentence es="Yo le gusto a María." en="María likes me." note="Now yo is the subject — I am the one being liked — and le...a María is who's doing the liking. Same machinery, reversed roles." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Nothing new is happening grammatically in the second sentence — it's the identical IO-subject pattern as always, just with a person instead of an object filling the subject slot. Reading it as "I am pleasing to María" keeps the logic straight.
</GrammarNote>

### Redundant, emphatic doubling: a mí me gusta...
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

[Chapter 16](../lesson-16) previewed this construction; here's the full picture. Because *me, te, le,* and *les* are short and sometimes ambiguous (*le* alone can't distinguish "him" from "her" from formal "you" — [Chapter 13](../lesson-13)), Spanish routinely adds *a* plus a prepositional pronoun ([Chapter 16](../lesson-16)) alongside the IO pronoun, for emphasis, contrast, or clarification. Both pieces stay — the *a*-phrase never replaces the pronoun:

<ExampleSentence es="A mí me gusta el café, pero a él no le gusta." en="I like coffee, but he doesn't." note="A mí and a él add contrast between two people; me and le are still both grammatically required." />
<ExampleSentence es="A los niños les encanta el parque." en="The kids love the park." note="A los niños is a full noun phrase, not just a pronoun, and les is still required alongside it — the same fronted-doubling rule from Chapter 13." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
A mí never stands in on its own — you can't say A mí gusta el café and drop me. The prepositional phrase is decoration for emphasis or clarity; the pronoun is doing the actual grammatical work and can't be dropped just because the a-phrase is there.
</GrammarNote>

### Infinitives and multiple items as the subject
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

An infinitive — or a whole list of them — counts as a singular subject, so gustar stays in its *gusta* form even when more than one activity is named:

<ExampleSentence es="Me gusta nadar." en="I like to swim." note="An infinitive subject is always treated as singular." />
<ExampleSentence es="Me gusta leer y escribir." en="I like reading and writing." note="Two infinitives together, still gusta — not gustan." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Gustar and its family also show up constantly with a que-clause instead of a noun (Me gusta que me escuches — "I like that you listen to me"), which almost always triggers the subjunctive mood, since liking/emotion is one of the classic subjunctive triggers covered in [Chapter 38](../lesson-38).
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="A mí me encanta el fútbol, pero a mi hermana no le interesa nada — a ella le gustan más los libros. A los dos nos molesta el mismo ruido, eso sí: el vecino que practica la batería a medianoche." en="I love soccer, but my sister isn't interested in it at all — she likes books more. There's one thing that bothers us both, though: the neighbor who practices drums at midnight." note="encanta and interesa and gustan and molesta all following the same IO-subject pattern, with a mí/a ella/a los dos doubling for contrast throughout." />

Faced with a gustar-family sentence, the questions are always the same: what's actually being liked, missed, or bothered — that's your subject, and it decides *gusta* vs. *gustan*. Who's experiencing it — that's your indirect object pronoun, always present, never optional. And is there any ambiguity or emphasis worth adding — that's what the *a mí, a él, a María* phrase is for, riding alongside the pronoun rather than replacing it.
