Spanish splits the simple past into two different tenses with two different jobs, and the preterite — *el pretérito indefinido* — is the one that reports a completed event: something that happened, started, or ended at a specific point, viewed as a finished whole rather than a backdrop or a habit. [Chapter 22](../lesson-22) already showed the present tense stretching to cover "yesterday" informally in casual narration; the preterite is the tense that does this job properly, on purpose, every time you need to say what happened. This chapter covers the full regular paradigm — every verb whose preterite you can build from a completely predictable rule — plus a set of verbs that only look irregular on the page because Spanish spelling has to work around them. The genuinely irregular verbs, the ones that change their actual stem, get their own chapter next.

## Core Pattern: The Regular Preterite Endings

### What the preterite means: a finished action
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

The single most useful idea to hold onto in this whole chapter: the preterite reports an action with a clear edge — a beginning, an end, or both — treated as a complete, closed event, not an ongoing state or a repeated habit.

<ExampleSentence es="Ayer hablé con mi hermana." en="Yesterday I talked with my sister." note="A single, finished conversation — not a description of an ongoing state." />
<ExampleSentence es="Viví en Sevilla durante tres años." en="I lived in Seville for three years." note="Even a long span of time counts as 'finished' if you're presenting it as a closed chapter, not an ongoing background state." />

Don't let "finished" mislead you into thinking the preterite is only for short or instant actions — *viví... durante tres años* is a completed three-year span, reported as one bounded block. The competing past tense, the imperfect (ongoing background, habitual action, description — [Chapter 28](../lesson-28)), is the one reserved for actions without that clear edge. This chapter doesn't need that contrast to make sense on its own — full treatment of choosing between the two lives in [Chapter 29](../lesson-29) — but it's worth knowing from the start that "completed, bounded event" is the preterite's entire job description.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
In the present tense ([Chapter 22](../lesson-22)), -er and -ir verbs mostly agree and only pull apart in the nosotros/vosotros forms (comemos/coméis vs. vivimos/vivís). In the preterite they go a step further: the endings are **completely identical** for every single one of the six forms. If you know comer's preterite, you already know vivir's — just swap the stem.
</GrammarNote>

<ExampleSentence es="Vivieron en Madrid antes de mudarse a Barcelona." en="They lived in Madrid before moving to Barcelona." />

## Building Out: Spelling Changes That Keep the Sound

The verbs in this section are not irregular in any real sense — their stem never changes, and the endings above still apply exactly as written. What changes is a single letter, and only ever to keep the spelling honest about how the word actually sounds. This is the same kind of "regular under the hood" adjustment [Chapter 2](../lesson-02) showed for plurals like *lápiz → lápices* and [Chapter 9](../lesson-09) showed for apocope like *bueno → buen* — a spelling rule reacting to a sound, not a break in the underlying pattern.

### -car, -gar, -zar verbs: a yo-form spelling swap
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Spanish spelling ties the letters *c* and *g* to different sounds depending on the vowel that follows them. The regular yo-ending *-é* starts with *e*, and *ce/ge* would sound wrong for these stems — so the spelling swaps in a letter that keeps the original sound intact, purely in the yo form:

- **-car → -qué**: *buscar → busqué* (the rest is regular: buscaste, buscó, buscamos, buscasteis, buscaron)
- **-gar → -gué**: *llegar → llegué* (llegaste, llegó, llegamos, llegasteis, llegaron)
- **-zar → -cé**: *empezar → empecé* (empezaste, empezó, empezamos, empezasteis, empezaron)

<BootTable
  label="buscar"
  caption="preterite"
  forms={{
    "yo": "busqué",
    "tú": "buscaste",
    "él/ella/usted": "buscó",
    "nosotros": "buscamos",
    "vosotros": "buscasteis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "buscaron"
  }}
/>

Only one of the six cells is actually different from the plain regular pattern — the same single-cell shape [Chapter 23](../lesson-23) showed for present-tense yo-irregulars like *hago*. Every other verb in this family works the same way: *tocar → toqué, sacar → saqué, explicar → expliqué, jugar → jugué, pagar → pagué, entregar → entregué, cruzar → crucé, comenzar → comencé, almorzar → almorcé.*

<ExampleSentence es="Busqué las llaves por toda la casa." en="I looked for the keys all over the house." />
<ExampleSentence es="Empecé a estudiar español el año pasado." en="I started studying Spanish last year." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This swap only ever touches the yo form. Every other person keeps the plain infinitive stem: *buscaste, buscó, buscamos* — never *busqué* anywhere but yo. Don't let the spelling change bleed into forms that don't need it.
</GrammarNote>

### When unstressed i becomes y between vowels
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

A second, unrelated spelling rule: when an -er or -ir verb's stem itself ends in a vowel, the regular -ió/-ieron endings would put an unstressed *i* directly between two other vowels, which Spanish spelling doesn't allow — that *i* becomes a *y* instead.

<BootTable
  label="leer"
  caption="preterite"
  forms={{
    "yo": "leí",
    "tú": "leíste",
    "él/ella/usted": "leyó",
    "nosotros": "leímos",
    "vosotros": "leísteis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "leyeron"
  }}
/>

Here the affected cells are él/ella/usted and ellos/ellas/ustedes — a different two-cell shape from the single-cell *busqué* pattern above, because this rule only ever fires where the ending's *i* actually lands between two vowels, which happens in exactly those two forms (*le-ió → leyó*, *le-ieron → leyeron*). The yo/tú/nosotros/vosotros forms keep their *i*, and — because a stressed *í* right after another vowel is a genuine hiatus — that *i* keeps its written accent: *leí, leíste, leímos, leísteis.*

The same pattern covers *creer* (creí, creíste, creyó, creímos, creísteis, creyeron), *oír* (oí, oíste, oyó, oímos, oísteis, oyeron), and *caer* (caí, caíste, cayó, caímos, caísteis, cayeron), and it extends to -uir verbs like *construir* and *destruir* as well (construí, construiste, construyó...).

<ExampleSentence es="Leí ese libro el verano pasado." en="I read that book last summer." />
<ExampleSentence es="No oyeron la alarma." en="They didn't hear the alarm." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### -AR and -ER stem-changing verbs: fully regular in the preterite
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

[Chapter 23](../lesson-23) taught a family of present-tense "boot" verbs whose stressed stem vowel shifts — *pensar → pienso, volver → vuelvo.* A very natural, very common mistake is assuming that shift carries into the preterite. For -ar and -er verbs, it never does:

<ExampleSentence es="Pensó mucho antes de responder, no pensó → piensó." en="He thought a lot before answering." note="pensó is correct and fully regular; piensó doesn't exist." />
<ExampleSentence es="Volvimos a casa tarde." en="We went back home late." note="volvió, volviste, volvieron — all regular, no o→ue anywhere in the preterite." />

Every -ar and -er stem-changing verb from Chapter 23 — *pensar, cerrar, entender, perder, volver, contar, poder* (poder's preterite is a separate, genuinely irregular story — see [Chapter 27](../lesson-27)) — drops its present-tense stem change completely once you're in the preterite and simply follows the regular pattern above.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
-IR stem-changing verbs (*pedir, dormir, sentir* and their relatives) are the one real exception to this — they keep a related, but different, vowel change *only* in the él/ella/usted and ellos/ellas/ustedes preterite forms (*pidió, durmió*). That pattern is dense enough, and different enough from ordinary "irregular" verbs, to earn its own clearly labeled section in [Chapter 27](../lesson-27) rather than being folded in here.
</GrammarNote>

### A common mix-up: present vs. preterite accent marks
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Because the only visible difference between some present and preterite forms is a single accent mark, this is a genuinely easy typo to make — and a genuinely important one, since it changes the tense entirely:

<ExampleSentence es="Hablo español todos los días. / Habló español en la reunión." en="I speak Spanish every day. / He spoke Spanish at the meeting." note="hablo (present, yo) vs. habló (preterite, él/ella/usted) — same letters, different accent, different subject, different tense." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Dropping the accent on a preterite yo or él/ella/usted form doesn't just look wrong — *tomo* (I drink, present) and *tomó* (he/she drank, preterite) are two entirely different sentences wearing the same letters. When typing quickly, this is one of the most common places to lose the accent that actually carries the meaning.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Ayer busqué trabajo toda la mañana. Empecé temprano, leí varios anuncios y llamé a dos empresas. Por la tarde comí con un amigo y hablamos del tema durante horas." en="Yesterday I looked for work all morning. I started early, read several listings, and called two companies. In the afternoon I ate with a friend and we talked about it for hours." note="busqué and empecé (spelling-change yo forms), leí (i→y family, yo form unaffected), llamé/comí/hablamos (fully regular) — every verb here is 'regular,' just wearing different spelling adjustments." />

Faced with a new verb in the preterite, the regular pattern covers it completely unless the stem itself changes — check first whether it ends in -car/-gar/-zar (a yo-only spelling swap) or has a vowel-final stem (an él/ellos-only *i→y* swap), apply the matching adjustment, and otherwise just attach the plain -ar or -er/-ir endings above. Only a genuine stem change — the subject of the next chapter — takes a verb outside this system entirely.
