Every conditional sentence makes the same basic move — a condition (the *si*-clause) paired with a consequence (the main clause) — but English lets its "if" clauses flex loosely across tenses in a way Spanish never does. Spanish locks each type of condition to one, and only one, correct pair of tenses: get the pairing wrong, and the sentence stops sounding like a minor stylistic slip and starts sounding simply broken. This chapter doesn't teach a single new verb form — every tense involved already has its own home ([Chapter 34](../lesson-34)'s simple conditional, [Chapter 35](../lesson-35)'s compound conditional, [Chapter 39](../lesson-39)'s imperfect subjunctive, [Chapter 40](../lesson-40)'s pluperfect subjunctive). What this chapter teaches is the pattern-matching itself: given a condition, which exact pair of tenses does Spanish demand, and why.

## Core Pattern: Real/Possible Conditions

### Si + present indicative, + present indicative / future / command
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

When a condition is genuinely open — plausible, still up for grabs, not contrary to any known fact — both clauses stay in the indicative. The *si*-clause takes the present indicative; the main clause takes the present indicative, the future, or an imperative.

<ExampleSentence es="Si llueve, no salgo." en="If it rains, I'm not going out." note="present + present — a general, real cause-and-effect rule." />
<ExampleSentence es="Si estudias, aprobarás el examen." en="If you study, you'll pass the exam." note="present + future — a real prediction." />
<ExampleSentence es="Si tienes tiempo, llámame." en="If you have time, call me." note="present + command." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Never *si* + present subjunctive for this type. Once a learner knows the subjunctive exists, *"si llueva"* is a common overcorrection — but the *si*-clause of a real/possible condition always stays fully indicative, with no exceptions.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Hypothetical Present Conditions

### Si + imperfect subjunctive, + simple conditional
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

When a condition is contrary to fact right now, or simply unlikely, the pattern shifts entirely: the *si*-clause takes the imperfect subjunctive ([Chapter 39](../lesson-39)), and the main clause answers with the simple conditional ([Chapter 34](../lesson-34)).

<ExampleSentence es="Si tuviera dinero, viajaría más." en="If I had money, I would travel more." note="tuviera signals the condition isn't real right now; viajaría is the hypothetical result." />
<ExampleSentence es="Si fuera tú, no lo haría." en="If I were you, I wouldn't do it." note="A fixed, extremely common way to give advice — always fuera, never sería, in this construction." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is the same pairing logic [Chapter 39](../lesson-39) already previewed: the imperfect subjunctive signals "this isn't really true," and only the simple conditional — never the present indicative, never the future — can answer it.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Hypothetical past conditions: si + pluperfect subjunctive, + compound conditional
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

When the condition itself is about the past — something that didn't happen — and its consequence is also about the past, both clauses step one full tense further back: the *si*-clause takes the pluperfect subjunctive ([Chapter 40](../lesson-40)), and the main clause takes the compound conditional ([Chapter 35](../lesson-35)).

<ExampleSentence es="Si hubiera estudiado más, habría aprobado el examen." en="If I had studied more, I would have passed the exam." note="hubiera estudiado (pluperfect subjunctive) states the unrealized past condition; habría aprobado (compound conditional) is its past-time consequence — the full system behind the preview in Chapters 35 and 39." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
The *si*-clause may just as correctly use *hubiese*: *Si hubiese estudiado más...* — identical meaning, the more literary/formal-Castellano register flagged back in [Chapter 39](../lesson-39).
</GrammarNote>

### Mixed-time conditions: a past condition, a present consequence
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

Sometimes a condition about the past has a consequence that's still being felt right now, in the present — not back then. When that happens, the *si*-clause still takes the pluperfect subjunctive, but the main clause drops back down to the *simple* conditional, not the compound one, because the result being described is a present state, not a past event.

<ExampleSentence es="Si hubiera estudiado, ahora tendría un buen trabajo." en="If I had studied, I would now have a good job." note="The condition (not studying) is in the past — pluperfect subjunctive. But its consequence (not having a good job) is felt right now, so the main clause takes the simple conditional (tendría), not the compound (habría tenido)." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The giveaway is almost always a present-time word in the main clause — *ahora*, *hoy*, *todavía* — signaling that the consequence belongs to the present, not the past. Without that signal, a hypothetical-past reading defaults to the fully-past pattern above (hubiera + habría).
</GrammarNote>

### Word order: the si-clause can come first or second
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Either clause can lead — Spanish doesn't require the *si*-clause to come first the way it usually appears in textbook examples. The meaning doesn't change either way. When the *si*-clause comes first, a comma separates the two clauses; when it comes second, no comma is needed.

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Si llueve,", role: "connector" },
    { text: "no salgo.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "No salgo", role: "verb" },
    { text: "si llueve.", role: "connector" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="No salgo si llueve." en="I'm not going out if it rains." note="Same meaning as Si llueve, no salgo. — clauses reversed, comma dropped." />

This flexibility holds across every conditional type in this chapter, not just the real/possible one above.

## Synthesis: The Complete Reference Table

### All four types, side by side
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

This table is the payoff of the whole chapter — everything above collapses into one lookup:

| Type | Si-Clause Tense | Main-Clause Tense | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Real / possible | Present indicative | Present indicative, future, or command | *Si llueve, no salgo.* |
| Hypothetical present | Imperfect subjunctive (Ch. 39) | Simple conditional (Ch. 34) | *Si tuviera dinero, viajaría más.* |
| Hypothetical past | Pluperfect subjunctive (Ch. 40) | Compound conditional (Ch. 35) | *Si hubiera estudiado más, habría aprobado el examen.* |
| Mixed-time (past condition, present result) | Pluperfect subjunctive (Ch. 40) | Simple conditional (Ch. 34) | *Si hubiera estudiado, ahora tendría un buen trabajo.* |

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Read the table as a decision checklist: is the condition still open? → real/possible. Is it about now, but not true? → hypothetical present. Is it about the past, with a past result? → hypothetical past. Is it about the past, but the result is felt today? → mixed-time. Once you can place a condition in one of these four boxes, the correct tense pairing follows automatically — it's never a free choice.
</GrammarNote>

### A worked passage: four types in context
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

> (1) Si llueve mañana, cancelaremos la excursión. (2) Aunque, la verdad, si yo fuera el organizador, elegiría una fecha con mejor pronóstico. (3) Si hubiéramos revisado el tiempo antes de reservar, habríamos elegido otro fin de semana. (4) Y ahora, si hubiéramos planeado mejor, no tendríamos este problema en absoluto.

> (1) If it rains tomorrow, we'll cancel the trip. (2) Although, honestly, if I were the organizer, I'd choose a date with a better forecast. (3) If we had checked the weather before booking, we would have chosen a different weekend. (4) And now, if we had planned better, we wouldn't have this problem at all.

| # | Si-Clause | Main Clause | Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Si llueve (present indicative) | cancelaremos (future) | Real/possible — an open, plausible condition. |
| 2 | si yo fuera (imperfect subjunctive) | elegiría (simple conditional) | Hypothetical present — contrary to fact right now (I'm not the organizer). |
| 3 | Si hubiéramos revisado (pluperfect subjunctive) | habríamos elegido (compound conditional) | Hypothetical past — an unrealized past condition with a past-time result. |
| 4 | si hubiéramos planeado (pluperfect subjunctive) | tendríamos (simple conditional) | Mixed-time — a past condition (didn't plan), a consequence felt right now (ahora). |
