*A*, *de*, *en*, and *con* are the four prepositions you'll reach for more than any other in Spanish — small words, but each one carries a cluster of distinct jobs that doesn't map onto a single English preposition. [Chapter 48](../lesson-48) already handled *por* and *para*, the pair whose entire teachable content is the contrast between them; these four are a different kind of chapter — each has its own independent core meaning, and each earns its place here on sheer frequency and range. This chapter builds each preposition's core sense from the ground up, then covers two genuinely distinct grammar points that ride on top of them: **personal *a***, the marker that appears before a specific human direct object with no English word to translate it, and the long list of common verbs that simply come bundled with one fixed preposition, learned verb by verb.

## Core Pattern: The Four Prepositions' Core Meanings

Each of the four does several jobs, but every job traces back to one central idea. Start with that central idea, and the extensions later in this chapter read as natural growth rather than a random pile of uses.

### A: Direction, a Point in Time, and the Indirect Object
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

At its core, *a* marks movement toward a destination or arrival at one specific point — in space or in time.

<ExampleSentence es="Voy a Barcelona este fin de semana." en="I'm going to Barcelona this weekend." note="Direction toward a destination — the most basic sense of a." />
<ExampleSentence es="La clase empieza a las nueve." en="Class starts at nine." note="a marking one specific point in time, not a duration or a span." />

*A* also marks the indirect object — the person a verb's action is done to or for — which is why you'll routinely see it doubled up with an indirect object pronoun ([Chapter 13](../lesson-13) covers that pairing in full):

<ExampleSentence es="Le di el regalo a mi madre." en="I gave the gift to my mother." note="a mi madre names the indirect object; le is the pronoun Chapter 13 requires alongside it." />

*A* has one more job — marking certain direct objects — substantial enough to earn its own full section later in this chapter.

### De: Origin, Possession, and Material
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

*De* marks where something comes from, who it belongs to, or what it's made of — three senses that share one underlying idea: *de* points backward, to a source.

<ExampleSentence es="Soy de los Estados Unidos." en="I'm from the United States." note="Origin — de marking where someone is from." />
<ExampleSentence es="Es el coche de mi hermano." en="It's my brother's car." note="Spanish has no apostrophe-s. Possession is always built with de, in the reverse order from English: literally 'the car of my brother.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="Quiero un anillo de oro." en="I want a gold ring." note="Material — de plus the substance something is made of." />

### En: Location, Transport, and a Span of Time
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

*En* marks being inside or at a location — and, a genuine contrast with English, it covers both "in" and "on" depending on context, since Spanish doesn't draw the same line English does:

<ExampleSentence es="Los libros están en la mesa." en="The books are on the table." note="en covering English 'on' here, not just 'in' — Spanish doesn't split location the way English does." />
<ExampleSentence es="Vivo en Sevilla." en="I live in Seville." />

*En* also marks the means of transport used to get somewhere:

<ExampleSentence es="Vamos en tren, no en coche." en="We're going by train, not by car." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Two everyday exceptions swap in *a* instead of *en* for how you get somewhere: *a pie* (on foot) and *a caballo* (on horseback). *Voy a pie al trabajo*, never *en pie*.
</GrammarNote>

And *en* marks a period of time something happens within, rather than one specific point (*a*'s job):

<ExampleSentence es="Nací en agosto." en="I was born in August." />
<ExampleSentence es="Terminé el examen en una hora." en="I finished the exam within an hour." note="en here means the whole task fit inside that span — a distinct sense from the hace/desde duration system in Chapter 50." />

### Con: Accompaniment, Instrument, and Manner
<LevelBadge level="A1+" />

*Con* marks who or what accompanies an action — a person you're with, a tool you use, or the manner something is done in — three variations on one "together with" idea.

<ExampleSentence es="Voy al cine con mis amigos." en="I'm going to the movies with my friends." note="Accompaniment — the most literal sense of con." />
<ExampleSentence es="Corto el pan con un cuchillo." en="I cut the bread with a knife." note="Instrument — the tool 'accompanying' the action." />
<ExampleSentence es="Habla con cuidado sobre ese tema." en="Speak carefully about that topic." note="Manner — con plus a noun standing in for an adverb (con cuidado ≈ cuidadosamente)." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Con* fuses irregularly with the first- and second-person prepositional pronouns and the reflexive: *conmigo, contigo, consigo* — [Chapter 16](../lesson-16) covers that fusion in full. Everywhere else, *con* behaves normally: *con él, con Ana, con nosotros*.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Contraction and the Verbs These Prepositions Govern

### Contraction: al and del
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

[Chapter 3](../lesson-03) already covers this in full as part of the article system — it's really a fact about *el* more than about *a* or *de* individually — but it's worth a quick reminder here, since *a* and *de* are exactly the two prepositions involved. Whenever *a* or *de* is immediately followed by the article *el*, the two words always fuse: *a + el → al*, *de + el → del*.

<ExampleSentence es="Vamos al parque después del trabajo." en="We're going to the park after work." note="al = a + el, del = de + el — see Chapter 3 for the full mechanics, including the one exception (proper nouns like El Salvador)." />

### Verbs That Take A, De, En, or Con Before a Noun or Pronoun
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A large number of common Spanish verbs come permanently bundled with one specific preposition — not because the meaning demands it, but simply because that's the preposition Spanish fixed on for that verb, the way English fixes "depend ON" and "consist OF" rather than any other choice. [Chapter 42](../lesson-42) already built a list of this same kind, scoped specifically to verbs that link to a *following infinitive* (empezar **A** trabajar, dejar **DE** fumar, soñar **CON** viajar). Many of those very same verbs also govern a noun or pronoun object with that identical preposition — *soñar con ese viaje* works exactly like *soñar con viajar*, just with a thing instead of an action on the other side. Rather than repeat Chapter 42's list, what follows is scoped to the noun/pronoun-object half: verbs whose defining relationship is to a person or thing, not to a following action. Where a verb below also commonly takes an infinitive with that same preposition, that pairing is Chapter 42's territory.

- **a** — *asistir a* (to attend), *jugar a* (to play [a game/sport]), *parecerse a* (to resemble), *acercarse a* (to approach), *oler a* (to smell like), *saber a* (to taste like), *renunciar a* (to give up/resign from)
- **de** — *depender de* (to depend on), *enamorarse de* (to fall in love with), *despedirse de* (to say goodbye to), *quejarse de* (to complain about), *fiarse de* (to trust), *carecer de* (to lack), *constar de* (to consist of/be made up of)
- **en** — *pensar en* (to think about), *confiar en* (to trust), *fijarse en* (to notice), *influir en* (to influence), *convertirse en* (to turn into/become), *especializarse en* (to specialize in)
- **con** — *soñar con* (to dream of), *contar con* (to count on), *casarse con* (to marry), *encontrarse con* (to run into), *enfadarse con* (to get angry with), *comparar con* (to compare to/with)

<ExampleSentence es="Este trabajo depende mucho de la suerte." en="This job depends a lot on luck." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Te acuerdas de aquel verano? Todavía sueño con él." en="Do you remember that summer? I still dream about it." note="sueño con él — soñar con governing a pronoun object, the identical preposition Chapter 42 uses for soñar con viajar." />
<ExampleSentence es="Pienso mucho en ti estos días." en="I think about you a lot these days." note="pensar en governing a pronoun object — contrast with Chapter 42's pensar en + infinitive (pienso en ir, 'I'm thinking about going')." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Consistir en*, not *consistir de* — English "consist of" tempts a direct calque, but Spanish always uses *en* here: *el examen consiste en tres partes*, never *consiste de tres partes*.
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="regional">
*Jugar al fútbol* keeps the *a* (and its contraction into *al*) in Castellano Spanish. You'll commonly hear *jugar fútbol*, without the preposition, in much of Latin America — not an error, just a different regional default.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: Personal A

Every use of *a* so far has mapped onto something English also expresses, one way or another — direction, a time point, an indirect object marker of some kind. Personal *a* is different: it's a use of *a* with no English equivalent whatsoever, a small word inserted purely to signal something about the direct object itself. It trips up English speakers not because the rule is complicated, but because English has nothing to translate it with — it simply vanishes in the English sentence and has to be supplied from the Spanish grammar alone.

### Personal A: Marking a Specific, Animate Direct Object
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

When the direct object of a verb is a specific, identifiable person — or a cherished pet, treated the same way — Spanish inserts *a* immediately before it. It isn't a preposition meaning "to" here; it's purely a marker flagging "the noun that follows is an animate, specific direct object, not the subject."

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Veo", role: "verb" },
    { text: "a", role: "connector" },
    { text: "María", role: "object" }
  ]}
/>

Compare that to an inanimate direct object, which never takes personal *a*:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Veo", role: "verb" },
    { text: "la película", role: "object" }
  ]}
/>

*Veo María*, with no *a* at all, is simply ungrammatical here — not informal, not a minor slip, genuinely wrong, the same way a missing required word would be. The logic isn't really "is this a person" in isolation; it's animacy *plus* specificity together. A specific, identifiable human or beloved animal as a direct object needs the marker; an inanimate thing never does, no matter how definite it is.

<ExampleSentence es="Quiero mucho a mi perro." en="I love my dog a lot." note="A cherished pet as direct object takes personal a, just like a person would." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Conoces a Marta?" en="Do you know Marta?" note="A specific, named person as direct object — personal a is required." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Conoces bien Madrid?" en="Do you know Madrid well?" note="Places don't take personal a even with the same verb conocer — animacy is what decides this, not merely 'is the object known or specific.'" />

### When Personal A Is Dropped or Optional
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Two situations soften the rule above. First, *tener* almost always drops personal *a* even before a human direct object, when it expresses simple possession or relationship rather than physically holding or locating someone:

<ExampleSentence es="Tengo tres hermanos." en="I have three brothers." note="No personal a with tener expressing a relationship — a well-known exception to the rule above." />

But once *tener* shifts to mean physically keeping or locating a specific person somewhere, personal *a* comes back:

<ExampleSentence es="Tengo a mi hijo en el hospital." en="I have my son in the hospital [right now]." note="tener isn't expressing a family relationship here — it's locating a specific person, closer to 'I'm keeping/have my son there,' which restores personal a." />

Second, when the direct object is a person but genuinely non-specific — any member of a category, not one particular individual in mind — personal *a* is often dropped, the same way the indefinite article gets dropped for unspecified quantities ([Chapter 3](../lesson-03)):

<ExampleSentence es="Busco un médico que hable inglés." en="I'm looking for a doctor who speaks English." note="Any doctor fitting the description — non-specific, so no personal a. Compare Busco al médico que me atendió ayer (the one specific doctor who treated me yesterday), which does take personal a." />

### Personal A with Indefinite Pronouns, and Telling It Apart from the Indirect-Object A
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The indefinite pronouns *alguien*, *nadie*, and *quién* always count as specific-enough people for this rule, even though they don't name anyone in particular — treat them as automatically triggering personal *a* whenever they're a direct object:

<ExampleSentence es="No veo a nadie en la calle." en="I don't see anyone in the street." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿A quién buscáis?" en="Who are you all looking for?" note="Castellano vosotros form (buscáis) — quién as a direct object always takes personal a, question word or not." />

One more thing worth separating cleanly: personal *a* and the indirect-object *a* ([Chapter 13](../lesson-13)) are two different jobs wearing the identical word. A single sentence can use both at once, marking two different objects:

<ExampleSentence es="Le presenté a mi hermano a mi jefe." en="I introduced my brother to my boss." note="The first a mi hermano is personal a marking the direct object; the second a mi jefe is the indirect-object phrase from Chapter 13, doubled by the required pronoun le. Same word, two separate grammatical jobs in one sentence." />

## Bringing It Together

Faced with *a*, *de*, *en*, or *con*, start from each one's core sense — direction/time-point/indirect-object for *a*, origin/possession/material for *de*, location/transport/time-span for *en*, accompaniment/instrument/manner for *con* — and most everyday uses fall out naturally from there. Two things don't fall out of core meaning alone and simply have to be learned as their own facts: which preposition a specific verb happens to be bundled with (memorize verb by verb, using the reference list above), and personal *a*, which isn't really about meaning at all — it's a marker that appears whenever a direct object is a specific, animate being, with no English word to translate it.

<ExampleSentence es="Conozco a la mujer de mi vecino: vive en Sevilla, viaja siempre con su hermana, y se parece mucho a su madre." en="I know my neighbor's wife: she lives in Seville, she always travels with her sister, and she looks a lot like her mother." note="a la mujer = personal a; de mi vecino = possession; en Sevilla = location; con su hermana = accompaniment; se parece a su madre = the governed-preposition verb parecerse a." />
