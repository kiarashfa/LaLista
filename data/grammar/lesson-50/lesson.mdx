Beyond *por*, *para*, and the core four from the last two chapters, Spanish has a second, smaller tier of prepositions — each one narrower in range, but each still earning its own place because no other word does quite its job. This chapter works through that tier as a scannable inventory, moves on to the handful of fixed prepositional phrases built by combining ordinary words, and finishes with one of Spanish's most genuinely confusing systems for an English speaker: three closely related ways — *desde*, *desde hace*, and *hace...que* — of saying how long something has been going on, each built on a different piece of grammar and each pairing with a different tense.

## Core Pattern: A Working Inventory of the Smaller Prepositions

The nine prepositions below are worth learning as one set, since several form natural pairs or contrasts with each other. Each gets one core meaning and a couple of examples — treat this section as a reference to come back to, not something to memorize in one sitting.

### Sin, Sobre, and Bajo: Absence, Surface, and Underneath
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

*Sin* means "without":

<ExampleSentence es="Prefiero el café sin azúcar." en="I prefer coffee without sugar." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se fue sin decir adiós." en="He left without saying goodbye." note="sin, like every Spanish preposition, is always followed by the infinitive, never a gerund (Chapter 42)." />

*Sobre* covers three related senses: on top of something (physical), about a topic, and — with a clock time — "around, approximately":

<ExampleSentence es="El mando está sobre la mesa." en="The remote is on the table." />
<ExampleSentence es="El documental trata sobre el cambio climático." en="The documentary is about climate change." />
<ExampleSentence es="Llegamos sobre las ocho." en="We'll arrive around eight." note="sobre plus a clock time means 'around/approximately' — a third sense distinct from 'on top of' and 'about.'" />

*Bajo* means "under, beneath," used both literally and figuratively:

<ExampleSentence es="El gato duerme bajo la cama." en="The cat sleeps under the bed." />
<ExampleSentence es="Bajo estas circunstancias, prefiero esperar." en="Under these circumstances, I'd rather wait." note="A common figurative extension — bajo introducing the conditions something happens under, not just physical position." />

### Entre: Between and Among
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Entre* covers both "between" (two things) and "among" (more than two) — Spanish doesn't split these the way English does:

<ExampleSentence es="La tienda está entre el banco y la farmacia." en="The store is between the bank and the pharmacy." />
<ExampleSentence es="El ganador está entre estos tres candidatos." en="The winner is among these three candidates." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Entre* has one genuine irregularity: it pairs with the ordinary subject pronouns *tú* and *yo*, not the prepositional-pronoun forms *ti* and *mí* that every other preposition uses ([Chapter 16](../lesson-16)). *Esto queda entre tú y yo* — never *entre ti y mí*.
</GrammarNote>

### Hacia and Hasta: Direction Without Arrival vs. a Boundary Reached
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Hacia* ("toward") describes the direction of movement without claiming arrival. *Hasta* ("up to, as far as, until") marks a limit actually reached:

<ExampleSentence es="Caminamos hacia el centro." en="We walked toward downtown." note="hacia says nothing about whether you arrived — just the direction of movement." />
<ExampleSentence es="Caminamos hasta el centro." en="We walked all the way downtown." note="hasta marks the walk's endpoint as reached — contrast with hacia above." />
<ExampleSentence es="La tienda está abierta hasta las diez." en="The store is open until ten." note="The same 'boundary reached' logic, extended from a place to a time limit." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Hasta* also works as a plain adverb meaning "even": *hasta yo lo entendí* ("even I understood it") — a common, useful extra meaning worth recognizing outside its prepositional use.
</GrammarNote>

### Desde: A Starting Point in Space or Time
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Desde* marks the point something starts from — spatial or temporal:

<ExampleSentence es="Desde mi ventana se ve el mar." en="From my window you can see the sea." note="A spatial starting point." />
<ExampleSentence es="Trabajo aquí desde marzo." en="I've worked here since March." note="A temporal starting point — this time-expression use of desde gets its full treatment, alongside desde hace and hace...que, later in this chapter." />

### Ante and Tras: Formal "Before" and "After"
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Ante* and *tras* are more written and formal than their everyday counterparts, common in journalism, official language, and literary prose:

<ExampleSentence es="Ante tantas dudas, decidió consultarlo con un abogado." en="Faced with so many doubts, he decided to consult a lawyer." note="ante here means 'faced with/in the presence of,' almost always figurative." />
<ExampleSentence es="Tras el partido, los dos equipos se saludaron." en="After the match, the two teams greeted each other." note="tras is common in written and journalistic Spanish; everyday speech usually reaches for después de instead." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Ante* ≠ *antes de*. *Ante* means "in the face of / before" in the sense of standing in front of someone or something, usually figuratively; *antes de* is strictly temporal ("before" in time). *Ante la ley* (before the law) and *antes de la reunión* (before the meeting) are not interchangeable.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Compound Prepositional Phrases

### Everyday Location Phrases Built with De
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Spanish builds a large family of location prepositions by combining an adverb with *de* — and in everyday speech, several of these are actually more common than the single-word formal prepositions above for the same idea (*debajo de* instead of *bajo*, *delante de/detrás de* instead of *ante/tras*):

| Phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| debajo de | underneath |
| encima de | on top of |
| delante de | in front of |
| detrás de | behind |
| cerca de | near |
| lejos de | far from |
| dentro de | inside |
| fuera de | outside |
| alrededor de | around |

<ExampleSentence es="El perro duerme debajo de la mesa." en="The dog sleeps under the table." />
<ExampleSentence es="Vivimos cerca de la playa, pero lejos del centro." en="We live near the beach, but far from downtown." note="lejos del = lejos de + el — the same al/del contraction rule from Chapter 3 applies here too, since de is still de." />

### Fixed Prepositional Phrases
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A closed, useful list of compound phrases built from a fixed combination of words, each doing the job of a single preposition:

| Phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| a través de | through, by means of |
| a pesar de | despite, in spite of |
| en cuanto a | as for, regarding |
| junto a | next to, together with |
| además de | besides, in addition to |
| en lugar de / en vez de | instead of |
| acerca de | about, concerning |
| frente a | facing, opposite |

<ExampleSentence es="Conseguimos el permiso a través de un amigo." en="We got the permit through a friend." />
<ExampleSentence es="A pesar del tráfico, llegamos a tiempo." en="Despite the traffic, we arrived on time." note="a pesar del = a pesar de + el, another del contraction." />
<ExampleSentence es="El hotel está junto a la playa, no frente a ella." en="The hotel is next to the beach, not facing it." note="junto a (right beside) and frente a (facing, across from) describe different spatial relationships and are easy to mix up." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: Desde vs. Desde Hace vs. Hace...Que

[Chapter 47](../lesson-47) already introduced *hace* + time + *que* for describing an action that's been going on for a stretch of time, and promised the full system belonged here. Spanish gives you three overlapping ways to talk about duration and starting points, and the genuine difficulty isn't any one of them alone — it's knowing which one fits which situation, and which tense each one pairs with.

### Hace + Time + Que, and Its Twin, Desde Hace
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

To say an action has been going on for a length of time and is still true right now, Spanish offers two equally natural, interchangeable word orders — both pairing with the present tense, because the action is still happening as you speak:

<ExampleSentence es="Hace tres años que vivo en Barcelona." en="I've lived in Barcelona for three years." note="Hace + [duration] + que + present tense — que introduces the clause describing what's been going on." />
<ExampleSentence es="Vivo en Barcelona desde hace tres años." en="I've lived in Barcelona for three years." note="Exactly the same meaning as the sentence above, just built verb-first with desde hace instead of hace...que — Spanish genuinely offers both orders." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The tense is the trap here: English reaches for a perfect ("I have lived"), but Spanish uses the plain present (*vivo*), because from Spanish's point of view the action is simply happening right now — not completed, nothing for *haber* to mark. *He vivido en Barcelona hace tres años* is not what this pattern means.
</GrammarNote>

### Desde: Marking the Starting Point Itself
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Desde* is different from the two patterns above in one key way: it doesn't state a duration at all — it names the specific point something started from, and lets the listener work out how long that is.

<ExampleSentence es="Vivo en Barcelona desde 2021." en="I've lived in Barcelona since 2021." note="desde + a specific point (a year, a date, an event) — not a length of time the way hace tres años is." />

When that starting point is itself an event rather than a date, *desde que* introduces a full clause, and the event is usually marked with the preterite — the tense for a bounded, completed action ([Chapter 26](../lesson-26)) — even though the main clause describes something still ongoing:

<ExampleSentence es="No he vuelto a fumar desde que lo dejé." en="I haven't smoked again since I quit." note="dejé (preterite) marks the one-time event that started the situation; he vuelto (present perfect) describes the ongoing result — Chapter 26's preterite doing its normal job inside this larger pattern." />

### The Past-Tense Parallel: Hacía...Que and Desde Hacía
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Move the whole situation into the past — describing a duration that was still ongoing at some earlier moment, not right now — and every piece above shifts to the imperfect ([Chapter 28](../lesson-28)), for exactly the reason the imperfect exists: an ongoing state with no defined endpoint from the perspective of that past moment.

<ExampleSentence es="Hacía tres años que vivía en Barcelona cuando conocí a mi esposa." en="I had been living in Barcelona for three years when I met my wife." note="Hacía (imperfect) + [duration] + que + vivía (imperfect) — both halves shift to the imperfect together, mirroring hace...que's present-tense pairing one tense back." />
<ExampleSentence es="Vivía en Barcelona desde hacía tres años cuando la conocí." en="I had been living in Barcelona for three years when I met her." note="The same desde hace reordering from the present-tense pattern above, shifted one tense back to desde hacía." />

*Desde* itself, pointing at a starting event rather than a duration, usually keeps the preterite for that starting event even inside a past-tense sentence — only the main, ongoing-result clause shifts back to the imperfect:

<ExampleSentence es="No fumaba desde que lo dejó." en="He hadn't been smoking since he quit." note="dejó (preterite) marks the bounded quitting event; fumaba (imperfect) describes the ongoing non-smoking state around it — the exact same desde que pattern as the present-tense example above, moved one tense back." />

### Summary Table and Common Mistakes
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

| Pattern | Structure | Tense | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| hace...que | Hace + duration + que + verb | Present | "for [duration]," ongoing now |
| desde hace | verb + desde hace + duration | Present | Same meaning, reordered |
| desde | verb + desde + point | Present / Present Perfect | "since [point]," a starting moment |
| hacía...que | Hacía + duration + que + verb | Imperfect | "had been [duration]," ongoing at a past moment |
| desde hacía | verb + desde hacía + duration | Imperfect | Same meaning, reordered |

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Spain also very commonly expresses this same ongoing-duration idea with *llevar* + time + gerundio — *llevo tres años viviendo en Barcelona*, identical in meaning to both present-tense patterns above and arguably even more colloquial ([Chapter 45](../lesson-45) covers *llevar* + gerund in full). You may also encounter *tener* + time + gerundio (*tengo tres años viviendo aquí*) for the same idea — that construction is characteristic of Mexican and Caribbean Spanish and much less common in Castellano.
</GrammarNote>

The most common errors with this system, in order of how often they show up: reaching for a perfect tense (*he vivido*) instead of the plain present when the action is still ongoing; forgetting to shift *hace* to *hacía* when narrating in the past; and pairing *desde* directly with a bare duration instead of a point.

<ExampleSentence es="Vivo aquí desde hace tres años, no 'desde tres años.'" en="I've lived here for three years, not the ungrammatical 'since three years.'" note="desde needs a point (desde 2021, desde que llegué), never a bare duration on its own — pair a duration with hace or desde hace, not desde alone." />

## Bringing It Together

Faced with a preposition beyond *por/para/a/de/en/con*, most of the work is simply knowing which narrow job each one owns — *sin* for absence, *sobre/bajo* for surface and underneath, *entre* for between/among, *hacia/hasta* for direction versus a reached limit, *ante/tras* for formal before/after. The compound *de*-phrases fill in the rest of everyday spatial and logical relationships. And when duration or a starting point is the actual question, work through it in order: still ongoing right now → *hace...que* or *desde hace*, both present tense; a specific starting point rather than a length → *desde*; the whole situation moved into the past → shift every piece one tense back, to *hacía...que* and *desde hacía*.

<ExampleSentence es="Vivo en Madrid desde hace cinco años, aunque he viajado mucho a través de Europa. A pesar de eso, sigo enamorado de esta ciudad, y no pienso irme de aquí." en="I've lived in Madrid for five years, although I've traveled a lot throughout Europe. Despite that, I'm still in love with this city, and I don't plan on leaving here." note="desde hace (ongoing duration), a través de (through), a pesar de (despite) — several of this chapter's constructions in one natural passage." />
