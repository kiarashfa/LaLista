[Chapter 22](../lesson-22) covered the -ar/-er/-ir pattern that most Spanish verbs follow without exception. This chapter covers the verbs that don't — and there are a lot of them, including some of the most common verbs in the language: *querer, poder, tener, ir, ser, decir.* You reach for these constantly, so it's worth knowing up front that native speakers don't experience them as a random pile of exceptions memorized one by one. They cluster into a small number of recognizable families, and once you can name which family a new verb belongs to, you already know how it conjugates. That's the real skill this chapter builds — not memorizing hundreds of individual verbs, but recognizing which of a handful of patterns a verb falls into.

## Core Pattern: The E → IE Stem Change

### The boot shape: yo, tú, él/ella/usted, ellos/ellas/ustedes
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The single largest family of present-tense irregulars is the **stem-changing** (or "boot") verbs. In four of the six forms — *yo, tú, él/ella/usted,* and *ellos/ellas/ustedes* — the last vowel of the stem shifts, while *nosotros* and *vosotros* keep the stem exactly as it appears in the infinitive. Drawing a line around the four changed forms in a two-column grid traces a boot, or shoe, shape — which is where the traditional name comes from:

<BootTable
  label="perder"
  caption="present tense"
  forms={{
    "yo": "pierdo",
    "tú": "pierdes",
    "él/ella/usted": "pierde",
    "nosotros": "perdemos",
    "vosotros": "perdéis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "pierden"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Siempre pierdo las llaves." en="I always lose my keys." />
<ExampleSentence es="Nosotros nunca perdemos la paciencia." en="We never lose patience." note="Nosotros keeps the plain perd- stem — no stem change outside the boot." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
The reason nosotros/vosotros escape the change isn't arbitrary: Spanish stem changes only ever affect a *stressed* stem vowel. In pierdo, pierdes, pierde, and pierden, the stress falls on the stem itself (PIER-do). In perdemos and perdéis, the stress shifts onto the ending (per-DE-mos, per-DÉIS), so the stem is unstressed and simply stays as it is in the infinitive. Once you hear it as a stress pattern rather than a memorized list, the boot shape stops being arbitrary.
</GrammarNote>

### More e→ie verbs
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The same pattern governs a long list of common verbs across all three conjugations: *empezar, pensar, cerrar, comenzar* (-ar); *entender, querer* (-er); *preferir, sentir, mentir* (-ir).

<ExampleSentence es="¿En qué piensas?" en="What are you thinking about?" />
<ExampleSentence es="Prefiero el té al café." en="I prefer tea to coffee." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
The -ir verbs in this list (preferir, sentir, mentir) have one extra layer: they show a *second*, different vowel change in the gerund and in certain preterite forms (prefiriendo, sintió). That's not part of the present tense — it belongs to [Chapter 25](../lesson-25) (gerunds) and [Chapter 27](../lesson-27) (preterite irregulars) — but it's worth knowing the two changes are related, not two unrelated irregularities to memorize separately.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: The Other Stem-Change Families

The same boot-shaped logic — stressed stem vowel changes, nosotros/vosotros don't — extends to two more vowel patterns. Once e→ie is second nature, these are the same skill applied to a different starting vowel.

### O → UE
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

<BootTable
  label="dormir"
  caption="present tense"
  forms={{
    "yo": "duermo",
    "tú": "duermes",
    "él/ella/usted": "duerme",
    "nosotros": "dormimos",
    "vosotros": "dormís",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "duermen"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Duermo ocho horas cada noche." en="I sleep eight hours every night." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Puedes ayudarme?" en="Can you help me?" note="poder follows the identical pattern: puedo, puedes, puede, podemos, podéis, pueden." />

Other common o→ue verbs: *poder, volver, contar, encontrar, recordar, costar, morir.*

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Jugar ("to play") is a lone wolf: it changes u→ue rather than o→ue (juego, juegas, juega, jugamos, jugáis, juegan), simply because its stem vowel is a u to begin with. It's grouped with this family by logic, not by literal vowel — the same stress rule is doing the same work.
</GrammarNote>

### E → I (only in -IR verbs)
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

<BootTable
  label="pedir"
  caption="present tense"
  forms={{
    "yo": "pido",
    "tú": "pides",
    "él/ella/usted": "pide",
    "nosotros": "pedimos",
    "vosotros": "pedís",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "piden"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Siempre pido la misma tapa." en="I always order the same tapa." />

Other e→i verbs: *servir, repetir, vestir(se), seguir, elegir.* This third pattern is narrower than the other two in one useful way: it **only ever happens in -ir verbs.** There's no -ar or -er verb anywhere in Spanish that changes e→i — if you see that change, you already know the verb ends in -ir.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A quick way to hold all three families in your head: e→ie is the most common and shows up across all three conjugations; o→ue is the second most common and also crosses all three; e→i is the rarest and is locked to -ir verbs only. All three share the same rule underneath — a stressed stem vowel changes, an unstressed one (nosotros/vosotros) doesn't.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Yo-irregulars: g-insertion, sometimes teamed up with a stem change
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

A separate family of verbs adds a **g** to the yo form only — a spelling/sound irregularity, not a stem-vowel one. *Tener* is the clearest example, and it happens to combine both irregularities at once: g-insertion in yo, plus the e→ie stem change everywhere else in the boot:

<BootTable
  label="tener"
  caption="present tense"
  forms={{
    "yo": "tengo",
    "tú": "tienes",
    "él/ella/usted": "tiene",
    "nosotros": "tenemos",
    "vosotros": "tenéis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "tienen"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Tengo dos hermanos." en="I have two siblings." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Tienes hambre?" en="Are you hungry?" />

All four boot cells here differ from the nosotros/vosotros baseline (tenemos, tenéis), so the outline still traces the familiar four-cell boot — but for two genuinely different reasons stacked together: *tengo* breaks the pattern by adding a consonant, while *tienes/tiene/tienen* break it with the ordinary e→ie vowel shift. *Venir* (vengo, vienes, viene, venimos, venís, vienen) and *decir* (digo, dices, dice, decimos, decís, dicen — g-insertion plus e→i this time) work the same combined way.

### Pure g-insertion: only the yo form changes
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Many more common verbs add the same g in yo but are otherwise completely regular — no stem change anywhere else in the paradigm:

<BootTable
  label="hacer"
  caption="present tense"
  forms={{
    "yo": "hago",
    "tú": "haces",
    "él/ella/usted": "hace",
    "nosotros": "hacemos",
    "vosotros": "hacéis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "hacen"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Hago la cena todos los martes." en="I make dinner every Tuesday." />
<ExampleSentence es="Él hace ejercicio por la mañana." en="He exercises in the morning." note="hace is perfectly regular — only hago (yo) is irregular." />

Here, only one of the four "boot" cells is actually irregular — *haces, hace,* and *hacen* follow the plain regular pattern from Chapter 22. The irregularity traces a single cell around *yo*, not a four-cell boot. It's still a genuine, worth-learning irregularity — just not boot-shaped, since three of the four boot cells behave normally. *Salir* (salgo, sales, sale, salimos, salís, salen), *poner* (pongo, pones...), *traer* (traigo, traes...), and *caer* (caigo, caes...) all work the same single-cell way.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Four otherwise unrelated verbs — ser, estar, dar, and ir — share a small family resemblance in their own yo forms: soy, estoy, doy, voy all end in -oy. It isn't a rule you can apply to new verbs, but recognizing the cluster makes all four easier to hold onto together.
</GrammarNote>

### The -zco family: conocer, conducir, and relatives
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A related but distinct single-cell pattern: verbs ending in a vowel + *-cer* or *-cir* insert a z before the c in the yo form only, to keep the /θ/ sound consistent before the o ending.

| Infinitive | yo form |
|---|---|
| conocer (to know/be familiar with) | conozco |
| conducir (to drive) | conduzco |
| traducir (to translate) | traduzco |
| parecer (to seem) | parezco |
| ofrecer (to offer) | ofrezco |
| agradecer (to thank) | agradezco |

<ExampleSentence es="Conozco a tu hermana." en="I know your sister." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Don't confuse conocer with saber — both translate as "to know," but conocer is for people, places, and familiarity (conozco Madrid), while saber is for facts and skills (sé la respuesta). Saber has its own single-cell yo irregularity: sé.
</GrammarNote>

### Orthographic-only changes: preserving the sound, not changing it
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A final small family isn't really an irregularity at all in the spoken language — only in spelling. Spanish g and c change pronunciation depending on the vowel that follows them, so when a verb's stem ends in -ger, -gir, or -cer/-cir with a *hard* consonant sound, the yo form has to swap the letter to keep the sound the same before the -o ending:

| Infinitive | yo form | Why |
|---|---|---|
| coger (to take/grab) | cojo | g before o would sound hard; j keeps the /x/ sound |
| escoger (to choose) | escojo | same |
| proteger (to protect) | protejo | same |
| dirigir (to direct) | dirijo | same g→j logic before o |
| exigir (to demand) | exijo | same |
| elegir (to choose/elect) | elijo | g→j, *and* e→i stem change (eliges, elige, eligen) |
| vencer (to defeat) | venzo | c before o would lose its /θ/ sound; z keeps it |
| convencer (to convince) | convenzo | same |
| seguir (to follow/continue) | sigo | gu→g before o, *and* e→i stem change (sigues, sigue, siguen) |

<GrammarNote type="warning">
These aren't pronunciation changes at all — *cojo* and a hypothetical *"cogo"* would sound different, and Spanish spelling rules simply don't allow *"cogo."* Contrast this with the true irregularities above (tengo, hago, conozco), where the sound itself is genuinely different from what the regular pattern would produce. Here, the sound was never going to change — only the letter has to.
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Hold onto seguir (sigo) — it comes back in [Chapter 25](../lesson-25) as part of the "progressive family" of verbs (seguir + gerund, "to keep on doing something").
</GrammarNote>

### Fully irregular verbs: ser, estar, ir, haber
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

A handful of the most essential verbs in Spanish don't fit any family above — their forms come from entirely different, fused-together historical roots, a pattern linguists call *suppletion.* *Ir* is the clearest case:

<BootTable
  label="ir"
  caption="present tense"
  forms={{
    "yo": "voy",
    "tú": "vas",
    "él/ella/usted": "va",
    "nosotros": "vamos",
    "vosotros": "vais",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "van"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Voy al mercado los sábados." en="I go to the market on Saturdays." />

Notice there's no meaningful boot outline to draw here: the boot-table logic works by contrasting the yo/tú/él-ella-usted/ellos-ellas-ustedes forms against a "regular" nosotros/vosotros baseline, but *vamos* and *vais* are just as irregular — just as disconnected from a plain infinitive stem "i-" — as the rest of the paradigm. When every cell is irregular, including the ones that normally serve as the baseline, there's no unaffected shape left to trace a boot around.

*Ser, estar,* and *haber* are irregular the same fundamental way, though each keeps slightly more of a recognizable pattern:

| | yo | tú | él/ella/usted | nosotros | vosotros | ellos/ellas/ustedes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ser | soy | eres | es | somos | sois | son |
| estar | estoy | estás | está | estamos | estáis | están |
| haber | he | has | ha | hemos | habéis | han |

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Look closely at estar and you'll actually spot a genuine boot: estás, está, and están all carry a written accent that estamos and estáis don't. That's estar's own irregular pattern showing through, not something new — the full ser-vs-estar decision framework belongs to [Chapter 21](../lesson-21), which this chapter assumes rather than re-teaches.
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Haber is rarely used the way this table might suggest. Its personal forms (he, has, ha...) function almost exclusively as the auxiliary verb in compound tenses (he comido — "I have eaten," full treatment in [Chapter 30](../lesson-30)), and its existential meaning ("there is/are") uses the separate invariant form hay, not any of the six forms above. Haber never means "to have" in the sense of possession — that job belongs to tener.
</GrammarNote>

### A common learner error: overgeneralizing the boot
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Once the e→ie/o→ue/e→i pattern clicks, a predictable new mistake shows up: extending the stem change into nosotros or vosotros, where it never belongs.

<ExampleSentence es="Nosotros pensamos ir al cine, no nosotros piensamos ir al cine." en="We're planning to go to the movies." note="pensamos is correct; piensamos is a common overcorrection once a learner has internalized pienso, piensas, piensa." />

The fix is the same stress logic from the start of this chapter: nosotros and vosotros always stress the ending, never the stem, so the stem always stays in its plain infinitive form — no exceptions among any of the three vowel families.

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Cuando tengo hambre, pido algo de comer; si no puedo decidir, prefiero lo que pide mi hermano. Nosotros nunca perdemos tiempo pensando demasiado — simplemente vamos al restaurante y ya." en="When I'm hungry, I order something to eat; if I can't decide, I prefer whatever my brother orders. We never waste time overthinking it — we just go to the restaurant and that's that." note="tengo (yo-irregular), pido and prefiero (e→i, e→ie), puedo (o→ue), perdemos and pensando (regular nosotros forms, no stem change), vamos (fully irregular)." />

Faced with a new verb, work through it in this order: first, is it one of the handful of fully irregular verbs (ser, estar, ir, haber, and a few others you'll simply memorize)? If not, does the stressed stem vowel show e or o, and is it a verb you recognize as a stem-changer? If not, check the yo form specifically — does it need a g, a z, or a spelling swap to keep its sound? If none of that applies, it's simply regular, and Chapter 22's pattern covers it completely.
