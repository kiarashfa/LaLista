An indirect object is the person (almost always a person) who receives, benefits from, or loses out on the action described by the direct object — the *to whom* or *for whom* hiding inside a sentence like "I gave the book **to María**" or "I bought **her** a gift." English sometimes marks this with "to/for," sometimes drops it and just reorders the sentence ("I gave María the book"). Spanish marks it with its own short pronoun, *le* or *les*, and that pronoun follows exactly the placement system you already learned for direct object pronouns in [Chapter 12](../lesson-12). What's genuinely new here isn't where the pronoun goes — it's how it behaves once it's there: it tolerates, and often demands, the very thing direct object pronouns avoid — sitting right next to the noun it refers to.

## Core Pattern: The Indirect Object Pronoun Set & Placement

### The six indirect object pronoun forms
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Indirect object pronouns share four of their six forms with direct object pronouns ([Chapter 12](../lesson-12)) — only the third person differs:

<BootTable
  label="Indirect Object Pronouns"
  forms={{
    "yo": "me",
    "tú": "te",
    "él/ella/usted": "le",
    "nosotros": "nos",
    "vosotros": "os",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "les"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Le escribo un correo." en="I'm writing him/her/you (formal) an email." note="le covers él, ella, and usted — all three collapse into one form." />
<ExampleSentence es="Les mandé una invitación." en="I sent them/you all an invitation." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Unlike *lo/la/los/las*, the indirect object forms *le* and *les* never change for gender — there's no *"la"* or *"les/las"* split here. That single invariant third-person form is also exactly what makes *le* ambiguous enough to need the clarifying strategies covered later in this chapter.
</GrammarNote>

### Placement: the same rules as direct object pronouns
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Indirect object pronouns follow the identical placement system taught in full in [Chapter 12](../lesson-12), so it's only restated briefly here rather than re-derived: a bare conjugated verb takes the pronoun immediately in front of it; an infinitive or gerund gives a free choice between attaching the pronoun to the end or moving it before the whole verb phrase; an affirmative command requires attachment (with the same written-accent rule), while a negative command sends the pronoun back to its normal pre-verb position.

<ExampleSentence es="Le doy el regalo." en="I give him/her the gift." note="Bare conjugated verb — pronoun goes directly in front, same as a direct object pronoun would." />
<ExampleSentence es="Quiero darle el regalo. / Le quiero dar el regalo." en="I want to give him/her the gift." note="Infinitive present — both positions are equally correct, exactly as in Chapter 12." />
<ExampleSentence es="Dale el regalo. / No le des el regalo." en="Give him/her the gift. / Don't give him/her the gift." note="Affirmative command attaches le; negative command keeps it in front." />

If any of these placement patterns feel unfamiliar, [Chapter 12](../lesson-12) is where they're taught from the ground up — this chapter assumes that groundwork and builds on it rather than repeating it.

## Building Out: What Makes Indirect Objects Different

### Clarifying or emphasizing *le/les* with *a* + a person
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Because *le* collapses "him," "her," and formal "you" into one word, it's often genuinely unclear who it refers to out of context. Spanish resolves that ambiguity — and separately, adds emphasis even when there's no ambiguity to resolve — by adding an *a* + prepositional pronoun or noun phrase alongside *le/les*. The prepositional pronoun set (*a mí, a ti, a él, a ella, a usted...*) gets full treatment in [Chapter 16](../lesson-16):

<ExampleSentence es="Le hablo a ella, no a él." en="I'm talking to her, not to him." note="Without a ella, le alone couldn't distinguish 'her' from 'him' or 'you formal.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="A mí me encanta esta canción." en="I really love this song." note="Purely emphatic here — me alone is already unambiguous, but a mí adds emphasis, similar to stressing 'I' in English." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Notice the pronoun *le/les* doesn't disappear when the clarifying phrase shows up — both stay. That's your first hint of this chapter's central theme: indirect object pronouns are comfortable appearing right alongside the noun or pronoun they refer to, which direct object pronouns generally aren't ([Chapter 12](../lesson-12)).
</GrammarNote>

### Doubling: *le/les* appearing alongside the full noun
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The clarifying pattern above extends past pronouns to full noun phrases, and this is where indirect objects genuinely part ways with direct objects. Spanish routinely keeps *le/les* even when the noun it refers to is spelled out right there in the same sentence:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Le", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "di", role: "verb" },
    { text: "el libro", role: "object" },
    { text: "a María.", role: "other" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Le di el libro a María." en="I gave the book to María." note="Both le and a María appear — this is normal, natural Spanish, not redundant or wrong." />
<ExampleSentence es="Les compré regalos a mis padres." en="I bought gifts for my parents." note="Les anticipates a mis padres; native speakers say this constantly." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
English intuition points the wrong way here. A direct translation instinct says the pronoun and the noun are doing the same job, so surely only one should appear — that's true for direct objects (*Compré el libro y lo leí*, never *lo leí el libro*) but not for indirect objects. Dropping *le* here — *"Di el libro a María"* — isn't ungrammatical, but it sounds noticeably less natural than keeping it. Native speakers default to doubling.
</GrammarNote>

### When doubling becomes required: a fronted indirect object
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Doubling goes from "strongly preferred" to genuinely obligatory in one specific case: when the indirect object noun phrase is moved before the verb for emphasis or topic focus. Drop *le/les* here and the sentence is simply ungrammatical:

<ExampleSentence es="A María le di el libro." en="To María, I gave the book." note="María fronted for emphasis — le is now mandatory. A María di el libro is not acceptable Spanish." />
<ExampleSentence es="A los niños les encanta el parque." en="The kids love the park." note="Same fronting pattern, and a preview of the gustar-type verbs covered just below — les is required once los niños is fronted." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Verbs that grammatically require an indirect object where English doesn't
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A small but heavily-used group of Spanish verbs structure their whole sentence around an indirect object even though the closest English translation doesn't feel like it has one. *Gustar* is the flagship example: Spanish doesn't really say "I like the book" — it says something closer to "the book is pleasing **to me**," with *me* as an obligatory indirect object pronoun and *el libro* as the grammatical subject:

<ExampleSentence es="Me gusta el libro." en="I like the book." note="Literally closer to 'the book is pleasing to me' — el libro is the subject, me is the indirect object, and gusta agrees with el libro, not with me." />
<ExampleSentence es="A ella le encantan los conciertos." en="She loves concerts." note="Same pattern, plus the fronted-phrase doubling rule from just above — a ella requires le." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is only a preview. *Gustar* and its whole family (*encantar, importar, molestar, faltar, doler, interesar...*) get complete treatment in [Chapter 24](../lesson-24), including why the verb agrees with what's being liked rather than with the person doing the liking. What matters here is just recognizing the shape: if you see an unexpected *le/les* with a verb like this, it's not a mistake — it's this same indirect-object machinery at work.
</GrammarNote>

### Doubling vs. replacing: the real contrast with direct objects
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

It's worth stating the contrast with [Chapter 12](../lesson-12) plainly, because it's the single most useful takeaway of this chapter: direct object pronouns normally **replace** the noun — you pick the pronoun or the noun, not both. Indirect object pronouns normally **accompany** the noun — doubling, not replacement, is the default:

| | Direct object (Ch. 12) | Indirect object (this chapter) |
|---|---|---|
| With the noun spelled out | *Veo a María.* | *Le doy el libro a María.* |
| With a pronoun only | *La veo.* | *Le doy el libro.* |
| With both together | Not normal Spanish (*La veo a María*) | Normal, even preferred, Spanish |

<GrammarNote type="regional">
If you ever see *le* used where you'd expect a direct object pronoun (*le* for a masculine person instead of *lo*), that's *leísmo* — a separate, widespread Castellano phenomenon covered fully in [Chapter 12](../lesson-12), not a doubling issue. The reverse error — using *lo/la* where grammar calls for *le* — is *loísmo/laísmo*, also mapped in that chapter's regional table.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="A mi abuela le encantan las flores, así que siempre le compro un ramo cuando la visito." en="My grandmother loves flowers, so I always buy her a bouquet when I visit her." note="A mi abuela fronted and doubled by le (required); le compro doubling a benefactor who's never even named again as a noun; la visito is an ordinary direct object pronoun, replacing rather than doubling, exactly as Chapter 12 predicts." />

Faced with a new sentence, the questions to ask are the same ones from Chapter 12 first — bare verb, or verb-plus-infinitive/gerund, or command? — and then one more, specific to indirect objects: is there a noun or *a*-phrase this pronoun could be doubling? If the noun comes before the verb, doubling isn't optional. If it comes after, doubling is what a native speaker will do by default, even though it isn't always strictly required. Hold onto that instinct — it's exactly what makes [Chapter 14](../lesson-14)'s combined pronouns (*te las, me las, se las*) feel natural instead of arbitrary once a direct object pronoun joins *le* on the same verb.
