Subordinating conjunctions build one complex sentence out of two unequal parts: a main clause that can stand alone, and a dependent clause that can't — it only makes sense attached to the main clause it explains, qualifies, or sets the conditions for. That's the structural line separating this chapter from its two neighbors. [Chapter 51](../lesson-51) covered coordinating conjunctions, which join two *equal* elements; this chapter covers conjunctions that create dependency instead. And unlike [Chapter 56](../lesson-56)'s discourse connectors — words like *sin embargo* or *por lo tanto* that link two separate sentences together in a paragraph — the conjunctions here fuse everything into a single sentence with one main clause and one subordinate clause hanging off it. [Chapter 38](../lesson-38) already introduced the core idea that some of these conjunctions force the subjunctive mood in the clause they introduce, and previewed two specific systems — *aunque* and the *cuando* family — without fully resolving either one. This chapter is where both of those promises get paid off, alongside the causal and purpose conjunctions that round out the system.

## Core Pattern: Causal Conjunctions — Porque, Como, Ya Que, Puesto Que

### Porque: the default word for "because"
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Porque* is the ordinary, everyday way to attach a reason to a statement, and — unlike every other conjunction in this chapter — it never affects the mood of the verb that follows: a reason is being stated as fact, so the indicative is automatic.

<ExampleSentence es="No fui a la fiesta porque no tenía dinero." en="I didn't go to the party because I didn't have money." />
<ExampleSentence es="Estudio español porque quiero viajar a España." en="I'm studying Spanish because I want to travel to Spain." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Keep *porque* (one word, causal conjunction) apart from three look-alikes: *por qué* (two words, the interrogative "why," [Chapter 18](../lesson-18)), *el porqué* (one word, a noun meaning "the reason"), and *por que* (rare, a preposition *por* plus the relative *que*). All four are pronounced almost identically and are commonly confused even by native writers: *No sé por qué lo hizo* ("I don't know why he did it") vs. *No entiendo el porqué de su decisión* ("I don't understand the reason for his decision").
</GrammarNote>

### Como: the same reason, stated first
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Como* also means "because," but it comes with a real word-order restriction: it can only open the sentence, stating the reason before the result it caused. *Porque* works the opposite way — it follows the result clause and virtually never leads a multi-clause sentence (its one exception is answering a direct *¿por qué?* question on its own: *—¿Por qué no viniste? —Porque estaba enfermo.*).

<ExampleSentence es="Como no tenía dinero, no fui a la fiesta." en="Since I didn't have money, I didn't go to the party." note="Same underlying fact as the porque example above, reversed in order — como always leads." />
<ExampleSentence es="Como llovía tanto, decidimos quedarnos en casa." en="Since it was raining so hard, we decided to stay home." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Watch out for two other jobs *como* does elsewhere in Spanish: as "like/as" in a comparison (*nada como el café por la mañana*, "nothing like coffee in the morning") and as the *yo*-form of the verb *comer* (*yo como*, "I eat"). Context — especially sentence-initial position followed by a full clause and a comma — makes the causal use unambiguous.
</GrammarNote>

### Ya que and puesto que: the same logic, a more formal register
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Ya que* and *puesto que* both mean "since/given that" and work like *como* or *porque* — indicative, no mood choice involved — but they carry a noticeably more formal, written register, common in essays, reports, and careful spoken register, less common in casual conversation. Both can lead or follow the main clause, unlike *como* and *porque*.

<ExampleSentence es="Ya que insistes, te acompaño." en="Since you insist, I'll go with you." />
<ExampleSentence es="Puesto que no hay más preguntas, damos por terminada la reunión." en="Since there are no further questions, we consider the meeting concluded." note="Puesto que is the most formal of the four causal conjunctions here — natural in a business or academic register, stiff in casual conversation." />

## Building Out: Para Que and A Fin de Que — Purpose Clauses

### Purpose is never a settled fact
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Para que* ("so that") introduces the purpose behind the main clause's action — and a purpose, by definition, hasn't happened yet at the moment it's stated; it's the goal, not a completed reality. That's why *para que* takes the subjunctive every single time, with no known-fact exception the way [Chapter 38](../lesson-38)'s other triggers sometimes have one:

<ExampleSentence es="Te lo explico para que entiendas." en="I'm explaining it to you so that you understand." note="Entiendas is subjunctive — understanding is the goal of the explanation, not yet an accomplished fact at the moment of speaking." />
<ExampleSentence es="Te doy la llave para que entres tú mismo." en="I'm giving you the key so that you can let yourself in." />

### A fin de que: the formal synonym
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*A fin de que* means exactly the same thing as *para que* and follows the identical subjunctive rule — it simply belongs to the same formal, written register as *ya que* and *puesto que* above.

<ExampleSentence es="El gobierno implementó la ley a fin de que todos los ciudadanos tuvieran acceso a la sanidad." en="The government implemented the law so that all citizens would have access to healthcare." note="Tuvieran is imperfect subjunctive — the main verb implementó is in the past, so the purpose clause's subjunctive shifts to match, the same sequence-of-tense logic Chapter 39 covers in full." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
When the subject doesn't change between the two clauses, drop *que* entirely and use a plain infinitive instead — the identical same-subject rule [Chapter 38](../lesson-38) and [Chapter 42](../lesson-42) already established for noun clauses, extended here to purpose clauses. *Estudio para aprender* ("I study to learn," one subject, infinitive) vs. *Estudio para que aprendas* ("I study so that you learn," two subjects, subjunctive).
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Don't confuse *para que* (two words plus subjunctive, "so that") with *porque* (one word plus indicative, "because") — they're spelled and pronounced similarly enough to blur together, but they express opposite logical relationships: *porque* looks backward to a cause, *para que* looks forward to a goal.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: Aunque and the Mood-Choice System

### Aunque: known fact vs. hypothetical — the chapter's central decision
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

[Chapter 38](../lesson-38) flagged this exact contrast forward without resolving it: *aunque* ("although/even if") works on the same known-fact-vs-hypothetical logic as every WEIRDO subjunctive trigger, just applied to a concession instead of a wish or a doubt. The mood you choose changes what the sentence actually claims:

<ExampleSentence es="Aunque llueve, salgo." en="Although it's actually raining, I'm going out." note="Indicative (llueve) — the speaker is asserting, as a known fact, that it is currently raining, and going out anyway." />
<ExampleSentence es="Aunque llueva, salgo." en="Even if it rains, I'm going out." note="Subjunctive (llueva) — the speaker isn't claiming it will rain, only conceding that rain wouldn't change the plan. The rain is a dismissed possibility, not an asserted fact." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A reliable test: try translating with "although, and it's actually true that..." If that paraphrase fits, use the indicative. If the better paraphrase is "even if, whether or not it turns out to be true," use the subjunctive. Nothing about the *rest* of the sentence changes between the two versions above — only the speaker's commitment to the rain being real does.
</GrammarNote>

The same logic carries into the past, where the subjunctive shifts to the imperfect subjunctive ([Chapter 39](../lesson-39)) for a hypothetical or unrealized concession:

<ExampleSentence es="Aunque no tenía mucho dinero, viajó por Europa." en="Although he didn't have much money, he traveled around Europe." note="Indicative (tenía) — the lack of money is presented as a known fact about the past." />
<ExampleSentence es="Aunque no tuviera mucho dinero, viajaría por Europa." en="Even if he didn't have much money, he would travel around Europe." note="Imperfect subjunctive (tuviera) paired with the conditional (viajaría) — a hypothetical scenario, not an assertion about his actual finances. Chapter 41 covers this pairing in full." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
*A pesar de que* ("despite the fact that") often works as a near-synonym of concessive *aunque*, though it leans slightly more formal. Its close relative *a pesar de* (without *que*, plus a noun or infinitive rather than a full clause) is covered in [Chapter 50](../lesson-50) — the *que* version here is the one that behaves like a subordinating conjunction with its own mood choice.
</GrammarNote>

### Cuando, en cuanto, tan pronto como: the full reference for future time clauses
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

This is [Chapter 38](../lesson-38)'s other unresolved preview. Time conjunctions like *cuando* ("when"), *en cuanto* ("as soon as"), and *tan pronto como* ("as soon as") follow the exact same known-fact-vs-not-yet-real logic as *aunque*, just measured against time instead of truth. A habitual or already-real event stays indicative; an event that hasn't happened yet from the speaker's vantage point — almost always a future event — takes the subjunctive:

<ExampleSentence es="Cuando llego a casa, ceno." en="When I get home, I eat dinner." note="Indicative (llego) — a routine, habitually true fact, not tied to any one specific future moment." />
<ExampleSentence es="Cuando llegue, te llamo." en="When I get there, I'll call you." note="Subjunctive (llegue) — a specific future arrival that hasn't happened yet. The main clause can sit in the present (colloquial future, Chapter 22) or the true future tense (te llamaré) without changing the subordinate clause's mood." />

The identical pattern extends to genuinely past, habitual time as well — *cuando* is about known-vs-unreal, not strictly present-vs-future:

<ExampleSentence es="Cuando era niño, jugaba al fútbol todos los días." en="When I was a child, I used to play soccer every day." note="Indicative (era, imperfect) — a settled fact about repeated past reality, exactly the same 'known, not hypothetical' logic as the habitual present example above, just one tense further back." />

*En cuanto* and *tan pronto como* both mean "as soon as" and split the same way:

<ExampleSentence es="En cuanto llego a casa, me quito los zapatos." en="As soon as I get home, I take off my shoes." note="Habitual, indicative — the same everyday-routine logic as cuando llego a casa above." />
<ExampleSentence es="En cuanto llegue el tren, subimos." en="As soon as the train arrives, we'll get on." note="Subjunctive — the train's arrival is a specific future event, not yet real." />
<ExampleSentence es="Te avisaré tan pronto como termine el examen." en="I'll let you know as soon as I finish the exam." note="Subjunctive — termine describes a future completion, not a settled fact yet." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is the same mechanism [Chapter 38](../lesson-38) named at the noun-clause level — *not asserted as plain, settled reality* — just relocated to a time expression instead of a wish or an opinion. It generalizes beyond the three conjunctions drilled here: other time conjunctions you'll encounter elsewhere, like *mientras* ("while") and *hasta que* ("until"), follow the identical future-time-triggers-subjunctive rule.
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Don't confuse this future-time subjunctive with *si*-clause hypotheticals ([Chapter 41](../lesson-41)) — *cuando* concedes that the event **will** eventually happen, just not yet; *si* genuinely leaves open whether it happens at all. *Cuando llegue* assumes arrival is coming; *si llega* does not.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together: One Decision Tree for Adverbial Mood Choice

Every conjunction in this chapter falls into one of three buckets, and knowing which bucket you're in tells you the mood before you've even reached the verb:

1. **Always indicative — a reason is being stated as settled fact.** *Porque, como, ya que, puesto que.* The only choice left is word order and register: *como* leads, *porque* follows, and *ya que/puesto que* skew formal.
2. **Always subjunctive — a purpose is inherently not-yet-real.** *Para que, a fin de que.* There's no known-fact escape hatch here the way there is for *aunque* or *cuando* — a goal can never be a settled fact by definition.
3. **Depends on known-fact vs. not-yet-real.** *Aunque* (known concession vs. dismissed possibility) and the *cuando* family (habitual/past reality vs. future not-yet-happened) both run on the identical test: is the subordinate clause's content something the speaker is asserting as true, or is it being held at arm's length as merely possible, future, or hypothetical?

<ExampleSentence es="Como no tenía coche, siempre caminaba al trabajo, aunque hacía frío; ahora, en cuanto compre uno, iré en coche para que no llegue tarde." en="Since I didn't have a car, I always walked to work, even though it was cold; now, as soon as I buy one, I'll drive so that I'm not late." note="Stacks all three buckets in one passage: como (indicative reason), aunque (indicative, known past fact), en cuanto (subjunctive, future not-yet-real), and para que (subjunctive, purpose)." />
