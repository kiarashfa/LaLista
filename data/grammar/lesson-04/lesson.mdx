Spanish adjectives do two things English adjectives don't: they change form to match the noun they describe, and — for many adjectives — where they sit relative to the noun is itself a meaningful choice, not just a fixed word-order rule. An adjective's form tells you it agrees with a specific noun; its position can tell you whether the speaker means something classifying and objective or something more subjective and expected. This chapter covers agreement first, since it's the foundation every adjective needs, then works through where adjectives go and what moving them changes.

## Core Pattern: Gender and Number Agreement

### Basic agreement
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Adjectives change form to match the gender and number of the noun they describe — the same -o/-a and -s/-es patterns from [Chapter 1](../lesson-01) and [Chapter 2](../lesson-02), now applied to a second word class:

- **-o/-a adjectives** change for both gender and number: *rojo, roja, rojos, rojas.*
- **Adjectives ending in -e or a consonant** (other than a few nationality adjectives, below) don't change for gender, only number: *un coche grande, una casa grande, unos coches grandes.*
- **Nationality adjectives ending in a consonant** add -a for the feminine, unlike other consonant-ending adjectives: *español → española, francés → francesa, alemán → alemana.*

<ExampleSentence es="El coche rojo y la bicicleta roja están en el garaje." en="The red car and the red bicycle are in the garage." note="rojo/roja agree with coche (masc.) and bicicleta (fem.)." />
<ExampleSentence es="Tengo un amigo francés y una amiga francesa." en="I have a French friend (m.) and a French friend (f.)." note="francés loses its accent in the feminine (francesa) — the stress is now on a different, regularly-stressed syllable." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
An adjective describing two or more nouns of mixed gender defaults to masculine plural: *el hijo y la hija son listos* — *listos*, not *listas*, even though *hija* is feminine.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Where Adjectives Go

### The default: after the noun
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Most Spanish adjectives — especially anything classifying, distinguishing, or describing an objective, verifiable quality (color, shape, nationality, material, type) — follow the noun by default:

<ExampleSentence es="Quiero una camisa azul de algodón." en="I want a blue cotton shirt." note="Both azul and de algodón follow camisa, the normal Spanish order." />

This is the reverse of English's default adjective-before-noun order, and it's the single biggest word-order adjustment most English-speaking learners have to make.

### Adjectives that typically come before the noun
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

A smaller set of adjective-like words routinely precede the noun: quantifiers and limiting words (*mucho, poco, otro, cada, varios*), ordinal numbers (*primer, segundo, tercer*), and possessives (*mi, tu, su* — full treatment in [Chapter 7](../lesson-07)). These aren't classifying a noun against alternatives in its category so much as quantifying or situating it before you even get to the noun itself:

<ExampleSentence es="Tengo otra idea, pero necesito más tiempo." en="I have another idea, but I need more time." />

### Apocope: shortened forms before masculine singular nouns
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A handful of common adjectives drop their final **-o** when they come immediately before a **masculine singular** noun — a purely phonetic shortening called apocope:

*bueno → buen, malo → mal, primero → primer, tercero → tercer, uno → un, alguno → algún, ninguno → ningún.*

<ExampleSentence es="Es un buen libro, pero el primer capítulo es aburrido." en="It's a good book, but the first chapter is boring." note="buen and primer — both shortened because libro and capítulo are masculine singular and immediately follow." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The full form returns anywhere else: feminine (*buena idea*), plural (*buenos libros*), or when the adjective follows the noun instead (*un libro bueno* is grammatical, if less common than *un buen libro*).
</GrammarNote>

*Grande* is the odd one out: it shortens to **gran** before *any* singular noun, masculine or feminine — not just masculine — and only in the singular:

<ExampleSentence es="Es una gran mujer." en="She's a great woman." note="gran before a feminine singular noun — grande is the only apocope adjective that shortens for both genders." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Santo* shortens to *San* before most masculine saints' names (*San Pedro, San José*), except names starting with *To-* or *Do-* (*Santo Tomás, Santo Domingo*) — a small, separate apocope pattern worth recognizing even though it isn't a descriptive adjective in the same sense as the others.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Position that changes meaning
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

For a specific set of adjectives, moving from before to after the noun doesn't just shift emphasis — it changes what the sentence actually means. Before the noun tends toward a subjective, figurative, or already-assumed quality; after the noun tends toward the literal, objective, distinguishing quality:

| Adjective | Before the noun | After the noun |
|---|---|---|
| grande/gran | *un gran hombre* — a great man (admirable) | *un hombre grande* — a big/large man (size) |
| viejo | *un viejo amigo* — a longtime friend (duration) | *un amigo viejo* — an elderly friend (age) |
| pobre | *el pobre hombre* — the poor guy (pitiable) | *el hombre pobre* — the man who has no money |
| nuevo | *un nuevo coche* — a different/new-to-me car | *un coche nuevo* — a brand-new car |
| único | *el único libro* — the only book (there's just one) | *un libro único* — a one-of-a-kind book (unique quality) |
| antiguo | *mi antiguo jefe* — my former boss | *un edificio antiguo* — an old/ancient building |

<ExampleSentence es="Mi antiguo jefe trabaja ahora en un edificio antiguo del centro." en="My former boss now works in an old building downtown." note="Same adjective, antiguo, twice in one sentence with two different meanings depending on position." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
English intuition doesn't help much here — English speakers default to putting every adjective before the noun, so there's no equivalent "which position" choice to transfer from. Each of these pairs has to be learned as its own small fact.
</GrammarNote>

### Stacking multiple adjectives
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

When more than one adjective describes the same noun, Spanish doesn't enforce a strict adjective-order chain the way English does (opinion-size-age-color-origin...). Two general tendencies instead:

- Two postnominal adjectives of similar weight are usually joined with *y*: *una casa grande y bonita* (a big, pretty house).
- When one adjective is more inherent/classifying and another is more incidental, the more classifying one sits closer to the noun: *una mesa pequeña de madera* (a small wooden table) — *de madera* (material, the most defining trait) stays adjacent, while *pequeña* can precede or follow depending on emphasis.

<ExampleSentence es="Vive en una pequeña casa blanca." en="She lives in a small white house." note="pequeña (subjective size judgment) before the noun, blanca (objective, classifying color) after — the two default positions from earlier in this chapter working together in one phrase." />

### Subjective vs. objective positioning: the deeper logic
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

Every pattern in this chapter — apocope, default position, and the meaning-changing pairs above — comes from one underlying principle: postnominal position is **restrictive**, distinguishing the noun from other members of its category (*el vino tinto* singles out red wine as a category, as opposed to white or rosé); prenominal position is **non-restrictive**, attributing a quality the speaker already takes as true or expected of that specific noun, closer to a poetic epithet than a classification (*la blanca nieve* — snow, described as white, not distinguished from some non-white snow).

This is why the meaning-changing pairs work the way they do: *un hombre grande* restricts "man" to the subset that's physically large, while *un gran hombre* doesn't restrict at all — it comments on a man already identified. Native speakers use this distinction fluidly and often unconsciously; recognizing it explicitly is what lets an advanced learner predict a natural-sounding position for an adjective they've never seen paired with a noun before, rather than memorizing pairs one at a time.

## Bringing It Together

Start every adjective from agreement — gender and number must always match the noun, with no exceptions. Then default to placing it after the noun, unless it's a quantifier, ordinal, or possessive (which default to before), or one of the small set of adjectives whose position itself carries meaning — in which case, before the noun leans subjective/assumed, after leans objective/restrictive.

<ExampleSentence es="Es un gran problema: mi antiguo profesor, un hombre viejo y muy tradicional, no acepta ningún cambio." en="It's a big problem: my former teacher, an old and very traditional man, won't accept any change." note="Combines apocope (gran, ningún), a meaning-changing pair used correctly (antiguo before the noun = 'former,' viejo after = literal age), and basic agreement (tradicional) with problema, one of Chapter 1's -ma masculine nouns." />
