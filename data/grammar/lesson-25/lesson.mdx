The present progressive is Spanish's tool for catching an action mid-motion — not "I read" or "I'm a reader," but "I am reading, right now, as I speak." English leans on this construction constantly, for far more than just the current moment ("I'm working at a bank," "I'm seeing someone"). Spanish builds the identical form — estar plus a gerund — but reaches for it far more narrowly, which means the real skill in this chapter isn't just building the form correctly; it's learning when a Spanish speaker would actually choose it over the plain present tense you already know from [Chapter 22](../lesson-22).

## Core Pattern: Estar + Gerund

### Forming the gerund: -ando / -iendo
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The gerund is a single invariant form built from the infinitive — it never changes for person or number. Drop the infinitive ending and add -ando for -ar verbs, -iendo for -er and -ir verbs:

<ExampleSentence es="trabajar → trabajando" en="to work → working" />
<ExampleSentence es="comer → comiendo" en="to eat → eating" />
<ExampleSentence es="vivir → viviendo" en="to live → living" />

<ExampleSentence es="Estoy trabajando." en="I'm working." />
<ExampleSentence es="Los niños están comiendo." en="The kids are eating." />

### Estar carries the conjugation; the gerund never changes
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

All of the person-and-number information sits on *estar*, not on the gerund — so building any progressive sentence is really just Chapter 21's estar paradigm with a fixed gerund attached at the end:

<BootTable
  label="estar + trabajando"
  caption="present progressive"
  forms={{
    "yo": "estoy trabajando",
    "tú": "estás trabajando",
    "él/ella/usted": "está trabajando",
    "nosotros": "estamos trabajando",
    "vosotros": "estáis trabajando",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "están trabajando"
  }}
/>

Any outline this table shows isn't new grammar — it's simply estar's own irregular pattern from [Chapter 21](../lesson-21) (the accent on estás/está/están, the irregular estoy) showing through. *Trabajando* itself is identical in all six cells; only estar does any conjugating at all.

## Building Out: Irregular Gerunds

### -IR stem-changing verbs: a lighter version of the change
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

-ir verbs that stem-change in the present tense ([Chapter 23](../lesson-23)) carry a related — but not identical — vowel change into the gerund. An o→ue or e→ie verb weakens to a single o→u or e→i shift instead:

| Infinitive | Present-tense change (Ch. 23) | Gerund |
|---|---|---|
| pedir (e→i) | pido | pidiendo |
| dormir (o→ue) | duermo | durmiendo |
| decir (e→i) | digo | diciendo |
| sentir (e→ie) | siento | sintiendo |
| morir (o→ue) | muero | muriendo |
| seguir (e→i) | sigo | siguiendo |

<ExampleSentence es="Está pidiendo la cuenta." en="He/she is asking for the check." />
<ExampleSentence es="Los niños están durmiendo." en="The kids are sleeping." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This gerund-level weakening is restricted to -ir verbs, with one well-known exception: poder, an -er verb, still shows it (pudiendo). No -ar verb ever changes its gerund vowel — pensar simply gives pensando, never a hypothetical "piensando."
</GrammarNote>

### Y-insertion for vowel-stem verbs
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

When a verb's stem itself ends in a vowel, the expected -iendo would stack three vowels in a row (*le-iendo*), which Spanish spelling doesn't allow. The unstressed i turns into a y instead:

<ExampleSentence es="leer → leyendo" en="to read → reading" />
<ExampleSentence es="traer → trayendo" en="to bring → bringing" />
<ExampleSentence es="oír → oyendo" en="to hear → hearing" />
<ExampleSentence es="construir → construyendo" en="to build → building" />
<ExampleSentence es="ir → yendo" en="to go → going" note="Ir's gerund is genuinely rare in everyday speech — Spanish usually avoids estar + yendo entirely (see below)." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Pronoun attachment and the written-accent rule
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

[Chapter 12](../lesson-12) flagged this rule without fully explaining it — here's the complete mechanism. Object pronouns with a progressive follow the same two-position choice as with any infinitive: attach to the end of the gerund, or move in front of the whole *estar* phrase. Both are equally correct:

<ExampleSentence es="Estoy leyéndolo. / Lo estoy leyendo." en="I'm reading it." note="Both positions mean exactly the same thing." />

The accent is not optional decoration. A gerund is always stressed on the syllable right before -ndo (le-YEN-do). Spanish's normal stress rules would put the default stress somewhere else once a pronoun syllable is added onto the end, so a written accent is required to keep the stress exactly where it already was:

<ExampleSentence es="Está haciéndolo ahora mismo." en="He/she is doing it right now." note="haciendo → haciéndolo — the accent locks the stress on the -ie- where it belongs." />
<ExampleSentence es="Estoy viéndola." en="I'm watching it/her." note="viendo → viéndola." />
<ExampleSentence es="Está diciéndoselo." en="He/she is telling it to him/her." note="Two pronouns attached at once (se + lo, the Chapter 14 combination) — same accent rule, regardless of how many pronouns are attached." />

### When progressive is required vs. when simple present is enough
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

This is the chapter's real contrastive lesson. English uses its "-ing" progressive for far more than the current instant — including habitual facts and general states ("I'm working at a bank" doesn't mean this exact second, it means "that's my job"). Spanish's progressive is narrower: it's reserved for an action genuinely underway, emphasized as in-progress, right now or around now — not for describing what's generally true.

<ExampleSentence es="Trabajo en un banco." en="I work at a bank." note="A general fact about your job — simple present, not progressive, even though English naturally says 'I'm working at a bank.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="Ahora mismo estoy trabajando en un informe." en="Right now I'm working on a report." note="Genuinely in-progress at this moment — progressive fits." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Building Estoy trabajando en un banco to mean "I have a job at a bank" is the single most common English-transfer error with this tense — it isn't ungrammatical, but it says something narrower and odder than intended: that you happen to be physically at the bank working this very moment. For a general state, reach for the plain present from Chapter 22.
</GrammarNote>

Spanish also avoids the progressive for near-future plans, where English often uses "-ing" ("I'm leaving tomorrow"). Spanish prefers *ir a* + infinitive ([Chapter 45](../lesson-45)) or the simple future ([Chapter 33](../lesson-33)) instead: *Salgo mañana* or *Voy a salir mañana*, not *Estoy saliendo mañana*.

### B2 extension: other progressive-family periphrases
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

A few other verbs pair with a gerund to build related, but distinct, meanings beyond plain in-progress action. Each gets its full treatment as a verbal periphrasis in [Chapter 45](../lesson-45); this is enough to recognize them:

<ExampleSentence es="Sigo estudiando español después de tantos años." en="I'm still studying Spanish after all these years." note="seguir + gerund = to keep on doing something." />
<ExampleSentence es="Anda diciendo que va a dejar el trabajo." en="He's going around saying he's going to quit his job." note="andar + gerund often carries a mildly gossipy or critical undertone English doesn't automatically supply." />
<ExampleSentence es="Llevo dos horas esperando el autobús." en="I've been waiting for the bus for two hours." note="llevar + gerund frames an action's ongoing duration — contrast with llevar used with just a time expression, which means something different." />

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Estoy leyendo un libro buenísimo — llevo tres días leyéndolo y no puedo dejarlo. Normalmente trabajo desde casa, así que ahora mismo estoy sentado en el sofá, disfrutándolo en paz." en="I'm reading a really good book — I've been reading it for three days and I can't put it down. I normally work from home, so right now I'm sitting on the couch, enjoying it in peace." note="estoy leyendo and disfrutándolo are genuinely in-progress; trabajo desde casa stays simple present because it's a general fact, not a right-now action." />

Before reaching for estar + gerund, ask: is this action genuinely happening right now, or being emphasized as temporarily in-progress — not a general fact, a habitual routine, or a near-future plan? If yes, build the gerund (watching for the -ir vowel weakening and y-insertion families above), conjugate estar, and attach or place any pronoun with its accent intact. If the sentence is really describing what's generally true, Chapter 22's plain present is very likely the more natural, more native-sounding choice.
