English's plain past tense — "I ate," "I knew," "it rained" — doesn't force you to say whether an action was a one-off event or an ongoing state, a single occurrence or a repeated habit. Spanish does. [Chapters 26](../lesson-26)–[27](../lesson-27) gave you the preterite, for actions viewed as complete and bounded; [Chapter 28](../lesson-28) gave you the imperfect, for actions viewed as ongoing, habitual, or simply the backdrop other things happen against. Both tenses translate as English's one simple past, which is exactly why choosing between them doesn't come naturally to an English speaker — there's no English signal to lean on. This chapter is entirely about that choice: not new conjugation to learn, but the judgment call itself, which turns out to be one of the genuinely hardest, most rewarding skills in Spanish past-tense grammar.

## Core Pattern: The Contrast, Restated

### Bounded and completed vs. ongoing and open-ended
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Here's the core idea from Chapters 26–28, placed side by side for the first time: the **preterite** treats an action as a complete, bounded whole — it started, it happened, it ended, and the sentence is reporting that whole unit. The **imperfect** treats an action as ongoing, open-ended, or habitual — no interest in when it started or whether it's finished, just a description of how things were.

<ExampleSentence es="Comí paella." en="I ate paella." note="Preterite — a single, complete event. It happened, and the sentence treats it as a whole, edges included." />
<ExampleSentence es="Comía paella los domingos." en="I used to eat paella on Sundays." note="Imperfect — a repeated habit, with no interest in when it started or stopped." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A quick test: if you can comfortably picture drawing a box around the action — it started, it happened, it ended — reach for the preterite. If the action is more like a stretch of background with no box around it — ongoing, repeated, or still unfolding at the moment you're describing — reach for the imperfect.
</GrammarNote>

### A shared example, two different pasts
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

<ExampleSentence es="Ayer llovió." en="It rained yesterday." note="Preterite — a completed weather event; it happened and it's over." />
<ExampleSentence es="Llovía cuando llegamos." en="It was raining when we arrived." note="Imperfect — the rain is the ongoing backdrop against which llegamos (preterite) happens." />

That second pair is already a preview of the rest of this chapter: the two tenses aren't just choosing between two isolated sentences, they're doing two different *jobs* whenever they show up together — and that's where the real decision logic lives.

## Building Out: Two Structural Frameworks for Choosing

The same bounded/open-ended distinction from above scales up once you're building more than one sentence at a time. Two structural patterns cover most of the everyday judgment calls: which tense carries a story forward, and which tense marks an interruption.

### Narrative backbone vs. background: the story and its scenery
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

In any stretch of narration, the preterite carries the sequence of events forward — what happened, then what happened next. The imperfect paints the surrounding scene: what things looked like, what was already true, what was going on while the events unfolded. Neither tense moves the story's timeline forward on its own; only the preterite does that job.

<ExampleSentence es="Era una noche fría de invierno. La calle estaba vacía y solo se oía el viento. De repente, un coche paró frente a la casa y un hombre bajó rápidamente." en="It was a cold winter night. The street was empty and only the wind could be heard. Suddenly, a car stopped in front of the house and a man got out quickly." note="Era, estaba, se oía — imperfect, painting the unmoving scene. Paró and bajó — preterite, the two events that actually move the story forward." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A useful test: if you removed a sentence and the sequence of events would still make sense without it, it's probably background (imperfect). If removing it would leave a gap in what actually happened next, it's backbone (preterite).
</GrammarNote>

### Interruption structure: one action cuts through another
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A specific, extremely common version of backbone-vs-background: an ongoing action already in progress (imperfect), interrupted by a sudden, punctual event (preterite).

<ExampleSentence es="Dormía cuando sonó el teléfono." en="I was sleeping when the phone rang." note="Dormía (imperfect) is the ongoing action already in progress; sonó (preterite) is the sudden event that interrupts it." />
<ExampleSentence es="Leía el periódico cuando empezó a llover." en="I was reading the newspaper when it started to rain." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
English marks this with two different verb forms ("was sleeping" vs. "rang"), which usually makes the choice feel intuitive once you notice the parallel — was/were + -ing lines up with the imperfect, and the plain past lines up with the preterite here. Don't lean on that shortcut too hard, though: English "-ing" maps onto Spanish's own progressive far more narrowly than the imperfect does ([Chapter 25](../lesson-25)), so this works well for the interruption pattern specifically, without being a general rule for choosing between the two tenses everywhere else.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

The single richest — and hardest — piece of this whole chapter is a small set of verbs whose English translation genuinely changes depending on which past tense you pick. These aren't stylistic shades of difference; the preterite and imperfect versions describe two different things, and mixing them up says something you didn't mean to say.

### Conocer: conocí vs. conocía
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

*Conocer* means "to know" in the sense of being familiar with a person, place, or thing — a state. The imperfect describes that state as already standing; the preterite marks the specific moment the state began, which is exactly what "meeting someone for the first time" is.

<ExampleSentence es="Conocía a Marta desde hace años." en="I had known Marta for years." note="Imperfect — an ongoing state of familiarity, already in place." />
<ExampleSentence es="Conocí a Marta en una fiesta el año pasado." en="I met Marta at a party last year." note="Preterite — the state of knowing her didn't exist before this moment; the preterite marks the point it began." />

### Saber: supe vs. sabía
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

*Saber* works the same way for facts and information instead of people: the imperfect names an existing state of knowledge, and the preterite marks the moment that knowledge arrived — "found out," not "knew."

<ExampleSentence es="Sabía que Juan vivía en Madrid." en="I knew that Juan lived in Madrid." note="Imperfect — an ongoing state of knowledge." />
<ExampleSentence es="Supe que Juan vivía en Madrid cuando me lo dijo mi hermana." en="I found out that Juan lived in Madrid when my sister told me." note="Preterite — the exact moment the knowledge began, not the state of having it." />

### Poder: pude vs. podía
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

*Poder* splits ability itself apart from the outcome of using it. The imperfect *podía* names a general capacity or circumstance — true over a stretch of time, with no claim about whether anything came of it. The preterite *pude* bundles the outcome into the meaning: not just "was able to," but "actually did it, successfully, on this specific occasion."

<ExampleSentence es="Podía levantar cien kilos cuando era joven." en="I was able to lift a hundred kilos when I was young." note="Imperfect — a general ability, true over a stretch of time, independent of any one attempt." />
<ExampleSentence es="Por fin pude abrir la puerta después de varios intentos." en="I finally managed to open the door after several tries." note="Preterite — a single specific attempt that actually succeeded; the outcome is built into the meaning." />
<ExampleSentence es="No pude terminar el examen a tiempo." en="I couldn't (didn't manage to) finish the exam in time." note="Negated preterite implies a specific attempt that failed — not just a general, ongoing lack of ability, which no podía would express instead." />

### Querer: quise vs. quería (and no quise)
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

*Querer* in the imperfect names an ongoing desire, with no claim about whether it led anywhere. In the preterite, that desire gets bundled with a specific moment of action — which is why *quise* often translates better as "tried to" or "decided to" than as plain "wanted."

<ExampleSentence es="Quería ir a la fiesta, pero tenía que trabajar." en="I wanted to go to the party, but I had to work." note="Imperfect — an ongoing desire, with no claim about whether it led to action." />
<ExampleSentence es="Quise llamarte anoche, pero se me olvidó." en="I tried to call you last night, but I forgot." note="Preterite frames the desire as something that triggered a specific attempt at one moment — quise often translates better as 'tried to' or 'decided to' than plain 'wanted.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="No quise contestar esa pregunta." en="I refused to answer that question." note="No quise (negated preterite) has become a fixed, idiomatic meaning — a deliberate refusal in the moment — distinct from no quería, which would just mean an ongoing lack of desire." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Notice the pattern underneath all four verbs: the preterite version always turns a mental state — knowing, being able, wanting — into a bounded event: the moment it started, the moment it succeeded, the moment a decision got made. The imperfect version just describes the state as it already stood, without marking when it began or whether anything came of it. Once you see that shared logic, the four pairs stop being four separate lists to memorize and become one idea applied four times.
</GrammarNote>

### A common learner trap: English's single past tense hides the choice
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Because English happily uses one past-tense form for both jobs, the most common error isn't picking the "wrong" tense out of confusion about meaning — it's defaulting to the preterite everywhere, since that's usually the first past tense English speakers memorize forms for, and letting background description flatten into a string of reported events.

<ExampleSentence es="Cuando llegué a casa, llovía mucho y hacía frío." en="When I got home, it was raining hard and it was cold." note="Llegué (preterite) is the backbone event; llovía and hacía (imperfect) are the background weather. A learner who defaults to preterite for all three (llovió, hizo frío) isn't ungrammatical, but flattens the scene-setting the imperfect is built for." />

## Bringing It Together

Faced with a past-tense sentence, run through this in order: **Is this a single completed event, part of a sequence of things that happened?** Reach for the preterite. **Is this describing how things were, an ongoing or habitual state, or the backdrop another action interrupts?** Reach for the imperfect. **Is it conocer, saber, poder, or querer?** Double-check which specific meaning you intend — the aspect shift changes the translation, not just the flavor.

<ExampleSentence es="Eran las diez de la noche y llovía sin parar. Yo leía tranquilamente en el sofá cuando de repente alguien llamó a la puerta. No sabía quién podía ser a esas horas, así que me levanté despacio. Cuando abrí la puerta, no pude creer lo que vi: ¡era mi hermano, a quien no veía desde hacía cinco años!" en="It was ten at night and it was raining nonstop. I was reading peacefully on the couch when suddenly someone knocked on the door. I didn't know who it could be at that hour, so I got up slowly. When I opened the door, I couldn't believe what I saw: it was my brother, who I hadn't seen in five years!" note="Eran, llovía, leía, sabía, podía, veía set the scene (imperfect); llamó, me levanté, abrí, pude, vi advance the story (preterite) — including pude, whose preterite here specifically means 'managed to' rather than a general ability." />
