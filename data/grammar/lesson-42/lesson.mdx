The infinitive is the form of a Spanish verb you meet before any other — it's the shape printed in the dictionary, the -ar/-er/-ir citation form every conjugation chart starts from. But in Spanish, the infinitive is far more than a lookup form you conjugate your way out of: it does real grammatical work on its own, standing in for an entire idea the way a noun does, gluing onto prepositions, and chaining onto other verbs with no conjugation at all. This chapter is the infinitive's complete job description — where it goes, what follows it, and the one everyday choice (infinitive, or *que* plus subjunctive?) that trips up more English speakers than almost anything else in Spanish grammar.

## Core Pattern: The Infinitive as a Verbal Noun

### Fumar es malo: the infinitive standing in for a noun
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

In English, when you want to talk about an activity itself rather than someone doing it in the moment, you reach for "-ing": "Smoking is bad for you." "I love traveling." Spanish has the identical job to fill, but hands it to a different form entirely — not the gerund (that restriction is [Chapter 43's](../lesson-43) whole subject, and it's exactly why this chapter matters as much as it does), but the plain infinitive. Whenever a verb's action itself is the subject or object of a sentence — the whole activity treated as a single "thing" — Spanish reaches for the infinitive, unconjugated and unchanged.

<ExampleSentence es="Fumar es malo para la salud." en="Smoking is bad for your health." note="Fumar, not fumando — this is Spanish's real equivalent of English's '-ing'-as-subject." />
<ExampleSentence es="Nos encanta viajar." en="We love traveling." note="viajar is the object of encanta, functioning just like un libro would — a whole activity treated as one noun-like thing." />
<ExampleSentence es="Ver es creer." en="Seeing is believing." note="Two bare infinitives working as subject and predicate at once — a tidy proverb built entirely on this pattern." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A bare infinitive-as-subject can optionally take the definite article *el* for extra weight or a more formal tone — *el fumar es un hábito difícil de dejar* ("smoking is a habit that's hard to quit") — but the article is optional, and the plain infinitive alone is by far the more common everyday choice.
</GrammarNote>

Hold onto this pattern: it's the direct answer to a question English speakers instinctively ask — where's Spanish's "-ing"-as-subject? Right here, on the infinitive. [Chapter 43](../lesson-43) explains why the gerund, despite functioning as English's "-ing" in other ways, is never allowed to do this particular job.

## Building Out: Where the Infinitive Attaches

{/* This chapter is shaped as a catalog of the infinitive's attachment points rather than a single core pattern that extends outward — so "Building Out" here walks through several genuinely separate attachment contexts (prepositions, direct verb complements, linking prepositions, the perfect form) instead of one pattern branching into sub-variations. */}

The infinitive-as-noun pattern above is really just one corner of a much bigger picture: Spanish infinitives attach constantly onto prepositions and onto other conjugated verbs, sometimes directly and sometimes through a linking preposition. All of it is governed by one shared fact — the second verb never conjugates.

### After any preposition, the infinitive is the only option
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Any time a preposition in Spanish is followed by a verb, that verb has to be an infinitive — no exceptions. This is a genuine trap for English speakers, because English usually reaches for "-ing" right after a preposition ("before leaving," "without saying a word," "for understanding grammar") — a pattern Spanish never allows.

<ExampleSentence es="Antes de salir, cerré la puerta con llave." en="Before leaving, I locked the door." note="English: 'before leaving' (-ing). Spanish: antes de salir — always the infinitive after a preposition." />
<ExampleSentence es="Sin decir nada, se marchó." en="Without saying anything, he left." />
<ExampleSentence es="Estudio español para entender mejor a mi familia." en="I study Spanish to understand my family better." note="para + infinitive expresses purpose — 'in order to.'" />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Antes de saliendo* or *sin diciendo nada* are always wrong, no matter how natural the "-ing" shape feels in English — a Spanish preposition is grammatically incapable of taking a gerund. [Chapter 43](../lesson-43) revisits this same restriction from the gerund's side.
</GrammarNote>

### Straight after a conjugated verb: no preposition needed
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

A large family of common verbs — mostly verbs of wanting, ability, obligation, and opinion — attach directly to a following infinitive with no linking word at all.

<ExampleSentence es="Quiero comer algo antes de salir." en="I want to eat something before leaving." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Sabes nadar?" en="Do you know how to swim?" note="saber + infinitive means 'to know how to' — a distinct meaning from poder, which is about ability or permission in the moment." />
<ExampleSentence es="Debemos terminar el proyecto hoy." en="We have to finish the project today." />

Common verbs in this family include *querer, poder, deber, necesitar, saber, soler, pensar* (to intend), *preferir, esperar, lograr, conseguir, intentar,* and *decidir*.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Intentar* and *tratar de* both translate as "to try to" — but only *tratar de* takes the preposition *de*. *Intentar entenderlo* and *tratar de entenderlo* mean the same thing; *intentar de entenderlo* is never correct.
</GrammarNote>

### Al + infinitive: a fixed formula for "upon doing something"
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

One preposition-plus-infinitive combination is common and specific enough to earn its own mention. *Al* + infinitive describes one action happening right as, or right before, another one — "upon doing," "as soon as," "when." It's a compact stand-in for a full *cuando* clause, usable with any verb.

<ExampleSentence es="Al llegar a casa, se quitó los zapatos." en="Upon arriving home, he took off his shoes." note="A tidy alternative to Cuando llegó a casa, se quitó los zapatos — same meaning, more compact." />
<ExampleSentence es="Al oír la noticia, se puso a llorar." en="Upon hearing the news, she started to cry." />

### A genuinely large list: which preposition goes with which verb
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Beyond *al*, a long list of common verbs pair with a following infinitive through one specific linking preposition — *a/de/en/con/por/para* — and the preposition itself has to be learned verb by verb; there's no shortcut rule that predicts which one a given verb takes. What follows isn't exhaustive, but it covers the verbs you'll run into constantly. (A future chapter on core prepositions will eventually index preposition-taking verbs more broadly; this list is scoped specifically to the ones that link to an infinitive.)

- **a** — *empezar a, comenzar a* (to start doing), *aprender a* (to learn to), *enseñar a* (to teach someone to), *ayudar a* (to help to), *atreverse a* (to dare to), *decidirse a* (to make up one's mind to), *animarse a* (to work up the enthusiasm to), *invitar a* (to invite someone to), *volver a* (to do again — full treatment in [Chapter 45](../lesson-45)), *ir a* (near-future "going to" — full treatment in [Chapter 45](../lesson-45))
- **de** — *dejar de* (to stop doing), *tratar de* (to try to), *terminar de* (to finish doing), *acabar de* (to have just done — full treatment in [Chapter 45](../lesson-45)), *olvidarse de* (to forget to), *acordarse de* (to remember to), *alegrarse de* (to be glad to), *tener ganas de* (to feel like doing), *encargarse de* (to take charge of)
- **en** — *pensar en* (to think about doing), *quedar en* (to agree to), *insistir en* (to insist on), *tardar en* (to take time to), *consistir en* (to consist of/in)
- **con** — *soñar con* (to dream of), *contar con* (to count on), *amenazar con* (to threaten to)
- **por** — *luchar por* (to fight to), *esforzarse por* (to make an effort to), *preocuparse por* (to worry about), *optar por* (to opt to)
- **para** — *prepararse para* (to prepare to), *servir para* (to be used for)

<ExampleSentence es="Empezó a trabajar aquí el mes pasado." en="He started working here last month." />
<ExampleSentence es="Dejé de fumar hace un año." en="I quit smoking a year ago." note="dejar de + infinitive means 'to stop doing' — don't confuse it with dejar + infinitive without de, which means 'to let/allow' (see the exception verbs below)." />
<ExampleSentence es="Trato de entender la gramática, pero a veces es difícil." en="I'm trying to understand the grammar, but sometimes it's hard." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Soñáis con viajar a Sudamérica algún día?" en="Do you all dream of traveling to South America someday?" note="Castellano vosotros form — soñáis, from soñar con + infinitive." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Pensar* + infinitive directly (no preposition) means "to intend/plan to" — *pienso ir mañana* ("I plan to go tomorrow"). *Pensar en* + infinitive means something narrower: "to think about (doing)," without necessarily planning it — *pienso en ir* ("I'm thinking about going"). The preposition changes the meaning, not just the register.
</GrammarNote>

### The perfect infinitive: haber + participle
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

The infinitive has a compound form too, built exactly like every other *haber*-based compound tense in this book: the invariant infinitive *haber* plus a past participle. [Chapter 30](../lesson-30) has the full irregular-participle list — nothing here is different from those same participles, just glued onto *haber's* infinitive instead of a conjugated form.

<ExampleSentence es="Después de haber comido, salimos a caminar." en="After having eaten, we went out for a walk." />
<ExampleSentence es="Siento haber llegado tarde a la reunión." en="I'm sorry for arriving late to the meeting." note="Literally 'sorry to have arrived late' — the perfect infinitive marks that the lateness happened before the apology, not at the same time as it." />
<ExampleSentence es="Gracias por haberme ayudado." en="Thanks for helping me." note="por + perfect infinitive — thanking someone for something already completed." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
In formal or literary Spanish, *de* + perfect infinitive can substitute for a full *si* + pluperfect subjunctive hypothetical: *De haberlo sabido, te habría avisado* means exactly the same thing as *Si lo hubiera sabido, te habría avisado* ("Had I known, I would have told you"). [Chapter 41](../lesson-41) has the full si-clause system this shortcut stands in for.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Infinitive vs. subjunctive: the same-subject rule, in full
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

[Chapter 38](../lesson-38) introduced this rule from the subjunctive's side: *que* plus subjunctive only appears once a subordinate clause has picked up a subject of its own, different from the main clause's subject. Seen from the infinitive's side, it's the same coin's other face — whenever the person doing the wanting, hoping, or needing is the *same* person doing the second action, Spanish drops *que* and the subjunctive entirely and reaches for the plain infinitive instead. For the ordinary WEIRDO-trigger verbs, this holds with no exceptions:

<ExampleSentence es="Quiero comer." en="I want to eat." note="One subject (yo) — infinitive, no que, no subjunctive." />
<ExampleSentence es="Quiero que comas." en="I want you to eat." note="Two subjects (yo wants, tú eats) — que plus subjunctive required." />
<ExampleSentence es="Espero llegar a tiempo." en="I hope to arrive on time." note="Same subject throughout — infinitive." />
<ExampleSentence es="Espero que llegues a tiempo." en="I hope you arrive on time." note="Different subject — que plus subjunctive." />

The same logic governs impersonal expressions (*es necesario, es importante, es bueno...*): used as a general statement with no specific person in mind, they take a bare infinitive; the moment a specific subject enters the sentence, *que* plus subjunctive takes over.

<ExampleSentence es="Es importante estudiar." en="It's important to study." note="A general statement, true for anyone — infinitive." />
<ExampleSentence es="Es importante que estudies." en="It's important that you study." note="Now about one specific person — que plus subjunctive." />

### The exception: a few verbs allow either pattern
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

A small, specific class of verbs breaks the pattern above in a genuinely useful way: verbs of **permission and causation** — *dejar* (to let), *hacer* (to make/cause), *permitir* (to permit), *mandar* (to order), *prohibir* (to forbid), *invitar* (to invite) — allow the infinitive construction even when the second subject is a different person. That second person simply shows up as an object pronoun instead of as the subject of a *que*-clause, so both patterns are grammatical side by side:

<ExampleSentence es="Le dejé salir." en="I let him leave." note="Infinitive pattern — the causee (him) surfaces as the object pronoun le, not as a subject." />
<ExampleSentence es="Dejé que saliera." en="I let him leave." note="Que plus subjunctive pattern — exactly the same meaning, built the 'ordinary' way." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mis padres me permitieron viajar solo." en="My parents allowed me to travel alone." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mis padres permitieron que viajara solo." en="My parents allowed me to travel alone." note="Same meaning as above, que-clause version." />
<ExampleSentence es="Me hizo reír." en="He made me laugh." note="With hacer specifically, this infinitive pattern is overwhelmingly the natural everyday choice." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
The two patterns aren't perfectly interchangeable in feel: the infinitive version is more compact and conversational, fusing the causer and the caused action into a single event, while the *que* + subjunctive version reads as more explicit and formal, treating the caused action as its own separate clause. With *hacer* in particular, the *que*-clause version (*Hizo que me riera*) is grammatical but distinctly rarer and more literary than *Me hizo reír*.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Antes de salir esta mañana, decidí dejar de posponer las cosas: quiero terminar el informe hoy. Mi jefe prefiere que lo entregue mañana, pero le dije que prefiero acabarlo ya. Después de haberlo enviado, podré descansar." en="Before leaving this morning, I decided to stop putting things off: I want to finish the report today. My boss would rather I turn it in tomorrow, but I told him I'd rather finish it now. After having sent it, I'll be able to rest." note="Every pattern from this chapter in one passage: after a preposition (antes de salir), dejar de + inf, a direct complement (quiero terminar), the same-subject infinitive (prefiero acabarlo) against the different-subject subjunctive (prefiere que lo entregue), and the perfect infinitive (después de haberlo enviado)." />

Faced with a second verb, work through it in order: is there a preposition immediately before it? Infinitive, always. Is it glued straight onto a verb like *querer, poder,* or *deber*? Infinitive, no preposition needed. Does the subject change between the two verbs? If so, check whether the main verb belongs to the small *dejar/hacer/permitir/mandar/prohibir* family — if it doesn't, *que* plus subjunctive is required; if it does, either pattern works. And if the whole action happened before another point in time, *haber* plus participle turns any of the above into its perfect form.
