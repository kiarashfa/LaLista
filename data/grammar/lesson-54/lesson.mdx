English negates a sentence by adding a helper word — "don't," "doesn't," "isn't" — in front of the main verb. Spanish does something structurally simpler on the surface (just put *no* before the verb, no helper required) and something structurally stranger just underneath: once a sentence has one negative word in it, other negative words in the same clause don't cancel each other out the way two negatives do in careful English — they pile up and reinforce the negation instead. *No conozco a nadie* literally reads word-for-word as "not I-know to nobody," and in Spanish that's not sloppy, it's required. This chapter builds the whole negative-word vocabulary and then explains, as a real rule with real logic (not a list of exceptions to memorize), exactly when *no* shows up, when it doesn't, and why.

## Core Pattern: No — The Default Negator

### No directly before the verb
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

The single most useful fact about Spanish negation: to make any sentence negative, place *no* immediately in front of the conjugated verb. Nothing else changes — no helper verb, no shift in word order, no special form of *no* to agree with anything.

<ExampleSentence es="Fumo." en="I smoke." />
<ExampleSentence es="No fumo." en="I don't smoke." note="No shift in verb form — just no dropped in front of the verb that was already there." />
<ExampleSentence es="No hemos terminado todavía." en="We haven't finished yet." note="With a compound tense, no still goes before the whole verb phrase — before hemos, not between hemos and terminado." />

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "No", role: "connector" },
    { text: "fumo.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>

<GrammarNote type="tip">
English needs a helper verb to negate almost anything ("I don't smoke," not "I not smoke") because a bare English verb can't sit next to "not." Spanish has no such restriction — *no* attaches directly to any conjugated verb, which is one reason Spanish negation often *feels* simpler than English negation, right up until the double-negative system in the second half of this chapter.
</GrammarNote>

*No* also sits in front of any object pronouns attached to the verb, not just the verb itself — the pronouns and the verb move together as one unit, and *no* goes in front of that whole unit (full placement rules for these pronouns live in [Chapter 12](../lesson-12)):

<ExampleSentence es="No lo entiendo." en="I don't understand it." />
<ExampleSentence es="No me lo dijiste." en="You didn't tell me that." />

## Building Out: The Negative-Word Family

Once *no* alone feels automatic, the next step is a small set of words that name *what's* being negated — "never," "nothing," "no one," "not either," "not even." Each one can simply be added after the verb, in a sentence that still starts with *no* exactly as above. The twist — covered in full once this vocabulary is in place — is that most of these words can *also* move to the front of the sentence, and when they do, *no* disappears entirely. For now, focus on what each word means and sounds like in a sentence; the placement rule gets its own full treatment below.

### Nunca and jamás: never
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Nunca* is the everyday word for "never." *Jamás* means exactly the same thing — it is not a separate shade of meaning, just a stronger, more emphatic, more literary way of saying it, the way English speakers might reach for "never in my life" instead of plain "never" for extra force.

<ExampleSentence es="No voy al gimnasio nunca." en="I never go to the gym." />
<ExampleSentence es="Jamás olvidaré ese viaje." en="I will never forget that trip." note="Jamás up front, no dropped — the placement rule below explains exactly why." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
The two words can even stack for maximum emphasis: *nunca jamás* ("never ever"). *No pienso volver allí nunca jamás* — "I don't intend to ever go back there again." It's an intensifying idiom, not a case of the two words meaning different things.
</GrammarNote>

### Nada and nadie: nothing and no one
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Nada* ("nothing") and *nadie* ("no one") are the full pronoun pair introduced in [Chapter 19](../lesson-19) — invariable, standing in for the nonexistence of a thing or a person. Because *nadie* refers to a person, it triggers the personal *a* exactly like any other human direct object. Chapter 19 flagged one example here and promised this chapter would cover the placement logic behind it in full:

<ExampleSentence es="No conozco a nadie en esta ciudad." en="I don't know anyone in this city." note="This is exactly the sentence Chapter 19 promised this chapter would fully explain — a nadie, the personal a, plus the no that has to appear because nadie lands after the verb." />
<ExampleSentence es="No hay nada en la nevera." en="There's nothing in the fridge." />

### Tampoco: the negative counterpart to también
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Tampoco* means "not either" / "neither" — it's the exact negative mirror of *también* ("also," "too"). Like *también*, it's flexible about position, and it shows up constantly as a one-word answer:

<ExampleSentence es="—No me gusta el pescado. —A mí tampoco." en="'I don't like fish.' 'Me neither.'" note="A near-universal short-answer idiom — no verb needed at all." />
<ExampleSentence es="Tampoco quiero ir a la fiesta." en="I don't want to go to the party either." />
<ExampleSentence es="Vosotros tampoco tenéis razón esta vez." en="You all aren't right this time either." note="Castellano vosotros form — tampoco works identically with every subject." />

### Ni siquiera: not even
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Ni siquiera* means "not even" — unlike the other words in this section, it isn't a standalone pronoun or adverb naming a thing, person, or time. It's an intensifier: it attaches to whatever element of the sentence deserves the extra emphasis (a subject, an object, a quantity) and makes an already-negative statement more forceful.

<ExampleSentence es="Ni siquiera me llamó para explicarme qué pasó." en="He/She didn't even call to explain to me what happened." />
<ExampleSentence es="No tengo ni siquiera un euro encima." en="I don't have even a euro on me." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
In everyday spoken Castellano you'll also hear *ni tan siquiera* — a slightly more emphatic spoken variant of *ni siquiera*, same meaning, common in Spain in casual speech.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: Double Negation Is a Rule, Not a Mistake

Here's the system that ties everything above together. Think of the position directly in front of the conjugated verb as a single required slot: **every negative sentence in Spanish must have a negative word filling that slot.** *No* is the default word that fills it when nothing else does — but if one of this chapter's other negative words is already sitting there instead, it does *no*'s job, and *no* itself has no reason to appear.

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Unlike several other topics in this book, this rule doesn't vary by region — the double-negation system is identical across Spain and Latin America. What differs from one word to the next is only how formal or colloquial it sounds (*jamás* vs. *nunca*, *ni tan siquiera* vs. *ni siquiera*), never the underlying placement logic.
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="tip">
[Chapter 53, Basic Word Order & Sentence Structure](../lesson-53) covers how flexible Spanish word order can be in general. Negative-word placement is a special case of that flexibility, but it isn't a free stylistic choice the way some reordering is — it's governed by the specific rule below, with only one grammatical answer for a given position.
</GrammarNote>

### Negative word after the verb: no is required
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

If the only negative word in the clause comes *after* the verb — as an object, or as an adverb tacked on at the end — that preverbal slot is empty, and Spanish fills it with *no*. Leaving *no* out here isn't a stylistic shortcut; the sentence stops being grammatical.

<ExampleSentence es="No llegó nadie." en="Nobody arrived." note="Nadie sits after the verb, so no is required in front of it." />
<ExampleSentence es="No conozco a nadie." en="I don't know anyone." note="The Chapter 19 anchor sentence, in its shortest form." />

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "No", role: "connector" },
    { text: "llegó", role: "verb" },
    { text: "nadie.", role: "subject" }
  ]}
/>

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Llegó nadie*, with no *no* at all, isn't a looser or more casual version of the sentence above — it's simply ungrammatical. The same holds for every word in this chapter: *voy al gimnasio nunca* and *tengo ni siquiera un euro*, without *no*, are both flat errors, not stylistic variants.
</GrammarNote>

### Negative word before the verb: it replaces no
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Now the flip side, which is the actual reason the rule above exists rather than being an arbitrary requirement: if a negative word moves *in front of* the verb — most often because it's the subject, or because it's an adverb fronted for emphasis — it already fills the one required slot. *No* would be doing a job that's already done, so Spanish simply drops it.

<ExampleSentence es="Nadie llegó." en="Nobody arrived." note="Same meaning as No llegó nadie above — nadie itself now occupies the slot no would otherwise fill, so no doesn't appear." />
<ExampleSentence es="Nada me sorprende ya." en="Nothing surprises me anymore." note="The Chapter 19 opening example — nada as subject, before the verb, no no in sight." />

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Nadie", role: "subject" },
    { text: "llegó.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>

*Nadie llegó* and *No llegó nadie* are both fully correct and mean exactly the same thing — the only thing that changes between them is where the negative word sits, and whether *no* is needed to fill the gap it leaves behind.

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The two negative markers never combine in that same preverbal slot: *No nadie llegó* and *Nadie no llegó* are both wrong, for the same underlying reason — the slot only needs (and only allows) one negative word to fill it, and once something else is already there, adding *no* on top of it is redundant to the point of being ungrammatical.
</GrammarNote>

This is a general mechanism, not something specific to *nadie* — the same logic runs through every word in this chapter, and through *ninguno/ningún* from [Chapter 8](../lesson-08) as well:

<ExampleSentence es="No tengo ningún interés en eso." en="I have no interest in that." note="Ningún interés lands after the verb — no is required, exactly like nadie or nunca after the verb." />
<ExampleSentence es="Ningún estudiante llegó tarde." en="No student arrived late." note="Ningún estudiante is the subject, sitting before the verb — no drops, exactly like nadie or nada before the verb." />

### Ni...ni: the same rule, one more time
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

[Chapter 51](../lesson-51) introduced *ni...ni* ("neither...nor") as a coordinating conjunction and pointed here for the placement logic behind it, rather than teaching that logic as something unique to *ni...ni*. It isn't unique — *ni...ni* is simply this chapter's rule showing up with a paired conjunction instead of a single word:

<ExampleSentence es="No tengo ni tiempo ni dinero para el viaje." en="I have neither time nor money for the trip." note="Ni...ni lands after the verb, so no is required in front of it — same pattern as nadie or nunca after the verb." />
<ExampleSentence es="Ni Juan ni María llegaron a tiempo." en="Neither Juan nor María arrived on time." note="Ni...ni fronts the whole subject, before the verb, so no drops entirely — same pattern as Nadie llegó." />

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Ni Juan ni María", role: "subject" },
    { text: "llegaron", role: "verb" },
    { text: "a tiempo.", role: "other" }
  ]}
/>

Nothing new to learn here beyond recognizing the pattern: wherever a negative word or phrase sits relative to the verb — one word, or a paired conjunction like *ni...ni* — the same before/after logic decides whether *no* appears.

### Stacking multiple negative words
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Because the actual requirement is "at least one negative word before the verb," not "one *no* per negative word," a single clause can pile up several negative words at once with only one of them doing the preverbal work:

<ExampleSentence es="Nunca le cuento nada a nadie." en="I never tell anyone anything." note="Nunca fills the one required preverbal slot; nada and a nadie both stack after the verb for free, with no extra no needed for either of them." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Two negative words can even sit before the verb together, still satisfying the same single requirement: *Nunca nadie me lo había explicado así* — "Never had anyone explained it to me like that." Whether one word or two ends up in front of the verb, the clause still only ever needs (and only ever gets) one shot at that slot — it's just filled by a negative word instead of by *no*.
</GrammarNote>

### The English-speaker trap: don't "fix" the double negative
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

Standard English treats stacked negatives as an error to eliminate — "I don't know nobody" reads as nonstandard, and the "correct" fix is "I don't know anybody." That instinct, aimed at a Spanish sentence, produces exactly the wrong result. Faced with *No conozco a nadie*, an English-trained ear can feel a pull to delete one of the two negatives — but *Conozco a nadie*, with *no* removed, isn't a cleaner version of the sentence. It's simply broken.

<ExampleSentence es="No conozco a nadie." en="I don't know anyone." note="Word-for-word, this reads as 'not I-know to nobody' — two negatives that reinforce each other rather than cancelling out, the opposite of the English rule." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Hold onto one test when this instinct kicks in: ask whether the negative word comes before or after the verb, not whether the sentence already "has enough" negation. After the verb, *no* is mandatory, no matter how many negative words follow it.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

Faced with a negative sentence, work through it in two steps. First: is there a negative word in the sentence at all, beyond *no* itself? If not, *no* alone does the whole job, directly in front of the verb. If so, second: where does that word sit relative to the verb? After it — *no* still has to appear before the verb, and the negative word simply adds onto it. Before it — the negative word does *no*'s job on its own, and *no* itself doesn't appear. Every word in this chapter, and *ninguno* and *ni...ni* besides, follows that one two-step test without exception.

<ExampleSentence es="No he hablado nunca con nadie sobre esto, y tampoco pienso hacerlo ahora — ni siquiera contigo." en="I have never talked to anyone about this, and I don't intend to do it now either — not even with you." note="No...nunca...nadie stack after one preverbal no; tampoco fronts its own clause and drops no entirely; ni siquiera closes the sentence as a pure intensifier. Every rule in this chapter in one breath." />
