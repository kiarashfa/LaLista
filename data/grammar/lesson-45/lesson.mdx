Spanish has dozens of everyday constructions that pair a conjugated verb with an infinitive or a gerund to build a meaning neither verb carries alone: a near-future plan, an action that just finished, something happening again, an obligation, a habit still going strong. English does the same kind of thing constantly — "I'm going to leave," "I just ate," "I have to go" — without ever giving the pattern a name. Spanish grammar does: **perífrasis verbal**, "verbal periphrasis," literally a roundabout way of saying something a single verb form can't say alone. You've already met several of these piecemeal, previewed in earlier chapters — *ir a* + infinitivo in [Chapters 22](../lesson-22), [33](../lesson-33), and [36](../lesson-36); *seguir/andar/llevar* + gerundio in [Chapter 25](../lesson-25). This chapter gathers all of them in one place, gives each its full treatment, and — more importantly — shows that they share one underlying skeleton. That shared shape is the real insight to take from this chapter, more than any single construction on the list.

## Core Pattern: One Skeleton, Three Kinds of Link

### The shape every periphrasis shares
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Every verbal periphrasis has the same three-part anatomy: a **conjugated verb** that carries all the person, number, and tense information, followed by an optional **link**, followed by a **non-finite form** — an infinitive ([Chapter 42](../lesson-42)) or a gerund ([Chapter 43](../lesson-43)), never a second conjugated verb. What varies from one periphrasis to the next isn't the shape, only what fills the middle slot: nothing at all, a fixed preposition, or the word *que*.

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Debo", role: "verb" },
    { text: "salir.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Voy", role: "verb" },
    { text: "a", role: "connector" },
    { text: "salir.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Tengo", role: "verb" },
    { text: "que", role: "connector" },
    { text: "salir.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>

Three different meanings — "I should leave," "I'm going to leave," "I have to leave" — built from the exact same three-slot skeleton, just with a different link (none, *a*, *que*) filling the middle.

### Why the shared shape matters more than any single item on the list
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Once you can recognize this skeleton, learning a new periphrasis stops being "memorize a whole new sentence pattern" and becomes "learn this construction's link and its meaning" — a much smaller task. It also explains why periphrases show up throughout earlier chapters without being named as a group: *estar* + gerundio ([Chapter 25](../lesson-25)) is a periphrasis (no link), and you'll meet modal-flavored ones like *poder*/*querer* + infinitivo elsewhere, all built on this identical frame. The six constructions below are simply the members of this family substantial enough, and common enough, to deserve their own dedicated tour.

## Building Out: Six Periphrases, One at a Time

### Ir a + infinitivo: the true home of the near future
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

*Ir*, conjugated in the present ([Chapter 23](../lesson-23): *voy, vas, va, vamos, vais, van*), plus *a*, plus an infinitive — Spanish's everyday way to talk about something planned, decided, or expected to happen soon:

<ExampleSentence es="Voy a comer paella esta noche." en="I'm going to eat paella tonight." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Vais a venir a la fiesta?" en="Are you all going to come to the party?" />
<ExampleSentence es="Va a llover esta tarde." en="It's going to rain this afternoon." />

[Chapter 33](../lesson-33) already introduced this construction enough to use it correctly and contrasted it with the futuro simple (roughly: *ir a* + inf. for near, decided, conversational plans; futuro simple for predictions, promises, and more formal register). Nothing about that contrast changes here — this section is about rounding out everything Chapter 33 didn't have room for.

### Object pronouns with ir a + infinitivo
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Because the non-finite form here is a plain infinitive, pronoun placement follows the exact two-position rule [Chapter 12](../lesson-12) established for every conjugated-verb-plus-infinitive pairing: attach the pronoun to the end of the infinitive, or move it in front of the whole *ir a* unit. Both are equally correct and mean exactly the same thing.

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Voy", role: "verb" },
    { text: "a", role: "connector" },
    { text: "comprarlo.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure

    { text: "voy", role: "verb" },

/>

<ExampleSentence es="Voy a llamarte mañana. / Te voy a llamar mañana." en="I'm going to call you tomorrow." note="Identical meaning — free stylistic variation, just like with any other infinitive pairing." />

### Idiomatic extensions of ir a
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A few fixed expressions grow directly out of *ir a*, worth recognizing as set phrases rather than working out from scratch each time:

<ExampleSentence es="¡Vamos!" en="Come on! / Let's go!" note="As a standalone interjection, vamos is encouragement or urging, not literally 'we go' — context, not grammar, tells the two apart." />
<ExampleSentence es="Vamos a ver qué pasa." en="Let's see what happens." note="vamos a ver — a fixed way to signal 'we'll find out,' used constantly even with no literal seeing involved." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Vienes a la fiesta? Va a ser que no." en="Are you coming to the party? Guess not." note="va a ser que no — a common hedging idiom for softly declining or predicting a negative outcome, literally 'it's going to be that no.'" />

### Acabar de + infinitivo: something just happened
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Acabar de* + infinitive describes an action completed in the immediate past — English's "just did something." The grammatical twist worth noticing: *acabar* itself sits in the **present** tense here, not the past, even though the meaning is squarely about something already finished.

<ExampleSentence es="Acabo de comer." en="I just ate." note="Acabo is present tense — Spanish doesn't reach for a past tense at all to say 'just did,' unlike English's 'just + past tense.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="Acaban de llegar." en="They just arrived." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is a genuine structural mismatch with English worth holding onto: English marks "just did X" with its past tense plus the word "just." Spanish marks the identical meaning with its **present** tense plus *acabar de* — the "just-ness" lives entirely in the periphrasis, not in a past-tense verb form.
</GrammarNote>

### Acabar de in the imperfect: "had just" done something
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The same construction shifts naturally into the imperfect to describe something that had just happened relative to a point in the past — the exact past-tense parallel of the present-tense pattern above:

<ExampleSentence es="Acababa de llegar cuando sonó el teléfono." en="I had just arrived when the phone rang." note="acababa (imperfect) + de + llegar — the past-time version of acabo de llegar." />

### Volver a + infinitivo: doing it again
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Volver a* + infinitive means "to do something again" — a cleaner, more compact alternative to tacking *otra vez* onto the plain verb, and the more natural choice in a great deal of everyday Spanish:

<ExampleSentence es="Volvió a llamar." en="He called again." note="Cleaner and more common than Llamó otra vez for many everyday contexts, though both are correct." />
<ExampleSentence es="No vuelvas a hacerlo." en="Don't do it again." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Otra vez* hasn't disappeared — native speakers sometimes even stack both (*volvió a llamar otra vez*), which is redundant but heard constantly in casual speech. The periphrasis is simply the more economical default, not a hard replacement.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Llevar + gerundio: framing duration
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Llevar* + gerund is genuinely one of the most useful constructions in this chapter, and it has no single-word English equivalent — it frames how long an action has been ongoing:

<ExampleSentence es="Llevo dos años estudiando español." en="I've been studying Spanish for two years." note="Not he estudiado — Spanish doesn't reach for a perfect tense here at all; llevar + gerund is the natural way to frame ongoing duration." />
<ExampleSentence es="Llevamos una hora esperando el autobús." en="We've been waiting for the bus for an hour." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This is a real trap for English speakers: the instinct is to reach for a present-perfect-progressive equivalent ("I have been studying"), but Spanish's *haber* + participle system ([Chapter 30](../lesson-30)) doesn't build duration this way at all. *Llevar* + gerund is the idiomatic tool for "have been doing X for [amount of time]" — not a literal translation of the English perfect.
</GrammarNote>

*Llevar* extends the same duration logic to a negative action with *sin* + infinitive instead of a gerund:

<ExampleSentence es="Llevo tres días sin dormir bien." en="I haven't slept well in three days." note="sin + infinitive replaces the gerund when the ongoing state is the absence of an action." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Don't confuse this with *llevar* + **participle** (*llevo escritas tres páginas* — "I've got three pages written"), covered in [Chapter 44](../lesson-44). That construction agrees like an adjective and describes a completed result; this one is invariant and describes time still elapsing. Same verb, two genuinely different jobs.
</GrammarNote>

### Tener que, haber que, and deber: three ways to say "must"
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Spanish doesn't have one all-purpose word for obligation — it has three, and they aren't interchangeable. Which one fits depends on *who* the obligation belongs to and how strong it is.

**Tener que + infinitivo** is personal, subject-specific obligation — something *this particular person* has to do. *Tener* conjugates exactly as it did in [Chapter 23](../lesson-23) (*tengo, tienes, tiene, tenemos, tenéis, tienen*); *que* simply follows every form:

<ExampleSentence es="Tengo que terminar el informe hoy." en="I have to finish the report today." note="A concrete obligation tied to yo specifically." />

**Haber que + infinitivo** is impersonal, general obligation — something that needs doing, with no specific person named as responsible. Unlike *tener que*, this construction never personalizes: it only ever exists as *hay que* (present), *había que* (imperfect), *habrá que* (future), and so on — the same invariant *hay* you met as an existential in [Chapter 30](../lesson-30) and will see again in full in [Chapter 47](../lesson-47):

<ExampleSentence es="Hay que estudiar mucho para aprobar." en="One has to study a lot to pass. / It's necessary to study a lot to pass." note="No subject at all — hay que never conjugates for person." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
"Yo hay que estudiar" is always wrong — *hay que* can't take a subject, personal or otherwise. If the obligation belongs to a specific person, reach for *tener que* instead.
</GrammarNote>

**Deber (+ infinitivo)** is the softest of the three — "should" or "ought to" rather than a hard requirement. It conjugates as a fully regular *-er* verb, with no irregularity to flag:

<ExampleSentence es="Deberías dormir más." en="You should sleep more." note="The conditional deberías (Chapter 34) softens the advice even further — extremely common for suggestions." />
<ExampleSentence es="Debemos ser puntuales." en="We should be punctual." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Lined up together, the three make a genuine register ladder: *tengo que terminar el informe* (a real, personal deadline) is stronger than *hay que reciclar más* (a general, nobody-in-particular obligation), which is stronger than *deberías dormir más* (friendly advice, easy to decline without consequence).
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Watch for *deber de* + infinitive, a separate, older use of the same verb: historically *deber* + inf. meant obligation and *deber de* + inf. meant conjecture ("must be," in the sense of a probable guess — *debe de tener treinta años*, "he must be about thirty," a cousin of [Chapter 33's](../lesson-33) future-of-probability). In everyday modern speech, including in Spain, the two are frequently blurred and *deber* alone often covers both senses — but a careful writer, or a formal register, may still keep them apart.
</GrammarNote>

### Seguir/continuar (and andar) + gerundio: staying in motion
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Seguir* + gerund means to keep on doing something, with no interruption — an action that started before now and hasn't stopped:

<ExampleSentence es="Sigue lloviendo." en="It's still raining." />
<ExampleSentence es="Seguimos esperando una respuesta." en="We're still waiting for an answer." />

*Continuar* + gerund means the same thing and is fully interchangeable with *seguir*, just a shade more formal:

<ExampleSentence es="El precio continúa subiendo." en="The price keeps rising." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Don't confuse this with *volver a* + infinitive above: *volver a* marks a single action happening **again** after a genuine stop (*volvió a llover* — it rained again, after a dry spell), while *seguir*/*continuar* + gerund marks an action that **never stopped** in the first place. One resumes; the other never paused.
</GrammarNote>

A close relative rounds out this family: *andar* + gerund also describes an ongoing action, but it typically adds a mildly restless, critical, or gossipy undertone that neither *seguir* nor *continuar* carries — English usually needs a whole extra phrase ("going around," "walking around") to capture the same flavor:

<ExampleSentence es="Anda diciendo que va a dejar el trabajo." en="He's going around saying he's going to quit his job." note="andar + gerund — the ongoing action is framed with a hint of disapproval or gossip that plain estar + gerund (Chapter 25) wouldn't carry." />

## Bringing It Together

| Periphrasis | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| ir a + inf. | near future, plan | voy a salir |
| acabar de + inf. | just did something | acabo de salir |
| volver a + inf. | do it again | volví a salir |
| llevar + gerundio | ongoing duration | llevo una hora esperando |
| tener que + inf. | personal obligation | tengo que salir |
| hay que + inf. | impersonal obligation | hay que salir temprano |
| deber (+ inf.) | should / ought to | debo salir |
| seguir/continuar + gerundio | uninterrupted continuation | sigo esperando |

<ExampleSentence es="Llevo media hora esperando y todavía no ha llegado. Acaba de escribirme que va a salir tarde otra vez — tengo que avisar a los demás, aunque supongo que va a ser que no llegamos a tiempo." en="I've been waiting half an hour and he still hasn't arrived. He just texted me that he's going to be late again — I have to let the others know, though I guess we're not going to make it on time." note="llevo...esperando (duration), acaba de escribirme (just happened), va a salir (near future), tengo que (personal obligation), va a ser que no (hedging idiom) — five different periphrases, one short exchange." />

Faced with a sentence that needs a meaning English would build with "going to," "just," "again," "have been doing," or "have to," check first whether Spanish already has a dedicated periphrasis for it — it almost always does. Identify the conjugated verb, find its link (none, a preposition, or *que*), and attach the infinitive or gerund the construction calls for. The six constructions above cover the everyday core of the whole system.
