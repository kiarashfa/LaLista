Every subjunctive verb you'll ever build — present, imperfect, compound, all of it — ultimately traces back to the formation rule in this chapter. [Chapter 20](../lesson-20) introduced mood as a separate question from tense: not just *when* something happens, but *how* the speaker is framing it — as a plain fact, or as a wish, doubt, emotion, or command. This chapter is where that idea, only previewed there, finally becomes something you can build with your own hands. The good news is that most of the subjunctive's reputation for unpredictability collapses into a single move you already half-know: almost every present subjunctive form is built by taking a verb's own present-indicative *yo* form, stripping the final **-o**, and adding the "opposite" vowel. This chapter is formation only — getting every form right. [Chapter 38](../lesson-38) covers the separate, equally important question of when a sentence actually reaches for one of these forms.

## Core Pattern: The Flip-the-Vowel Rule

### From the present-indicative yo-form to the subjunctive stem
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

For **-ar** verbs, take the present-indicative yo-form, drop the **-o**, and add **-e, -es, -e, -emos, -éis, -en** — the exact opposite vowel family from the **-a** endings these verbs use in the indicative:

<BootTable
  label="hablar"
  caption="present subjunctive"
  forms={{
    "yo": "hable",
    "tú": "hables",
    "él/ella/usted": "hable",
    "nosotros": "hablemos",
    "vosotros": "habléis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "hablen"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Quiero que hables con ella." en="I want you to talk to her." note="hablo (indicative yo-form) minus -o, plus -es for tú, gives hables." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
No boot outline appears here, for the same reason [Chapter 22](../lesson-22)'s regular present-tense tables show none: every one of the six cells is built the same predictable way, with nothing unusual clustering around any particular person.
</GrammarNote>

### -ER and -IR verbs: the mirror image
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

**-er** and **-ir** verbs flip the other way: their present-indicative endings are built around **-e**, so their subjunctive endings switch to **-a, -as, -a, -amos, -áis, -an**.

<BootTable
  label="vivir"
  caption="present subjunctive"
  forms={{
    "yo": "viva",
    "tú": "vivas",
    "él/ella/usted": "viva",
    "nosotros": "vivamos",
    "vosotros": "viváis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "vivan"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Es mejor que vivas cerca del trabajo." en="It's better that you live near work." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Comer*'s subjunctive (coma, comas, coma, comamos, comáis, coman) is built the identical way — and just like the preterite endings [Chapter 26](../lesson-26) pointed out, **-er** and **-ir** verbs share exactly the same subjunctive endings. Only the stem vowel that carries through (com- vs. viv-) tells them apart, not the endings themselves.
</GrammarNote>

Whatever a verb's yo-form happens to be in the present indicative — regular or irregular — take it, drop the **-o**, add the opposite-vowel set above, and you have the entire present subjunctive paradigm. Nearly everything left in this chapter is really just different flavors of "what happens when that yo-form is already irregular."

## Building Out: Stem-Changes and Irregular Yo-Forms Carry Over

### -AR and -ER stem-changers keep the exact same boot
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

For the e→ie and o→ue stem-changing verbs [Chapter 23](../lesson-23) taught in the present indicative, the subjunctive traces the identical four-cell boot — *yo, tú, él/ella/usted, ellos/ellas/ustedes* change; *nosotros/vosotros* don't:

<BootTable
  label="querer"
  caption="present subjunctive"
  forms={{
    "yo": "quiera",
    "tú": "quieras",
    "él/ella/usted": "quiera",
    "nosotros": "queramos",
    "vosotros": "queráis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "quieran"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Ojalá que puedas venir mañana." en="I hope you can come tomorrow." note="poder follows the identical o→ue boot: pueda, puedas, pueda, podamos, podáis, puedan." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is the same stress logic Chapter 23 built the boot around in the first place: *nosotros* and *vosotros* stress the ending (que-RA-mos), not the stem, so the stem stays plain there — in the subjunctive exactly as in the indicative.
</GrammarNote>

### -IR stem-changers: the boot, plus a second shift in nosotros/vosotros
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

-IR stem-changers add one genuine wrinkle: alongside the familiar four-cell boot, *nosotros* and *vosotros* pick up a second, weaker vowel shift that never shows up in the present indicative — e→ie verbs weaken to i, and o→ue verbs weaken to u:

<BootTable
  label="dormir"
  caption="present subjunctive"
  forms={{
    "yo": "duerma",
    "tú": "duermas",
    "él/ella/usted": "duerma",
    "nosotros": "durmamos",
    "vosotros": "durmáis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "duerman"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Prefiero que durmamos aquí esta noche." en="I'd rather we sleep here tonight." note="durmamos — not dormamos — carries the weak o→u shift into nosotros, unlike dormir's present indicative (dormimos)." />

*Sentir* works the same way (sienta, sientas, sienta, sintamos, sintáis, sientan). The narrower e→i family — *pedir, servir, seguir* — doesn't add a distinct second shift at all, because its "weak" vowel and its "boot" vowel are the same e→i change already, so it simply carries e→i through all six cells: *pida, pidas, pida, pidamos, pidáis, pidan.*

<GrammarNote type="regional">
This same weak-vowel shift is the one behind the gerunds *durmiendo* and *pidiendo* ([Chapter 25](../lesson-25)) and the third-person preterite forms *durmió/pidió* ([Chapter 27](../lesson-27)) — three different grammar points sharing one underlying vowel-weakening rule in **-ir** verbs.
</GrammarNote>

### Pure yo-irregulars: the irregularity spreads everywhere — and the boot vanishes
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Here's the real payoff of [Chapter 23](../lesson-23)'s yo-form irregulars: because the *entire* subjunctive paradigm is built from that one irregular yo-form, the irregularity doesn't cluster in a boot shape at all — it spreads evenly across all six cells:

<BootTable
  label="tener"
  caption="present subjunctive"
  forms={{
    "yo": "tenga",
    "tú": "tengas",
    "él/ella/usted": "tenga",
    "nosotros": "tengamos",
    "vosotros": "tengáis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "tengan"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Espero que tengas suerte." en="I hope you have luck." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Notice what happened to *tener*'s e→ie change: it's completely gone. In the present indicative, *tener* is both g-inserted (tengo) and a stem-changer (tienes, tiene, tienen) — but because *tengo* itself never shows the ie change, and the whole subjunctive paradigm builds from *tengo*, no cell of the subjunctive ever sees *ie* either. The indicative's boot doesn't carry over — it simply isn't there anymore.
</GrammarNote>

Every other yo-irregular verb from Chapter 23 works the identical uniform way, whether the original irregularity was a g-insertion, a -zco ending, or an orthographic swap:

| Infinitive | yo (indicative) | Subjunctive (yo/usted form) |
|---|---|---|
| poner | pongo | ponga |
| salir | salgo | salga |
| hacer | hago | haga |
| decir | digo | diga |
| venir | vengo | venga |
| conocer | conozco | conozca |
| ver | veo | vea |
| oír | oigo | oiga |
| traer | traigo | traiga |
| caer | caigo | caiga |
| coger | cojo | coja |
| seguir | sigo | siga |
| elegir | elijo | elija |

<ExampleSentence es="No creo que ella conozca a mi hermano." en="I don't think she knows my brother." note="conozca — every one of the six forms shares the same conozc- stem, exactly like conozco itself." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Spelling-change verbs: the same "regular under the hood" logic, now spread across all six persons
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

**-car, -gar,** and **-zar** verbs need the familiar orthographic swap that keeps their hard consonant sound intact before a vowel that would otherwise soften it — the same kind of "regular under the hood" adjustment [Chapter 2](../lesson-02) showed for plurals like *lápiz → lápices*, [Chapter 9](../lesson-09) showed for apocope like *bueno → buen*, and [Chapter 26](../lesson-26) showed for the preterite yo-form. Here, though, the swap isn't confined to a single cell the way it was in the preterite: since every subjunctive ending for an **-ar** verb starts with **-e**, the swap now has to apply to *all six* persons, not just yo.

<BootTable
  label="buscar"
  caption="present subjunctive"
  forms={{
    "yo": "busque",
    "tú": "busques",
    "él/ella/usted": "busque",
    "nosotros": "busquemos",
    "vosotros": "busquéis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "busquen"
  }}
/>

- **-car → -que**: *buscar → busque* (busques, busque, busquemos, busquéis, busquen)
- **-gar → -gue**: *llegar → llegue* (llegues, llegue, lleguemos, lleguéis, lleguen)
- **-zar → -ce**: *empezar → empiece* (empieces, empiece — note the e→ie boot also applies — but empecemos, empecéis, without ie, since nosotros/vosotros stay outside the boot even while still needing the z→c spelling swap)

<ExampleSentence es="Es importante que lleguemos a tiempo." en="It's important that we arrive on time." note="lleguemos needs the g→gu swap even in nosotros — unlike the preterite's llegué, where only yo needed it." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
These are the exact same *busque, llegue,* and *empiece* [Chapter 36](../lesson-36) used for *usted* commands and negative *tú* commands. Command forms and subjunctive forms aren't just similar here — for these persons, they're mechanically the identical word.
</GrammarNote>

### The five true irregulars: ser, estar, ir, dar, saber
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A small, closed set of verbs doesn't even follow the yo-form-minus-o rule — their subjunctive stems have to be memorized outright. These are exactly the five [Chapter 36](../lesson-36) already named as commands' own irregular exceptions:

| | ser | estar | ir | dar | saber |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **yo** | sea | esté | vaya | dé | sepa |
| **tú** | seas | estés | vayas | des | sepas |
| **él/ella/usted** | sea | esté | vaya | dé | sepa |
| **nosotros** | seamos | estemos | vayamos | demos | sepamos |
| **vosotros** | seáis | estéis | vayáis | deis | sepáis |
| **ellos/ellas/ustedes** | sean | estén | vayan | den | sepan |

<ExampleSentence es="Espero que todo salga bien y que seas feliz." en="I hope everything goes well and that you're happy." />
<ExampleSentence es="No hay nadie que sepa la respuesta." en="There's no one who knows the answer." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Dé* keeps its written accent in every form that would otherwise collide with another word — here, the preposition *de.* Compare Chapter 36's *sé* (the *tú* command of *ser*), which needs its own accent to stay distinct from the pronoun *se.* Only *demos* and *deis* drop the accent, since neither collides with anything.
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Haber* has its own fully irregular subjunctive too — *haya* — but since *haber* almost only ever functions as the auxiliary in compound tenses ([Chapter 30](../lesson-30)), you'll meet *haya* properly in [Chapter 40](../lesson-40)'s compound subjunctive rather than here.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

Faced with any verb, work through it in this order: first, is it one of the five true irregulars (ser, estar, ir, dar, saber) — memorize those outright. If not, take the present-indicative yo-form, drop the **-o**. Is that yo-form already irregular (a stem change, a g-insertion, a -zco, an orthographic swap)? If so, that same irregularity now applies uniformly across every person — evenly, with no boot, unless the verb is an **-ir** stem-changer, which adds one more weak-vowel shift in *nosotros/vosotros*. Does the stem end in **-car, -gar,** or **-zar**? Apply the spelling swap across all six persons, not just yo. Otherwise, the verb is simply regular: flip the vowel — **-e** for **-ar**, **-a** for **-er/-ir** — and you're done.

<ExampleSentence es="Ojalá que tengamos suerte, que el vuelo no se retrase y que lleguemos a tiempo para que empiece la reunión." en="I hope we have luck, that the flight isn't delayed, and that we arrive in time for the meeting to start." note="tengamos (yo-irregular, uniform), se retrase (regular -ar), lleguemos (spelling-change, all six persons), empiece (spelling-change plus e→ie boot) — four different formation patterns in one sentence." />
