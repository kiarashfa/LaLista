The futuro compuesto and condicional compuesto are the "perfect" partners of the two tenses covered in the last two chapters: habré/habrás/habrá... + participle for "will have done," and habría/habrías/habría... + participle for "would have done." Both pieces should already feel familiar — the auxiliary is just *haber* conjugated in the future ([Chapter 33](../lesson-33)) or conditional ([Chapter 34](../lesson-34)) you already know, and the participle is the exact same irregular list from [Chapter 30](../lesson-30). This chapter is mostly about assembling parts you already own, plus a bit of genuinely new territory in when and why you'd reach for each one.

## Core Pattern: Habré / Habría + Participle

### Futuro compuesto: haber's future, plus a participle
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Haber* is one of the twelve irregular stems from [Chapter 33](../lesson-33) — part of the vowel-drop group, stem *habr-*. Its future-tense personal forms are simply that stem plus the future endings you already know, functioning here purely as an auxiliary, the same restricted role *haber* plays in every compound tense since [Chapter 30](../lesson-30).

<BootTable
  label="haber"
  caption="future — the same habr- stem grouped with Chapter 33's vowel-drop family"
  forms={{
    "yo": "habré",
    "tú": "habrás",
    "él/ella/usted": "habrá",
    "nosotros": "habremos",
    "vosotros": "habréis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "habrán"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Para las cinco, ya habré terminado el informe." en="By five o'clock, I'll have already finished the report." />

### Condicional compuesto: haber's conditional, plus a participle
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Swap the auxiliary's tense to the conditional ([Chapter 34](../lesson-34)) — same *habr-* stem, different ending set — and the meaning shifts from "will have done" to "would have done."

<BootTable
  label="haber"
  caption="conditional — the same habr- stem, Chapter 34's ending set"
  forms={{
    "yo": "habría",
    "tú": "habrías",
    "él/ella/usted": "habría",
    "nosotros": "habríamos",
    "vosotros": "habríais",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "habrían"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Yo habría llamado antes, pero se me olvidó el teléfono." en="I would have called earlier, but I forgot my phone." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Habríais terminado el proyecto para el lunes?" en="Would you all have finished the project by Monday?" />

### The participle: Chapter 30's list, not re-derived
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Both compound tenses attach to exactly the same past participle taught in [Chapter 30](../lesson-30): the regular -ado/-ido pattern, and the ten core irregulars (escrito, hecho, dicho, visto, puesto, roto, vuelto, muerto, abierto, cubierto) plus their compounds. Nothing about the participle changes moving from the present perfect to these two tenses — only the auxiliary's own tense shifts, exactly the same move [Chapter 31](../lesson-31) made from *he* to *había* for the pluperfect.

<ExampleSentence es="¿Quién habrá roto el jarrón?" en="I wonder who broke the vase." note="Roto — the same irregular participle from Chapter 30, unchanged." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
As always with haber + participle, the participle itself never agrees with anything — habré escrito, habrán escrito, never "habrán escritas" even with a feminine plural object in view. The full explanation of why the participle freezes inside a compound tense, and how that contrasts with its adjective use, lives in Chapter 30; this chapter's participles follow the identical rule without exception.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: What Each One Actually Means

### Futuro compuesto: a completed action, viewed from a future point
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The futuro compuesto describes an action that will already be finished before some other future reference point — often marked by *para* + a time expression, or by *cuando* + another clause.

<ExampleSentence es="Cuando llegues, ya habremos cenado." en="By the time you arrive, we'll have already had dinner." note="Cenado is complete before llegues, the future reference point the whole sentence is anchored to." />
<ExampleSentence es="Para el año que viene, habré ahorrado lo suficiente para el viaje." en="By next year, I'll have saved enough for the trip." />

### Condicional compuesto: an unrealized past hypothetical
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The condicional compuesto describes what would have happened under some condition — but didn't. It's the natural home for regret, missed opportunity, and "if only" territory.

<ExampleSentence es="Habría ido a la fiesta, pero tuve que trabajar." en="I would have gone to the party, but I had to work." />
<ExampleSentence es="Te habría avisado, pero no tenía tu número." en="I would have let you know, but I didn't have your number." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### A third conjecture use: guessing about a recently completed action
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

[Chapter 33](../lesson-33) showed the futuro simple guessing about the present (estará en casa), and [Chapter 34](../lesson-34) showed the condicional simple making the same kind of guess about a past moment (serían las tres). The futuro compuesto completes that pattern with a third variant: a guess that some action has probably already been completed.

<ExampleSentence es="No contesta el teléfono — habrá salido ya." en="He's not answering the phone — he's probably already left." note="Habrá salido isn't a real future statement; it's a guess that the leaving has already happened, the same conjecture logic as Chapter 33's estará and Chapter 34's serían, applied here to a completed action instead of an ongoing state." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se habrán quedado sin batería." en="Their phone has probably died." note="Literally 'they will have run out of battery' — but the real meaning is a present-tense guess about something already finished." />

### Where the condicional compuesto really earns its keep: si-clauses
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

"Habría ido, pero tuve que trabajar" is a complete, self-sufficient sentence on its own — but the condicional compuesto's richest and most frequent home is paired inside a full hypothetical-past si-clause, alongside the pluperfect subjunctive:

<ExampleSentence es="Si hubiera estudiado más, habría aprobado el examen." en="If I had studied more, I would have passed the exam." note="A preview only — the full si-clause system, including why hubiera pairs with habría here rather than any other combination, is Chapter 41's dedicated topic." />

This chapter has taught the form and its standalone meaning cleanly; [Chapter 41](../lesson-41) is where the two-clause pattern — which past-tense subjunctive form pairs with which compound conditional, and why — gets its full, systematic treatment. Don't expect fluency with full mixed-time conditionals from this chapter alone; that's genuinely Chapter 41's job, not this one's.

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Para el viernes, ya habré entregado el proyecto. Si hubiera empezado antes, ya lo habría terminado del todo, pero bueno — seguramente ya habrán visto que voy avanzando." en="By Friday, I'll have already turned in the project. If I had started earlier, I would have finished it completely by now, but oh well — they've probably already seen that I'm making progress." note="Habré entregado (future perfect, completed by a deadline), habría terminado inside a si-clause preview (Chapter 41's full topic), and habrán visto (conjecture about a likely-already-completed action)." />

Facing one of these two tenses, ask first which direction you're looking from. If you're standing at a future point and describing something that will already be done by then, that's the futuro compuesto. If you're describing something that would have happened under a condition that never actually held, that's the condicional compuesto. And if what you're really doing is guessing that something has probably already happened — with no real future or hypothetical meaning intended — the futuro compuesto's conjecture use, above, is very likely what you want.
