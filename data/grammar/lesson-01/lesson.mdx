Every noun in Spanish carries a gender — masculine or feminine — whether or not the thing it names has a sex at all. A table (*la mesa*) is grammatically feminine; a book (*el libro*) is grammatically masculine. This isn't decorative: gender controls which article, adjective, and pronoun forms a noun can pair with, so getting it right is a prerequisite for almost every other sentence you build in Spanish. The good news is that gender is far more predictable than it first looks — most nouns follow one of a handful of ending patterns, and this chapter walks through those patterns and the real exceptions worth memorizing.

## Core Pattern: The -o / -a Rule

### The reliable default
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

The single most useful fact about Spanish noun gender: nouns ending in **-o** are almost always masculine, and nouns ending in **-a** are almost always feminine. This holds for both people and objects.

<ExampleSentence es="El libro está en la mesa." en="The book is on the table." note="libro (-o) is masculine; mesa (-a) is feminine." />
<ExampleSentence es="El niño juega con la niña." en="The boy plays with the girl." note="Here the same -o/-a pattern also tracks the people's sex." />

For nouns naming people, the -o/-a pattern usually comes as a matched pair: *el niño/la niña*, *el amigo/la amiga*, *el gato/la gata* (for animals too). Use this as your default guess for any unfamiliar noun — it's right often enough to be worth relying on, and the rest of this chapter is devoted to the cases where it isn't.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Always learn a new noun together with its article (*la mesa*, not just *mesa*). The article is the fastest, most durable way to store a noun's gender in memory — and for the exceptions below, it's often the only clue you'll have.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: More Ending Patterns

The -o/-a rule only directly covers nouns that end in a vowel. The same underlying idea — that an ending predicts gender — extends to consonant-ending nouns too, just with a different set of endings to recognize.

### Feminine endings: -ción, -sión, -dad, -tad, -tud, -umbre
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

These endings are consistently feminine, with essentially no common exceptions:

- **-ción / -sión**: *la nación, la decisión, la televisión*
- **-dad / -tad**: *la ciudad, la libertad, la amistad*
- **-tud**: *la actitud, la juventud*
- **-umbre**: *la costumbre, la certidumbre*

<ExampleSentence es="La ciudad tiene una larga tradición." en="The city has a long tradition." />

### Masculine endings: -aje and (usually) -or
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

- **-aje**: *el viaje, el garaje, el equipaje* — reliably masculine, no common exceptions.
- **-or**: *el amor, el color, el dolor, el calor* — masculine in the great majority of cases.

<GrammarNote type="warning">
-or is a strong predictor but not a perfect one: *la flor* (flower) and *la labor* (task, labor) are feminine despite ending in -or. There's no shortcut around memorizing these two — they're common enough to be worth it.
</GrammarNote>

### Person nouns that don't end in -o/-a
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Not every person-noun pair follows -o/-a. A consonant ending often just adds -a for the feminine form (*el profesor/la profesora*, *el escritor/la escritora*), and a few common pairs are simply two different words:

<ExampleSentence es="El hombre y la mujer hablan con el padre y la madre." en="The man and the woman are talking with the father and the mother." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Masculine nouns ending in -a
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The most common trap for learners: a cluster of common nouns ending in **-ma** are masculine, not feminine, because they descend from Greek neuter nouns. *El problema* is the classic example — but there are many more, and they're frequent, everyday words:

*el problema, el programa, el sistema, el clima, el idioma, el tema, el planeta, el mapa, el poema, el drama*

<ExampleSentence es="El problema no tiene una solución fácil." en="The problem doesn't have an easy solution." note="Not 'la problema' — a very common learner error." />

A second, unrelated irregular: *el día* (day) is masculine despite ending in -a, with no deeper pattern behind it — it's simply worth memorizing on its own.

### Feminine nouns ending in -o
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The reverse trap is rarer but real. *La mano* (hand) is feminine and has no good explanation beyond history. A second small group are feminine nouns that are shortened forms of a longer feminine word, which is why they keep feminine gender despite the -o ending:

- *la foto* (short for *la fotografía*)
- *la moto* (short for *la motocicleta*)
- *la disco* (short for *la discoteca*, informal)

<ExampleSentence es="La foto que sacaste es preciosa." en="The photo you took is gorgeous." />

### Dual-gender nouns: el/la estudiante and the -ista family
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Some nouns for people have a single invariant form and let the article alone carry the gender:

<ExampleSentence es="El estudiante llegó tarde." en="The (male) student arrived late." />
<ExampleSentence es="La estudiante llegó tarde." en="The (female) student arrived late." />

This pattern covers most nouns ending in **-ista** (*el/la artista, el/la turista, el/la periodista, el/la dentista*) and several other person-nouns ending in **-e** or **-a** (*el/la joven, el/la testigo, el/la modelo*). None of these change spelling for gender — only *el* or *la* does the work.

### El agua and the stressed a-/ha- exception
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*El agua* (water) is grammatically feminine — it takes feminine adjectives (*el agua fría*, not *frío*) and reverts to *la* in the plural (*las aguas*). But in the singular it pairs with *el*, not *la*, purely to avoid two stressed a-sounds colliding (*la agua* is awkward to say). This is a rule about **article choice**, not a change in the noun's actual gender, so the full mechanics — which nouns qualify, and how it interacts with adjectives — belong to [Chapter 3, Los artículos](../lesson-03). For now, the fact to hold onto is: *agua* is feminine, even though *el agua* looks masculine at a glance.

### Nouns that change meaning with gender
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

A small set of nouns exist in both genders as effectively two different words that happen to share a spelling. Getting the article wrong here doesn't just sound off — it changes what you're saying:

| Noun | Masculine | Feminine |
|---|---|---|
| capital | *el capital* — money, funds | *la capital* — capital city |
| cura | *el cura* — priest | *la cura* — cure |
| guía | *el guía* — (male) guide | *la guía* — guidebook / (female) guide |
| policía | *el policía* — (male) police officer | *la policía* — the police force |
| orden | *el orden* — order, sequence | *la orden* — command; religious order |
| frente | *el frente* — front (war, weather) | *la frente* — forehead |
| cometa | *el cometa* — comet | *la cometa* — kite |

<ExampleSentence es="Necesitamos más capital para el proyecto." en="We need more capital for the project." />
<ExampleSentence es="Madrid es la capital de España." en="Madrid is the capital of Spain." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
*El pendiente* (earring) is common Spain vocabulary — Latin American Spanish more often uses *el arete*. Note that *pendiente* also has an unrelated, very common adjective use (*tengo una tarea pendiente* — "I have a pending task"), which isn't a gender pair at all, just the same word wearing two hats.
</GrammarNote>

### Gender in compound nouns and loanwords
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

Compound nouns built from a verb stem plus a plural noun (*sacar* + *corchos* → corkscrew) are consistently masculine, regardless of the gender either piece would have on its own: *el sacacorchos, el paraguas, el lavaplatos, el rascacielos*. Treat this as a structural rule of the compound pattern itself, not something to derive from the parts.

Loanwords are less predictable and often argued over even by native speakers. Two rough tendencies: a loanword sometimes inherits the gender of its closest native Spanish equivalent (*la red* is feminine, which is part of why *la web* is feminine), while a word with no obvious native counterpart often defaults to masculine (*el email, el póster, el marketing*). Usage can also genuinely vary by region and is still shifting for some recent borrowings — treat any single source's claim about a very new loanword's gender as a snapshot, not a permanent rule.

## Bringing It Together

Faced with an unfamiliar noun, work through it in this order: first, does it name a person or animal whose sex you know — if so, gender usually follows sex. If not, check the ending — -o and -a first, then the reliable consonant clusters (-ción, -dad, -aje, -or). Only after that should you suspect an exception, and even then, the -ma family and a short list of individually memorized words (*el día, la mano, el agua*) cover the overwhelming majority of cases you'll actually run into.

<ExampleSentence es="El programa tiene un problema con el sistema, pero la solución está en la mano." en="The program has a problem with the system, but the solution is at hand." note="A sentence deliberately stacking three -ma masculine nouns against one -mano feminine noun." />
