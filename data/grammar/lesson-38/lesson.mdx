[Chapter 37](../lesson-37) built the present subjunctive's forms; this chapter answers the question that actually matters day to day — when does a sentence reach for one of those forms instead of the plain indicative you've used everywhere else in this book? The short version: subjunctive shows up inside a subordinate clause — one introduced by *que* — whenever the main clause stops presenting that subordinate clause as a plain, settled fact. A wish isn't a fact yet. An emotion is a reaction to a fact, not the fact itself. A recommendation describes what should happen, not what does. Doubt actively unsettles something that looked settled. That single thread — *not asserted as plain reality* — is worth holding onto through everything below, because it's the actual logic behind the categories this chapter teaches, not just a mnemonic to memorize by rote.

## Core Pattern: Two Clauses, One Que, and a Mood Decision

### The basic architecture: que joins two clauses, and the main verb decides the mood
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Most subjunctive sentences share one shape: a main clause with its own subject, the connector *que*, and a subordinate clause with a (usually different) subject whose verb's mood depends entirely on the main clause's verb. Compare a main verb that simply reports a fact against one that expresses a wish:

<ExampleSentence es="Creo que Marta llega mañana." en="I think Marta arrives tomorrow." note="creer que states the subordinate clause as the speaker's straightforward belief — plain indicative, llega." />
<ExampleSentence es="Quiero que Marta llegue mañana." en="I want Marta to arrive tomorrow." note="querer que doesn't claim Marta's arrival is real, only that the speaker wants it — so the verb shifts to subjunctive, llegue." />

Nothing about *Marta* or *mañana* changed between those two sentences — only the main verb did, and that alone flipped the mood of the clause that follows it.

### Same subject vs. different subject: when subjunctive isn't even needed
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The *que* + subjunctive structure only appears when the subordinate clause has its **own subject**, different from the main clause's. When both clauses would share the same subject, Spanish drops *que* entirely and uses a plain infinitive instead:

<ExampleSentence es="Quiero comer." en="I want to eat." note="One subject (yo) doing both the wanting and the eating — infinitive, no que, no subjunctive at all." />
<ExampleSentence es="Quiero que comas." en="I want you to eat." note="Two different subjects (yo wants, tú eats) — que plus subjunctive is required." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This is a genuine trap for English speakers, because English uses an infinitive-shaped construction ("I want you to eat") even when the subject changes — something Spanish never allows. *Quiero que tú comer* is simply wrong; a change of subject always forces *que* plus a conjugated subjunctive verb. The full infinitive-vs-subjunctive picture, including a few verbs that allow both patterns, belongs to [Chapter 42](../lesson-42).
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: The WEIRDO Categories

**WEIRDO** — Wishes, Emotion, Impersonal expressions, Recommendations, Doubt, Ojalá — is a memory aid, not the reason itself. Each letter names a different situation where the main clause signals that the subordinate clause isn't being asserted as settled fact, which is exactly the mechanism from the section above. Treat the categories below as six worked examples of one idea, not six unrelated rules to memorize separately.

### W — Wishes and Volition
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Verbs of wanting, hoping, needing, and preferring for someone else to do something: *querer, esperar, desear, preferir, necesitar.*

<ExampleSentence es="Espero que tengas un buen viaje." en="I hope you have a good trip." />
<ExampleSentence es="Prefiero que vengas mañana en vez de hoy." en="I'd rather you come tomorrow instead of today." />

### Indirect Commands: Wishing Through a Third Person
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A direct command ([Chapter 36](../lesson-36)) addresses the listener straight on: *¡Ven!* An **indirect command** expresses the same wish about someone who isn't part of the conversation at all, and it's built from exactly the volition logic above with the main clause quietly dropped: *(Quiero) que pase el siguiente.*

<ExampleSentence es="Que pase el siguiente." en="Let the next person come in. / Have the next person come in." note="No main verb is spoken aloud at all — que plus subjunctive alone carries the full force of a wish directed at a third party." />
<ExampleSentence es="Que se mejore." en="Get well soon. (Literally: may he/she get better.)" note="A fixed, everyday expression built the identical way." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is the missing piece Chapter 36 pointed toward: negative commands and *usted/ustedes/nosotros* affirmative commands already use the subjunctive stem because ordering someone *not* to do something, or ordering a group indirectly, is really this same "que + subjunctive" wish structure — just spoken straight to the listener instead of about a third party.
</GrammarNote>

### E — Emotion
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Reactions and feelings about the subordinate clause's content: *alegrarse de que, sentir que, temer que, sorprender que, molestar que* — and the entire gustar family ([Chapter 24](../lesson-24)):

<ExampleSentence es="Me gusta que me escuches." en="I like that you listen to me." note="Exactly the me gusta que construction Chapter 24 flagged forward to this chapter — liking is a reaction to something, not a claim that it's simply true, so it triggers subjunctive like every other emotion verb." />
<ExampleSentence es="Nos alegra que puedas venir." en="We're glad you can come." />
<ExampleSentence es="Me sorprende que no llames más a menudo." en="It surprises me that you don't call more often." />

### I — Impersonal Expressions
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Impersonal *es + adjective/noun + que* constructions that pass judgment, rather than confirm fact: *es necesario que, es importante que, es bueno/malo que, es una lástima que, es posible que.*

<ExampleSentence es="Es importante que estudies para el examen." en="It's important that you study for the exam." />
<ExampleSentence es="Es una lástima que no puedas quedarte más tiempo." en="It's a shame you can't stay longer." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Not every impersonal *es que* expression triggers subjunctive — only the ones that judge or evaluate rather than confirm. *Es cierto que, es verdad que,* and *es obvio que* state something as settled fact, so they keep the indicative: *Es cierto que llueve mucho aquí* ("It's true that it rains a lot here"). This is the same certainty-vs-doubt logic the next section builds on directly.
</GrammarNote>

### R — Recommendations and Requests
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Verbs that push someone toward an action without directly commanding them: *recomendar, sugerir, aconsejar, pedir, exigir, insistir en.*

<ExampleSentence es="El médico recomienda que descanses más." en="The doctor recommends that you rest more." />
<ExampleSentence es="Te pido que me llames esta noche." en="I'm asking you to call me tonight." />

### D — Doubt and Denial: where mood genuinely flips the meaning
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

This is the category where indicative and subjunctive stop being a formality and start actually changing what a sentence claims. *Creer, pensar,* and *saber* used affirmatively assert the subordinate clause as fact — indicative. Negate them, or use a verb of doubt or denial outright (*dudar que, negar que*), and the clause is no longer asserted as true — subjunctive follows automatically:

<ExampleSentence es="Creo que tiene razón." en="I think she's right." note="A plain, positive belief, presented as the speaker's own fact — indicative, tiene." />
<ExampleSentence es="No creo que tenga razón." en="I don't think she's right." note="Negating creer flips the clause from asserted to doubted. Nothing else in the sentence changed — only the mood did." />
<ExampleSentence es="Dudo que ellos sepan la verdad." en="I doubt they know the truth." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*No dudar que* ("to not doubt that...") flips back to indicative, since removing the doubt restores the clause to asserted fact: *No dudo que tiene razón* ("I don't doubt that she's right"). Track what the main clause is actually claiming about the subordinate clause's truth — certain, and it's indicative; anything less than certain, and it's subjunctive — rather than memorizing individual verbs as fixed "subjunctive verbs" or "indicative verbs."
</GrammarNote>

### O — Ojalá
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Ojalá (que)* — from Arabic, literally something close to "God willing" — is always followed by the subjunctive, with or without *que*, and never changes form for person, since it isn't really a conjugated verb at all:

<ExampleSentence es="Ojalá que no llueva mañana." en="I hope it doesn't rain tomorrow." />
<ExampleSentence es="Ojalá tengas razón." en="I hope you're right." note="Que is entirely optional here — both versions are equally correct and common." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Adjective clauses: when the antecedent is unknown, hypothetical, or doesn't exist
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Everything above has been a **noun clause** — the *que*-clause functions as the object of the main verb. Subjunctive also governs **adjective clauses**, the *que*-clauses [Chapter 17](../lesson-17) covered as relative-pronoun descriptions of a noun — but here the trigger isn't the main verb's meaning, it's whether the noun being described (the antecedent) is a known, specific thing or an unknown, hypothetical, or nonexistent one:

<ExampleSentence es="Tengo un empleado que habla inglés." en="I have an employee who speaks English." note="A specific, known person — indicative, habla." />
<ExampleSentence es="Busco un empleado que hable inglés." en="I'm looking for an employee who speaks English." note="No particular person is identified yet — any qualifying candidate would do — so the verb inside the clause shifts to subjunctive, hable." />
<ExampleSentence es="No hay nadie que sepa la respuesta." en="There's no one who knows the answer." note="The antecedent doesn't exist at all — the strongest version of this same pattern." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A quick test: can you point to the exact person or thing the clause describes? If yes, indicative. If the clause is describing a *type* of thing you haven't found yet — or that may not exist — subjunctive.
</GrammarNote>

### Adverbial clauses: a quick preview
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Subjunctive also reaches into **adverbial clauses** — clauses that function like an adverb, answering "when," "why," or "under what condition" the main clause happens. A full reference belongs to [Chapter 52](../lesson-52); this chapter only previews the concept. Two patterns worth knowing now:

- **Always subjunctive, no exceptions**: *para que* ("so that") and a handful of other purpose conjunctions always take subjunctive, because a purpose is by definition not yet a realized fact — *Te lo explico para que entiendas* ("I'm explaining it so that you understand").
- **Depends on known-fact vs. not-yet-happened**: time conjunctions like *cuando, en cuanto, tan pronto como* take indicative for habitual or already-completed events, but subjunctive when the clause refers to the future — *Cuando llego a casa, ceno* (indicative, a routine fact) vs. *Cuando llegue a casa, cenaré* (subjunctive, a future event that hasn't happened yet). *Aunque* ("although/even if") works on the same known-fact-vs-hypothetical logic.

<ExampleSentence es="Llámame cuando tengas un momento." en="Call me when you have a moment." note="tengas is subjunctive — the moment hasn't happened yet from the speaker's point of view." />

### Common learner errors: why English intuition doesn't help here
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

English marks almost none of this with a distinct verb form — "I want you to come," "I hope you have fun," and "I think you're right" all use ordinary-looking verb shapes, with no audible signal that Spanish would treat these very differently. The result is a predictable error: an English-speaking learner defaults to the indicative (or an infinitive) exactly where Spanish requires subjunctive, especially right after a change of subject.

<ExampleSentence es="Quiero que vengas, no quiero que vienes." en="I want you to come." note="Vienes (indicative) is the error a direct word-for-word translation from English produces; vengas (subjunctive) is correct, since querer que is a Wishes trigger with a changed subject." />

## Bringing It Together: Subjunctive vs. Indicative — The Big Picture

{/* Required closing synthesis section, per the chapter's design — reaches forward to imperfect subjunctive (Ch. 39) and si-clauses (Ch. 41) without assuming the reader has read either yet, consistent with every chapter's self-containment rule. */}

Run through these questions, in order, for any *que*-introduced clause you're building:

1. **Is the subordinate clause simply being asserted as settled fact** — a plain statement with *creer, saber, decir,* or a certainty expression like *es cierto que*? → **Indicative.**
2. **Does the main clause express a wish, emotion, impersonal judgment, recommendation, doubt, or ojalá** about the subordinate clause — the WEIRDO categories above? → **Subjunctive**, built the way [Chapter 37](../lesson-37) taught.
3. **Is this a description of a noun (an adjective clause), and is that noun's identity unknown, hypothetical, or nonexistent** — *busco un empleado que...*, *no hay nadie que...*? → **Subjunctive.**
4. **Is the connector a subordinating conjunction that always requires subjunctive** (*para que*), **or one whose mood depends on known-fact vs. not-yet-real** (*cuando, aunque*)? → [Chapter 52](../lesson-52) has the complete reference; this chapter only previewed the idea.
5. **Is the whole sentence set in the past instead of the present** — "I wanted her to call," "I doubted he had arrived"? → The same trigger logic above still decides indicative vs. subjunctive, but the subjunctive verb form itself shifts to the past: the imperfect subjunctive ([Chapter 39](../lesson-39)) or a compound subjunctive ([Chapter 40](../lesson-40)).
6. **Is the sentence a hypothetical built around "if"** — real and possible, present-hypothetical, past-hypothetical, or mixed? → Conditional sentences have their own dedicated tense-pairing logic, in [Chapter 41](../lesson-41).

The acronym gets you in the door, but this decision tree — what is the main clause actually doing to the clause that follows it? — is the transferable skill. Once it's automatic, you'll find yourself choosing the right mood before you've consciously thought about which WEIRDO letter applies at all.
