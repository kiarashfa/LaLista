A direct object is the thing or person directly receiving a verb's action — what you see, buy, call, or read. English replaces a known direct object with a short pronoun that sits right where the noun was ("I bought the book" → "I bought it"). Spanish does the same job with an even shorter word, but that word doesn't stay put after the verb — it jumps to a specific position that depends on the shape of the surrounding verb. Getting that position right is the real content of this chapter, and it's a foundation the next several chapters build on directly: indirect object pronouns ([Chapter 13](../lesson-13)) follow the identical placement rules, and combining the two ([Chapter 14](../lesson-14)) only works once both are second nature on their own.

## Core Pattern: The Direct Object Pronoun Set & Basic Placement

### The six direct object pronoun forms
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Direct object pronouns fill the same six-way grid as subject pronouns ([Chapter 11](../lesson-11)), but only the third-person forms mark gender:

<BootTable
  label="Direct Object Pronouns"
  forms={{
    "yo": "me",
    "tú": "te",
    "él/ella/usted": "lo / la",
    "nosotros": "nos",
    "vosotros": "os",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "los / las"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="¿Me ves?" en="Can you see me?" />
<ExampleSentence es="Compré el libro y lo leí en un fin de semana." en="I bought the book and read it in a weekend." note="lo stands in for el libro (masculine singular)." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Only *lo/la* and *los/las* — the third-person forms — change for gender, because only the third person needs to distinguish "him/it (m.)" from "her/it (f.)." *Me, te, nos,* and *os* refer to people already in the conversation, so gender marking would be redundant.
</GrammarNote>

### Placement: immediately before a conjugated verb
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

The default position for a direct object pronoun is directly in front of the conjugated verb — the reverse of English, which keeps the object pronoun after the verb:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Veo", role: "verb" },
    { text: "la película.", role: "object" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "La", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "veo.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Compro el pan todos los días. Lo compro en la panadería de la esquina." en="I buy bread every day. I buy it at the corner bakery." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
English intuition misleads here: "I see it" keeps "it" after the verb, so the instinct is to say *"Veo la."* That's ungrammatical in Spanish — the pronoun has to move in front of the conjugated verb: *La veo.*
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Placement with Infinitives, Gerunds & Commands

This is the placement system every other object-pronoun chapter in this book relies on, so it's worth learning thoroughly here rather than piecing it together later.

### Two valid positions with infinitives and gerunds
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

When a conjugated verb is paired with an infinitive or a gerund (as in *querer* + infinitive, or *estar* + gerund for the progressive — [Chapter 25](../lesson-25)), the pronoun has two equally correct homes: attached to the end of the infinitive/gerund, or moved in front of the whole conjugated-verb-plus-infinitive/gerund unit. Both mean exactly the same thing:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Quiero", role: "verb" },
    { text: "ver", role: "verb" },
    { text: "la.", role: "pronoun" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "La", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "quiero", role: "verb" },
    { text: "ver.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Quiero verla esta noche." en="I want to see it/her tonight." note="Written as one fused word, verla — the pronoun physically attaches to the infinitive." />
<ExampleSentence es="La quiero ver esta noche." en="I want to see it/her tonight." note="Identical meaning, pronoun moved before the conjugated verb querer instead." />
<ExampleSentence es="Estoy leyéndolo. / Lo estoy leyendo." en="I'm reading it. / I'm reading it." note="Same two-position rule with a gerund; leyéndolo needs a written accent to keep the stress where it was before lo attached." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Neither position is more "correct" or more formal than the other — this is free stylistic variation, and native speakers use both constantly, sometimes in the same conversation.
</GrammarNote>

### Mandatory attachment: affirmative commands
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

With an affirmative command, there's no choice: the pronoun *must* attach to the end of the verb. With a negative command, it goes back to the normal pre-verb position. The full command paradigm belongs to [Chapter 36, Commands](../lesson-36) — what matters here is the placement contrast itself:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Come", role: "verb" },
    { text: "lo.", role: "pronoun" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "No", role: "connector" },
    { text: "lo", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "comas.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Cómelo antes de que se enfríe." en="Eat it before it gets cold." note="Cómelo — pronoun attached, and a written accent added to keep the stress on cóme-, exactly where it was before lo attached." />
<ExampleSentence es="No lo comas todavía." en="Don't eat it yet." note="Negative command: the pronoun returns to its normal position in front of the verb, no attachment at all." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The accent isn't optional decoration — *comelo* (no accent) and *cómelo* would be pronounced with the stress on different syllables, and only the accented form is correct. The same accent rule applies however many pronouns end up attached, including the double-pronoun commands covered fully in [Chapter 14](../lesson-14) (*dígamelo* — "tell it to me").
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Leísmo: Spain's use of *le* for a masculine personal direct object
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

In large parts of Spain, *le* commonly replaces *lo* specifically when the direct object is a **male person** — a widespread, standard pattern in Castellano called *leísmo*, not an error:

<ExampleSentence es="Le vi ayer en la fiesta." en="I saw him yesterday at the party." note="le here means 'him,' filling the same slot lo would fill — both are considered correct in Spain for a masculine personal direct object." />
<ExampleSentence es="Compré el pan y lo comí." en="I bought the bread and ate it." note="Lo, never le, for an inanimate direct object — leísmo is restricted to people, not things." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
This specific pattern — *leísmo de persona* — is common enough across Spain, and accepted enough by the Real Academia Española, that you'll hear it from educated speakers in everyday and even formal contexts. Latin American Spanish, by contrast, consistently uses *lo* for a masculine personal direct object. Neither choice is "more correct" — they're two standard regional patterns, and since this book focuses on Castellano, you'll see *le* used this way in later chapters without further comment.
</GrammarNote>

### Laísmo and loísmo: related patterns that aren't standard
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

Leísmo has two cousins that use the same logic in the other direction — swapping a direct object pronoun in for what should grammatically be an indirect object ([Chapter 13](../lesson-13)). Unlike personal leísmo, neither is accepted as standard, even within Spain:

| Phenomenon | What happens | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Leísmo (personal) | *le* used for a masc. personal direct object instead of *lo* | Widespread in Spain; accepted by the RAE |
| Leísmo (general) | *le* used for an inanimate or feminine direct object | Non-standard, even in Spain |
| Laísmo | *la* used for a feminine indirect object instead of *le* | Common in central-Spain speech (e.g. Madrid); considered incorrect |
| Loísmo | *lo/los* used for an indirect object instead of *le/les* | Rare, mostly rural; considered incorrect |

<ExampleSentence es="La dije que llegaría tarde." en="I told her I'd arrive late." note="Laísmo: dije needs an indirect object (told to her), so the standard form is Le dije, not La dije." />

You'll encounter all four labels in dialect discussions of Spanish; only the first is something you should feel free to adopt yourself when writing Castellano-flavored Spanish.

### Direct object pronouns usually replace the noun, not double it
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A direct object pronoun and the full noun it stands for don't normally appear together — you pick one or the other. This is a real point of contrast with indirect object pronouns, which routinely appear *alongside* the noun phrase they refer to (covered in [Chapter 13](../lesson-13)):

<ExampleSentence es="Veo a María en la fiesta." en="I see María at the party." note="Full noun phrase, no pronoun." />
<ExampleSentence es="La veo en la fiesta." en="I see her at the party." note="Pronoun instead of the noun phrase — not both together (Spanish doesn't normally say La veo a María en la fiesta)." />

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Tenía dos entradas para el concierto. Quería dártelas, pero mi hermano me las pidió primero, así que se las di a él." en="I had two tickets for the concert. I wanted to give them to you, but my brother asked me for them first, so I gave them to him." note="A preview of Chapter 14's combined pronouns (te las, me las, se las) — every one of them still obeys this chapter's placement rule, just doubled up." />

Whatever verb shape you're working with, the placement logic reduces to one question: is there a bare conjugated verb, or a conjugated verb plus an infinitive/gerund/affirmative command? A bare conjugated verb takes the pronoun in front, always. An infinitive or gerund gives you a free choice between attaching and moving it forward. An affirmative command requires attachment; its negative twin requires the normal front position instead.
