Some Spanish verbs need a second, smaller pronoun riding alongside the subject — one that points right back at the subject itself. *Me lavo* isn't "I wash," it's "I wash myself"; the action loops back onto the person doing it. Spanish leans on this reflexive pattern far more often than English does, and not always for the reason you'd expect: sometimes it really is a person acting on themselves (*me lavo*), sometimes it means two or more people acting on each other (*nos vemos*), and sometimes the "reflexive" pronoun has drifted so far from literal self-action that the verb barely resembles its non-reflexive twin at all (*irse* — "to leave" — has little to do with *ir*, "to go," acting on itself). This chapter covers the whole system: the pronouns themselves, how they attach to a verb, the several distinct jobs they do, and a genuinely tricky construction — the "no-fault" *se* — that uses this same small pronoun set to describe things that just *happen* to you.

## Core Pattern: The Reflexive Pronoun Set & True Reflexive Meaning

### The six reflexive pronoun forms
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Reflexive pronouns fill the same six-way grid as every other pronoun set in this book. Four of the six are already familiar — they're identical to the direct object pronouns from [Chapter 12](../lesson-12) — but the third-person forms collapse into a single word that never changes for gender or number:

<BootTable
  label="Reflexive Pronouns"
  forms={{
    "yo": "me",
    "tú": "te",
    "él/ella/usted": "se",
    "nosotros": "nos",
    "vosotros": "os",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "se"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Me ducho por la mañana." en="I shower in the morning." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿A qué hora te acuestas?" en="What time do you go to bed?" />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Se* is the one form worth committing to memory as its own thing: it covers *him, her, you (usted), them,* and *you all (ustedes)* — singular and plural, masculine and feminine, all with the same unchanging word. Context and the verb ending tell you which.
</GrammarNote>

### True reflexive meaning: when the subject acts on itself
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

In its most literal use, a reflexive pronoun means the subject and the object of the verb are the same person — whatever you're doing, you're doing it *to yourself*. English usually leaves this invisible ("I wash" already implies "myself" unless you say otherwise); Spanish makes it explicit every time:

<BootTable
  label="lavarse"
  caption="present tense — full reflexive conjugation"
  forms={{
    "yo": "me lavo",
    "tú": "te lavas",
    "él/ella/usted": "se lava",
    "nosotros": "nos lavamos",
    "vosotros": "os laváis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "se lavan"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Me lavo antes de desayunar." en="I wash up before breakfast." />
<ExampleSentence es="Los niños se visten solos." en="The kids get dressed by themselves." note="Vestirse — visto is an e→i stem change, same as vestir without the reflexive pronoun." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Watch the difference between the plain verb and its reflexive twin: *lavar* means "to wash [something else]," *lavarse* means "to wash oneself." *Lavo el coche* (I wash the car) and *Me lavo* (I wash myself) use the exact same verb with completely different objects — the reflexive pronoun is what tells you which one you mean.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Placement, Reciprocal Use, Pronominal Verbs & Tense

### Placement: the same rules as direct object pronouns
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Reflexive pronouns are unstressed clitic pronouns, exactly like the direct object pronouns covered in [Chapter 12](../lesson-12) — and they obey the identical placement logic, so it's worth reviewing that chapter's full treatment rather than re-deriving it here. The short version: in front of a bare conjugated verb, always —

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Me", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "levanto", role: "verb" },
    { text: "a las siete.", role: "other" }
  ]}
/>

— a free choice between attaching and moving forward when a conjugated verb is paired with an infinitive or gerund —

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Voy a", role: "verb" },
    { text: "levantarme", role: "verb" },
    { text: "temprano.", role: "other" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Me", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "voy a levantar", role: "verb" },
    { text: "temprano.", role: "other" }
  ]}
/>

— and mandatory attachment with an affirmative command, reverting to the normal pre-verb position for a negative one:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Levanta", role: "verb" },
    { text: "te.", role: "pronoun" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "No", role: "connector" },
    { text: "te", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "levantes.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Levántate ya, que llegamos tarde." en="Get up already, we're running late." note="Levántate — the accent shifts to keep the stress on the syllable it had before te attached, the same accent rule from Chapter 12." />
<ExampleSentence es="No te levantes todavía." en="Don't get up yet." />

### Reciprocal use: nos vemos, se quieren
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The plural reflexive pronouns — *nos, os, se* — carry a second job: instead of each person acting on themselves, the group acts on *each other*. This only works with a plural subject, since reciprocity needs at least two participants:

<ExampleSentence es="Nos vemos todos los domingos." en="We see each other every Sunday." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mis abuelos se quieren mucho, después de cincuenta años juntos." en="My grandparents love each other very much, after fifty years together." />

Some sentences are genuinely ambiguous between the reflexive and reciprocal reading — *Se miran* could mean "they look at themselves" (each at a mirror) or, far more naturally out of context, "they look at each other." When it matters, Spanish adds a clarifier: *el uno al otro* (*la una a la otra*, *los unos a los otros*...) to nail down reciprocal, or *a sí mismos/mismas* to nail down true reflexive:

<ExampleSentence es="Se ayudan el uno al otro." en="They help each other." note="Removes any ambiguity with the plain se ayudan, which out of context could theoretically mean 'they help themselves.'" />

### Pronominal verbs: reflexive in form, not in meaning
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A large group of Spanish verbs carry a reflexive pronoun that doesn't mean "self" at all anymore — the *se* is just permanently fused to the verb as part of its identity, often shifting the meaning from the plain verb in a specific, learnable way:

| Base verb | Meaning | Pronominal form | Meaning shift |
|---|---|---|---|
| ir | to go | irse | to leave, go away |
| dormir | to sleep | dormirse | to fall asleep |
| poner | to put | ponerse | to put on (clothing); to become (+ adj.) |
| llevar | to carry, wear | llevarse | to take away; to get along (**con**) |
| comer | to eat | comerse | to eat up — an intensified, "finish it off" reading |
| acordar | to agree (on) | acordarse (de) | to remember |

<ExampleSentence es="Voy al parque, pero mi hermano ya se fue." en="I'm going to the park, but my brother already left." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se puso nervioso antes del examen." en="He got nervous before the exam." note="Ponerse + adjective is a very common way to express becoming a state — no equivalent single verb exists in English." />

A second group has no everyday non-reflexive counterpart at all — the *se* isn't optional, it's simply part of how the verb is cited and used:

<ExampleSentence es="Se queja de todo." en="He complains about everything." note="Quejarse — always reflexive; 'quejar' alone isn't used this way." />
<ExampleSentence es="Me di cuenta de mi error demasiado tarde." en="I realized my mistake too late." note="Darse cuenta (de) — 'to realize,' built from dar + cuenta, but only as a reflexive phrase." />
<ExampleSentence es="No se atreve a hablar en público." en="He doesn't dare speak in public." />

Other common members of this inherently-pronominal group: *equivocarse* (to be wrong, to make a mistake), *arrepentirse (de)* (to regret), *sorprenderse (de)* (to be surprised), *alegrarse (de)* (to be glad).

<GrammarNote type="tip">
There's no shortcut for telling which verbs behave this way — treat *quejarse, darse cuenta, atreverse,* and their cousins as vocabulary items you learn *with* their *se* attached, the same way you'd learn a noun together with its article.
</GrammarNote>

### Conjugating reflexive verbs across tenses
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Everything above has used the present tense, but the reflexive pronoun rides along with a verb through every tense the same way — it never changes the conjugation itself, only sits in front of (or attaches to) whatever verb form the tense requires:

| Tense | lavarse (yo) |
|---|---|
| Presente | me lavo |
| Pretérito | me lavé |
| Imperfecto | me lavaba |
| Futuro | me lavaré |
| Presente progresivo | me estoy lavando / estoy lavándome |

<ExampleSentence es="Ayer me desperté tarde y me perdí el autobús." en="Yesterday I woke up late and missed the bus." note="Preterite — a single completed event, both verbs reflexive-in-form (perderse here just means 'to miss' a bus or event)." />
<ExampleSentence es="De pequeño, me bañaba todas las noches antes de dormir." en="As a kid, I used to bathe every night before sleeping." note="Imperfect — a habitual past routine." />

Stem-changing verbs keep their stem change when reflexive, in exactly the pattern from [Chapter 23](../lesson-23) — the reflexive pronoun sits outside the change entirely:

<BootTable
  label="despertarse"
  caption="present tense — e→ie stem change"
  forms={{
    "yo": "me despierto",
    "tú": "te despiertas",
    "él/ella/usted": "se despierta",
    "nosotros": "nos despertamos",
    "vosotros": "os despertáis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "se despiertan"
  }}
/>

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Notice the boot shape above is identical to any other stem-changing verb — *nosotros* and *vosotros* keep the unstressed *e*, while every other form stresses and breaks it into *ie*. The reflexive pronoun in front changes nothing about where the stem change does or doesn't happen.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Reflexive pronouns instead of possessives: body parts and clothing
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

This is the pattern [Chapter 1](../lesson-01) and [Chapter 7](../lesson-07) both pointed forward to, and it deserves the full explanation now. When you act on your own body part or clothing, Spanish almost always uses a reflexive verb plus a plain definite article — *never* a possessive:

<ExampleSentence es="Me lavo las manos antes de comer." en="I wash my hands before eating." note="Not lavo mis manos." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se puso la chaqueta y salió corriendo." en="She put on her jacket and ran out." note="Not su chaqueta." />
<ExampleSentence es="Nos cepillamos los dientes dos veces al día." en="We brush our teeth twice a day." />

Here's the mechanism, spelled out: a possessive's whole job is to answer "whose is it?" In *me lavo las manos*, that question is already answered before the possessive would even get a chance to — the reflexive pronoun *me* has already told you the hands belong to the subject, because *me* is grammatically both "myself" (the person the washing happens to) and, by ordinary real-world logic, the owner of whatever body part gets washed. Adding a possessive on top (*lavo mis manos*) doesn't add information; it just says "my" twice — once through the reflexive pronoun's built-in logic, once through *mis*. Spanish treats that as redundant in a way English doesn't, because English's "wash" carries no built-in reflexive marker to do that work first.

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This is exactly the trap flagged back in Chapter 7: English *requires* "my hands," "her jacket" — there's no other way to say it — so the instinct to reach for *mis manos* or *su chaqueta* is strong and wrong. Once a reflexive pronoun has already identified the owner, stacking a possessive on top sounds like unnecessary, slightly childish over-explanation to a native ear.
</GrammarNote>

The same logic extends past strict reflexives, to any construction where an indirect object pronoun (not necessarily reflexive) already identifies whose body part or clothing is involved — the article still does the possessive's job:

<ExampleSentence es="La enfermera le lava las manos al paciente." en="The nurse washes the patient's hands." note="Le (IO pronoun, not reflexive — see Chapter 13) identifies whose hands; las manos still takes the plain article, not su." />

This construction requires an action verb that plausibly targets a body part or clothing item — *lavarse, ponerse, quitarse, cepillarse, cortarse, romperse, secarse* and similar are the verbs you'll see it with constantly. It isn't a rule about reflexive verbs in general, only about this specific "acting on your own body/clothes" meaning.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A related, narrower pattern shows up even outside reflexive verbs: when a plural subject each has exactly one of something, Spanish often keeps the noun singular — *Los estudiantes levantaron la mano* (the students raised their hand — each one hand), not *las manos*. It's the same underlying comfort with letting context carry ownership instead of stacking on an explicit marker, just without a reflexive pronoun doing the identifying.
</GrammarNote>

### The no-fault se: unplanned and accidental events
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Spanish has one more job for *se* — arguably the trickiest one — used to describe things that happen *to* you rather than things you *do*: dropping, forgetting, breaking, running out of. Native speakers call this construction useful precisely because it removes blame; grammarians sometimes call it the "dative of unplanned occurrence." Compare:

<ExampleSentence es="Rompí el vaso." en="I broke the glass." note="Direct, active, arguably an admission of fault — yo is the subject." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se me rompió el vaso." en="The glass broke on me / I accidentally broke the glass." note="No-fault se — el vaso is now the grammatical subject, and me marks who it happened to." />

The structure is always the same four pieces, in this order: **se** + **indirect object pronoun** (who it happened to) + **verb, agreeing with whatever follows** + **the thing that underwent the action**. That last piece — not the person — is the grammatical subject, which is exactly what makes this construction feel inverted from English:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Se", role: "other" },
    { text: "me", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "olvidaron", role: "verb" },
    { text: "las llaves.", role: "subject" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Se me olvidó la cita." en="I forgot the appointment." note="Olvidó — singular, agreeing with la cita." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se me olvidaron las llaves." en="I forgot the keys." note="Olvidaron — plural, agreeing with las llaves, not with yo." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se nos acabó el tiempo." en="We ran out of time." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se te cayeron los papeles." en="You dropped the papers." note="Literally 'the papers fell on you' — cayeron agrees with los papeles." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se le ocurrió una idea." en="An idea occurred to her — she thought of an idea." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The single most common learner error here is conjugating the verb for the *person* instead of the *thing* — writing *se me olvidé* (wrong) instead of *se me olvidó* (correct). The indirect object pronoun *me* tempts you to think "I" is the grammatical subject, but it isn't: the forgotten, broken, or dropped thing is, every time, and the verb has to agree with it.
</GrammarNote>

Other verbs that regularly appear in this pattern: *romper* (to break), *caer* (to drop/fall), *perder* (to lose), *acabar* (to run out of), *quedar* (to have left, to be remaining), *quemar* (to burn), *ocurrir* (to occur to someone). It works across tenses like any other construction — *se me había olvidado* (I had forgotten), *se me va a olvidar* (I'm going to forget) — always keeping the same se + IO + agreeing-verb shape.

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Todos los días me despierto a las siete, me ducho y me visto rápido. Mi hermana y yo nos ayudamos por las mañanas — yo le hago el café mientras ella se peina. Ayer, sin embargo, se nos hizo tarde: a mí se me olvidó poner el despertador, y cuando por fin nos dimos cuenta de la hora, ya casi habíamos perdido el autobús." en="Every day I wake up at seven, shower, and get dressed quickly. My sister and I help each other in the mornings — I make her coffee while she does her hair. Yesterday, though, we ran late: I forgot to set the alarm, and by the time we finally realized what time it was, we had almost missed the bus." note="True reflexive (me despierto, me ducho, me visto), reciprocal (nos ayudamos), pronominal verbs (darse cuenta → nos dimos cuenta), and the no-fault se (se nos hizo tarde, se me olvidó), all in one everyday passage." />

Whatever form you're looking at, the same question sorts it: is the subject genuinely acting on itself (true reflexive), on each other (reciprocal), is the *se* just permanently fused to this particular verb's meaning (pronominal), or has the person been quietly demoted to an indirect object while the real subject is the thing that got forgotten, dropped, or broken (no-fault *se*)? All four use the exact same small set of pronouns and the exact same placement rules — only the surrounding logic changes.
