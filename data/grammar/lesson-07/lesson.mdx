Every language marks who something belongs to, but Spanish does it with two full, parallel systems rather than one: a short set of forms that sit before the noun (*mi casa*) and a longer set that sit after it or stand alone (*una casa mía*, *esta casa es mía*). Both agree with the thing owned, not the owner — a rule that trips up English speakers constantly, since English possessives (*his, her*) track the owner's gender instead. This chapter builds the short forms first, then the long forms, then works through the one genuinely ambiguous corner of the system: *su* and *suyo*, which can mean *his*, *her*, *your* (formal), or *their* with nothing in the word itself to tell you which.

## Core Pattern: Short-Form Possessives

### Mi, tu, su... and what they agree with
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Short-form possessives sit directly before the noun and only mark number, except for *nuestro* and *vuestro*, which mark gender too:

| Possessor | Singular possession | Plural possession |
|---|---|---|
| yo | mi | mis |
| tú | tu | tus |
| él/ella/usted | su | sus |
| nosotros | nuestro/nuestra | nuestros/nuestras |
| vosotros | vuestro/vuestra | vuestros/vuestras |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | su | sus |

<ExampleSentence es="Mi casa es pequeña, pero nuestra casa de verano es grande." en="My house is small, but our summer house is big." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mis padres viven en Toledo." en="My parents live in Toledo." note="Mis agrees with padres (plural) — not with yo, which is singular." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This is where English intuition misleads: English *his/her* tracks the *owner's* gender, but Spanish *su* doesn't change at all for the owner's gender — *su libro* is correct whether the owner is male, female, or *usted*. Every Spanish possessive agrees with what's owned, never with who owns it.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Long-Form Possessives

Short-form possessives are historically shortened (apocopated) versions of a fuller set of words. Those full forms — the long-form possessives — didn't disappear; they simply moved to a different position in the sentence and picked up a fuller agreement pattern along the way.

Each of these further inflects for the gender and number of the thing possessed, just like any adjective:

| | Masc. sing. | Fem. sing. | Masc. pl. | Fem. pl. |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| mine | mío | mía | míos | mías |
| ours | nuestro | nuestra | nuestros | nuestras |

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Look closely at the boot outline above: *mío, tuyo,* and *suyo* form a genuine irregular cluster against *nuestro/vuestro* — and there's a real reason. *Mi, tu,* and *su* are historically shortened forms of *mío, tuyo,* and *suyo* used before a noun. *Nuestro* and *vuestro* were never shortened — there's no separate "short *nuestro*." The same word does double duty before the noun (*nuestra casa*) and after it or standing alone (*esta casa es nuestra*), which is exactly why they look structurally different from the *mío/tuyo/suyo* family in the table above.
</GrammarNote>

### Where long-form possessives are used
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Long forms appear after *ser* (stating whose something is), after a noun without an article for emphasis, and in a handful of fixed exclamations:

<ExampleSentence es="Esta chaqueta es mía." en="This jacket is mine." />
<ExampleSentence es="Es un amigo mío." en="He's a friend of mine." note="No article — un amigo mío, not un mi amigo." />
<ExampleSentence es="¡Dios mío!" en="My God!" />

### Agreement recap: possessor vs. possessed
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The core rule from the short forms carries over unchanged: every possessive, short or long, agrees with the thing possessed, never the possessor. The long forms just have a fuller paradigm to do it with — four shapes (*mío/mía/míos/mías*) instead of two (*mi/mis*).

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### The ambiguity of su and suyo
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Su/sus* and *suyo/suya/suyos/suyas* cover four different possessors — *his, her, your* (formal *usted/ustedes*), and *their* — with a single form:

<ExampleSentence es="Es su libro." en="It's his/her/your (formal)/their book." note="Context alone decides which — nothing in su itself narrows it down." />

Context resolves this most of the time, but not always — and when it doesn't, Spanish has a fix.

### Disambiguating with de él, de ella, de usted...
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Replace *su/suyo* with the definite article plus the noun plus **de** + a prepositional pronoun:

<ExampleSentence es="Es el libro de ella." en="It's her book." />
<ExampleSentence es="Es el libro de usted." en="It's your book (formal)." />
<ExampleSentence es="Es el libro de ellos." en="It's their book." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Use this construction only when context genuinely leaves ownership unclear — reaching for *de él/de ella* on every single possessive reads as unnaturally over-explicit. Native speakers lean on *su* and let context do the work most of the time. The full *mí/ti/él...* pronoun set used after *de* is covered in [Chapter 16, Prepositional Pronouns](../lesson-16).
</GrammarNote>

### Possessives and body parts/clothing: the reflexive-article pattern
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

With body parts and clothing, Spanish typically skips the possessive altogether and uses a reflexive pronoun with a plain definite article instead:

<ExampleSentence es="Me lavo las manos antes de comer." en="I wash my hands before eating." note="Not lavo mis manos — the reflexive me already establishes whose hands they are." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se puso la chaqueta y salió." en="She put on her jacket and left." note="Not su chaqueta — se already identifies the owner." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This is a genuine English-intuition trap: English requires "my hands," "her jacket," but stacking a possessive on top of an already-reflexive verb sounds redundant and unnatural in Spanish. The reflexive pronoun is doing the possessive's job. This pattern really belongs to reflexive-verb grammar wearing a possessive-shaped hat — see [Chapter 15, Reflexive Pronouns & Reflexive Verbs](../lesson-15) for the full mechanics of how and when the reflexive pronoun carries this meaning.
</GrammarNote>
