The present tense is the first verb form most learners lean on for everything — describing routines, stating facts, even talking about tomorrow — and it's also the cleanest possible place to see [Chapter 20](../lesson-20)'s stem-and-ending logic in action. This chapter builds the complete regular present-tense paradigm for all three conjugation classes and walks through the several jobs that single tense does in real Spanish. Every verb here is fully regular — no stem changes, no irregular *yo* forms, nothing that breaks the pattern once you know it. Irregular verbs get their own full chapter next; this one is deliberately the clean, predictable first stop.

## Core Pattern: The -AR Present Tense

### Hablar: the model -AR verb
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

<BootTable
  label="hablar"
  caption="present tense"
  forms={{
    "yo": "hablo",
    "tú": "hablas",
    "él/ella/usted": "habla",
    "nosotros": "hablamos",
    "vosotros": "habláis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "hablan"
  }}
/>

Six endings — *-o, -as, -a, -amos, -áis, -an* — attach to the stem *habl-* and nothing else changes. Notice there's no boot outline drawn around this table: that's expected, not a bug. [Chapter 20](../lesson-20) explained why the boot shape exists at all — it traces genuine irregularity — and a fully regular verb like *hablar* simply doesn't have any to trace.

<ExampleSentence es="Hablo español e inglés." en="I speak Spanish and English." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Hablas con tu familia todos los días?" en="Do you talk to your family every day?" />

Neither table shows a boot outline either — both verbs are completely regular, and the same absence of an outline is exactly what you should expect to see for any regular verb from this point forward.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
As [Chapter 20](../lesson-20) previewed, -ER and -IR share almost every present-tense ending — compare *comes/come/comen* to *vives/vive/viven*, identical patterns — and only pull apart in the *nosotros/vosotros* forms: *comemos/coméis* versus *vivimos/vivís*. That single divergence is the entire present-tense difference between the two classes.
</GrammarNote>

<ExampleSentence es="Como fruta todas las mañanas." en="I eat fruit every morning." />
<ExampleSentence es="Vivimos cerca de la playa." en="We live near the beach." />

## Nuance & Contrast: One Tense, Several Jobs

### Habitual actions and general truths
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

The most basic job of the present tense is exactly what its name suggests: routines and facts that hold true in general, not tied to this specific moment.

<ExampleSentence es="Estudio en la biblioteca los sábados." en="I study at the library on Saturdays." note="Habitual — a regular routine, not something happening right now." />
<ExampleSentence es="La Tierra gira alrededor del sol." en="The Earth revolves around the sun." note="A general truth — always true, not tied to any particular moment." />

### Standing in for the progressive: "what are you doing" vs. "what do you do"
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Spanish's simple present is more flexible than English's. English forces a choice between "what do you do" (habitual) and "what are you doing" (right now) — Spanish's plain present can cover both, with context deciding which reading applies:

<ExampleSentence es="¿Qué preparas?" en="What are you preparing? / What do you prepare?" note="Context decides — Spanish's simple present naturally covers both readings without needing a separate progressive form." />

Spanish does have a true progressive (*estar* + gerund), and it gets used constantly for actions genuinely in progress — full treatment is [Chapter 25](../lesson-25) — but don't assume every English "-ing" automatically needs it. The plain present frequently does the job on its own.

### Near-future colloquial use
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Just as English can say "I leave tomorrow" for a definite plan, Spanish routinely uses the present tense to talk about the near future in casual speech — often with a time word doing the real work of signaling "future":

<ExampleSentence es="Mañana voy al médico." en="Tomorrow I'm going to the doctor." note="Present tense, future meaning — voy is irregular (Chapter 23), but this near-future use applies to any verb, regular or not." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mañana trabajo desde casa." en="Tomorrow I'm working from home." note="The same near-future use with a fully regular verb." />

For a future that needs to sound more explicit or more distant, Spanish has two more dedicated tools: the future simple ([Chapter 33](../lesson-33)) and the *ir a* + infinitive construction ([Chapter 45](../lesson-45)) — but the plain present, as shown here, is often all a casual plan needs.

### Historical present: narrating the past as if it's happening now
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Spanish, like English, can use the present tense to narrate a past event for vividness — a storytelling device rather than a literal claim about when something happened:

<ExampleSentence es="En 1492, Colón llega a América." en="In 1492, Columbus arrives in America." note="A stylistic swap-in for the preterite (Chapter 26), common in narration, history writing, and animated storytelling — English does the same thing ('so this guy walks up to me...')." />

## Bringing It Together

Once you know a verb is regular and which of the three classes it belongs to, the present tense needs nothing beyond the stem and that class's six endings — no exceptions, no memorized irregular forms. The harder part, as this chapter's second half showed, is knowing what job that one tense form is doing in a given sentence: a routine, a general fact, an action genuinely in progress, a near-future plan, or a vivid retelling of the past.

<ExampleSentence es="Vivo en Madrid, trabajo en una oficina y como con mis compañeros todos los días. Mañana como con mi jefe — tenemos una reunión importante." en="I live in Madrid, I work in an office, and I eat with my coworkers every day. Tomorrow I'm eating with my boss — we have an important meeting." note="Habitual present (vivo, trabajo, como) and the same tense doing double duty as near-future (mañana como), all fully regular forms." />
