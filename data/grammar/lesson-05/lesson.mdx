Comparison is one of the first things a learner reaches for past the beginner stage — not just "big" but "bigger than," not just "good" but "the best." Spanish builds comparison out of a small set of moving parts (*más, menos, tan, tanto, que, como*) that recombine in predictable ways across adjectives, nouns, verbs, and even whole clauses, so once the core pattern is solid, most of what looks like new material later in this chapter is really the same handful of pieces doing a new job. The same intensifying instinct — wanting to say something is bigger, smaller, more affectionate, or more sarcastic than the plain word conveys — also drives a related but structurally different system: suffixes like *-ito* and *-azo* that reshape a word's size or tone without any comparison word at all. Both systems are, at bottom, about turning a word's intensity up or down, which is why this chapter treats them together.

## Core Pattern: Comparatives of Inequality

### Más/menos + adjective + que
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

The default way to compare two unequal things wraps **más** (more) or **menos** (less) around the adjective, with **que** introducing what you're comparing against:

<ExampleSentence es="Mi hermano es más alto que yo." en="My brother is taller than I am." />
<ExampleSentence es="Este hotel es menos caro que aquel." en="This hotel is less expensive than that one." note="Same más/menos...que frame — menos simply flips the direction." />

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Mi hermano", role: "subject" },
    { text: "es", role: "verb" },
    { text: "más alto", role: "other" },
    { text: "que", role: "connector" },
    { text: "yo", role: "other" }
  ]}
/>

The same frame works with adverbs (*más rápido*) and nouns (*más dinero, menos tiempo*) — only the word slotted between *más/menos* and *que* changes. Agreement and placement of the noun-comparison side are covered in Building Out below.

## Building Out: Equality and Irregular Comparatives

The core *más/menos...que* pattern handles unequal comparisons. Spanish extends the same logic — a marker word wrapped around the compared item, with a second word introducing the target — to equal comparisons and to a handful of adjectives that skip the *más/menos* frame entirely.

### Comparatives of equality: tan...como
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

To say two things are equally something, replace *más/menos* with **tan** and *que* with **como**:

<ExampleSentence es="Ana es tan simpática como su madre." en="Ana is as nice as her mother." />
<ExampleSentence es="Vosotros coméis tan rápido como nosotros." en="You all eat as fast as we do." />

### Equality with nouns and verbs: tanto...como
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

When the thing being equally compared is a *noun* rather than an adjective or adverb, **tan** becomes **tanto**, and it agrees in gender and number with that noun — *tanto/tanta/tantos/tantas*:

<ExampleSentence es="Tengo tantos primos como tú." en="I have as many cousins as you." />
<ExampleSentence es="No tenemos tanta suerte como ellos." en="We don't have as much luck as they do." />

When comparing *verbs* — how much an action happens rather than how much of a noun there is — **tanto** stays invariable, functioning as an adverb:

<ExampleSentence es="Ella trabaja tanto como estudia." en="She works as much as she studies." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
The test for which *tanto* you need: if it's modifying a noun, it agrees with that noun (*tantos libros*, *tanta paciencia*). If it's modifying a verb — describing how much an action happens — it never changes shape (*trabaja tanto*, *duermen tanto*).
</GrammarNote>

### Irregular comparatives: mejor, peor, mayor, menor
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Four common adjectives skip *más* + adjective entirely and use a single irregular comparative word instead:

| Adjective | Comparative | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| bueno | mejor | better |
| malo | peor | worse |
| grande | mayor | older / greater (age, importance) |
| pequeño | menor | younger / lesser (age, importance) |

<ExampleSentence es="Este restaurante es mejor que el otro." en="This restaurant is better than the other one." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mi hermana mayor vive en Bilbao." en="My older sister lives in Bilbao." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Mayor* and *menor* are reserved mainly for age and abstract magnitude (*mi hermano mayor*, *un problema menor*). For physical size, Spanish still uses *más grande* and *más pequeño*: "Necesitamos una mesa más grande" (a physically bigger table), not "una mesa mayor."
</GrammarNote>

## Superlatives

Superlatives extend the same comparative machinery one step further: instead of comparing two things, you're placing one thing above (or below) an entire group.

### The relative superlative: el/la/los/las (+ noun) + más/menos...de
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Add a definite article in front of the comparative, and swap *que* for **de** to introduce the group being compared against:

<ExampleSentence es="Es la ciudad más bonita de España." en="It's the prettiest city in Spain." />
<ExampleSentence es="Este es el problema menos grave de todos." en="This is the least serious problem of all." />

The irregular comparatives from Building Out become superlatives the same way, just without a second *más*:

<ExampleSentence es="Es el mejor restaurante de la ciudad." en="It's the best restaurant in the city." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Because *mejor*, *peor*, *mayor*, and *menor* are already comparative, they never take *más* in front of them. "El más mejor" is a genuine, very common learner error — the correct form is simply *el mejor*.
</GrammarNote>

### The absolute superlative: -ísimo
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A separate superlative doesn't compare against anything at all — it just intensifies an adjective on its own, roughly equivalent to English "extremely." Drop the final vowel and add **-ísimo/-ísima/-ísimos/-ísimas**; consonant-ending adjectives add it directly:

<ExampleSentence es="Esta película es aburridísima." en="This movie is extremely boring." />
<ExampleSentence es="El examen fue facilísimo." en="The exam was extremely easy." />

Adjectives ending in a stressed vowel plus **-n** insert an extra **-c-** before *-ísimo* for pronunciation: *joven* → **jovencísimo**, not *jovenísimo*.

### Spelling changes and suppletive forms
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Some adjectives change their final consonant in writing to preserve the original sound before the *í*:

- **c → qu**: rico → riqu**í**simo
- **g → gu**: largo → largu**í**simo
- **z → c**: feliz → feli**c**ísimo

A few adjectives have irregular or Latin-derived absolute superlatives instead of a regular *-ísimo* form: *antiguo* → **antiquísimo** (though *antiguísimo* also occurs informally). Separately, four very common adjectives have historical Latin superlatives — **óptimo** (bueno), **pésimo** (malo), **máximo** (grande), **mínimo** (pequeño) — that have drifted into functioning as independent adjectives with their own specialized meanings (*velocidad máxima*, *salario mínimo*) rather than as everyday intensifiers. *Pésimo* is genuinely common in casual speech ("la comida estaba pésima"); *óptimo* stays fairly formal/technical.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
In everyday speech, *buenísimo* and *malísimo* are far more common than *óptimo* and *pésimo* for simply saying something is "really good" or "really bad." Reach for the Latin-derived forms when the register is more formal or technical.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance & Extension: From Phrase to Clause

Everything so far compares one adjective, adverb, noun, or verb against another single item. The same underlying logic — a marker plus a target — extends naturally to comparing against a whole clause or an implied idea, though a few of the connecting pieces change shape when they do.

### Pronouns after "que": que yo, not que mí
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

When the comparison target is a pronoun, Spanish uses **subject pronouns** (*yo, tú, él...*), not the prepositional forms (*mí, ti...*) you'd expect after most prepositions:

<ExampleSentence es="Corre más rápido que yo." en="He runs faster than I do." note="Not 'que mí' — que is not acting as a true preposition here." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This is a real trap for English speakers, who reach for "than me" and are tempted to translate literally into a prepositional form. Spanish comparisons keep the subject pronoun because *que* introduces an elliptical clause ("que yo [corro]"), not a prepositional phrase — contrast this with true prepositions like *para* or *sin*, covered in [Chapter 16, Prepositional Pronouns](../lesson-16), which do take *mí/ti*.
</GrammarNote>

### Comparing to a clause: más/menos de lo que
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

When you're comparing an adjective's degree to an idea or expectation rather than to another noun, *que* becomes **de lo que**:

<ExampleSentence es="Es más difícil de lo que pensaba." en="It's more difficult than I thought." note="Compare this to 'más difícil que el otro examen,' which compares two nouns directly." />

The rule of thumb: *que* compares two things; *de lo que* compares a quality to a clause.

### Comparisons with numbers: más de vs. no...más que
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Before a number, Spanish uses **de**, not *que*, even in an affirmative sentence:

<ExampleSentence es="Hay más de cien invitados en la boda." en="There are more than a hundred guests at the wedding." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
In a *negative* sentence, "no...más que" flips meaning entirely — it becomes an idiom for **only**, not "not more than": *"No tengo más que diez euros"* means "I only have ten euros," while *"No tengo más de diez euros"* means "I don't have more than ten euros" (I could still have fewer). The two are easy to mix up and genuinely mean different things.
</GrammarNote>

### Correlative comparatives: cuanto más..., más...
<LevelBadge level="B2+" />

Spanish has its own version of English "the more..., the more...," built from **cuanto más/menos** in the first clause and **más/menos** (optionally with *tanto*) in the second:

<ExampleSentence es="Cuanto más practicas, mejor hablas." en="The more you practice, the better you speak." note="The tanto that traditionally opens the second clause (tanto más) is usually dropped in modern speech." />

### Idiomatic intensifiers
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

Colloquial Spanish has several intensifiers that sit outside the *más/-ísimo* system entirely. **Súper** attaches directly to an adjective (*súper simpático*) across the Spanish-speaking world. Two are more distinctly Castellano:

<ExampleSentence es="Está la mar de contenta con su nuevo trabajo." en="She's really pleased with her new job." note="La mar de + adjective is a colloquial Spain intensifier, less common in Latin American Spanish." />
<ExampleSentence es="Es, con diferencia, el mejor equipo de la liga." en="It's by far the best team in the league." note="Con diferencia reinforces a superlative that's already been stated." />

## Productive Suffixes: Diminutives, Augmentatives & the Grammar of Intensity

{/* This section runs its own mini Core Pattern → Nuance arc, since per the table of contents this chapter groups three related subtopics (comparatives, clause-level comparison, and productive suffixes) rather than a single linear one. */}

Spanish has a second, entirely different way to intensify a word: attaching a suffix directly to it, with no *más* or *que* in sight. These suffixes shrink or enlarge a noun's literal size, but just as often they shift its *tone* — warmer, ruder, funnier, softer — which is why they belong in the same chapter as *-ísimo*: all of them are the grammar of turning a word's volume up or down. In fact, the *-c-* insertion you just saw in *jovencísimo* shows up again below in exactly the same words, for exactly the same reason.

### Diminutives: -ito/-ita and the phonological variants
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The default diminutive drops a final vowel and adds **-ito/-ita**:

<ExampleSentence es="Vivimos en una casita cerca de la playa." en="We live in a little house near the beach." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mi perrito duerme todo el día." en="My little dog sleeps all day." />

Words ending in **-e**, or in a stressed final syllable, or in **-n**/**-r**, typically insert **-ec-** instead, for the same pronunciation reasons as *jovencísimo* above:

<ExampleSentence es="¿Me pones un cafecito, por favor?" en="Could you get me a little coffee, please?" note="café → cafecito, not cafeíto." />
<ExampleSentence es="Ese es mi amorcito." en="That's my sweetheart." note="amor → amorcito — a very common term of endearment." />

Diminutives aren't limited to nouns — they attach to adjectives and adverbs too: *pequeño* → *pequeñito*, *despacio* → *despacito* ("slowly," made famous well beyond Spanish class by the 2017 song of the same name).

### Regional diminutive alternatives: -illo and -ico
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

**-illo/-illa** is common throughout Spain, with a strong colloquial and southern-Peninsular flavor, and shows the same *c → qu* spelling change seen with *-ísimo*:

<ExampleSentence es="Ese chiquillo no para de correr." en="That little kid never stops running." note="chico → chiquillo." />

Some *-illo* forms have lexicalized into their own distinct words rather than simply meaning "small X": *ventana* (window) → **ventanilla**, which now specifically means a car window or a service/ticket window, not just any small window.

**-ico/-ica** is a real but geographically marked feature — strong in Aragón, Murcia, and parts of Navarra within Spain, and widespread across much of Latin America — rather than the neutral national default:

<GrammarNote type="regional">
*Un momentico* and *un ratico* ("a little moment," "a little while") are everyday speech in the regions above, but a learner aiming for neutral Castellano should default to *-ito* (*un momentito*, *un ratito*) unless deliberately imitating a regional voice.
</GrammarNote>

### Augmentatives: -ón, -ote, -azo
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Augmentatives enlarge instead of shrink, and often carry an attitude along with the size:

<ExampleSentence es="Vive en una casona antigua en el centro." en="She lives in a big old house downtown." note="casa → casona — grand, impressive size, not just 'bigger.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="Mi vecino es un cabezón — nunca cambia de opinión." en="My neighbor is stubborn as a mule — he never changes his mind." note="cabezón, literally 'big-headed,' has drifted into meaning 'stubborn.'" />

**-ote/-ota** leans informal and sometimes coarse: *palabra* (word) → **palabrota**, which has fully lexicalized into "a swear word," not just "a big word." *amigo* → *amigote* ("old buddy," a bit rough around the edges).

**-azo/-aza** is extremely productive in Spain for "a blow or hit with X" — *puerta* → *portazo* (a door slam), *codo* → *codazo* (an elbow jab) — a different job from pure size-intensifying.

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Using *-azo* to mean "an awesome/impressive X" (*un carrazo* = "an awesome car") is common colloquial usage in much of Latin America, but in Castellano *-azo* is heard far more often in its "blow/strike" sense than as a pure intensifier.
</GrammarNote>

### What suffixes are really doing: size, affection, sarcasm, softening
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

The same handful of suffixes cover four genuinely different jobs, and only context tells you which one is in play:

- **Size**: *una casita* (a small house), *un libro grandote* (a hefty book)
- **Affection**: *hijito*, *abuelita* — warmth toward someone you love
- **Sarcasm**: *listillo* (from *listo*, "clever") means "a know-it-all" — said mockingly, not affectionately
- **Softening**: *¿me haces un favorcito?*, *espera un segundito* — making a request feel smaller and less imposing, a common pragmatic strategy explored further in [Chapter 57, Hedging, Emphasis & Pragmatic Particles](../lesson-57)

<ExampleSentence es="Qué listillo te has vuelto." en="What a smart aleck you've become." note="Sarcastic, not affectionate — tone and context carry the difference, not the suffix itself." />

### Castellano regional productivity
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Across the Spanish-speaking world, *-ito* is the closest thing to a universal default and is always a safe choice in standard Castellano. *-illo* is broadly understood but carries a folksier, more colloquial color, especially associated with southern Spain. *-ico* and the pure-intensifier use of *-azo* are the two most clearly regionally marked choices covered here — real, frequent, and worth recognizing, but not what a learner should reach for by default when writing or speaking neutral Spain Spanish.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

Faced with comparing two things, start simple: *más/menos...que* for inequality, *tan...como* for equality — and check first whether the adjective is one of the four irregulars (*mejor, peor, mayor, menor*) that skip *más* entirely. Reach for *-ísimo* when you want to intensify without comparing to anything at all, and reach for a diminutive or augmentative suffix when what you want to change isn't degree but tone.

<ExampleSentence es="Mi hermano mayor es muchísimo más alto que yo, pero mi hermanito es tan simpático como él." en="My older brother is way taller than I am, but my little brother is just as nice as he is." note="An irregular comparative (mayor), an absolute superlative (muchísimo), a regular comparative (más alto que), a diminutive (hermanito), and a comparative of equality (tan...como) — five patterns from this chapter in one sentence." />
