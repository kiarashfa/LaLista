[Chapter 26](../lesson-26) covered every preterite verb built on a fully predictable stem — the completed-past-action meaning and the regular endings taught there apply to everything in this chapter too, so this chapter doesn't re-teach either one. What it covers instead is the verbs that don't follow that stem pattern at all, and there's no way around the fact that there are a lot of them — worse, they include some of the most common verbs in the language: *tener, hacer, decir, poder, querer, venir, poner, saber, ir, ser, dar, ver.* You will use these constantly, which is exactly why they're irregular in the first place — the most frequent verbs in any language tend to resist regularization over centuries of everyday use. The good news, as with [Chapter 23](../lesson-23)'s present-tense irregulars, is that these aren't a random alphabetical pile: most of them share one unifying trick, and once you see it, you're not memorizing twelve separate paradigms — you're memorizing twelve stems and one pattern.

## Core Pattern: One Irregular Stem, One Shared Ending Set

### The "strong" preterite: a new stem, a new set of endings
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Most irregular preterite verbs replace the infinitive stem with a completely different irregular stem, and then — this is the key insight — attach one shared set of endings that isn't the regular set from Chapter 26 at all:

<BootTable
  label="tener"
  caption="preterite"
  forms={{
    "yo": "tuve",
    "tú": "tuviste",
    "él/ella/usted": "tuvo",
    "nosotros": "tuvimos",
    "vosotros": "tuvisteis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "tuvieron"
  }}
/>

Compare these endings — *-e, -iste, -o, -imos, -isteis, -ieron* — to the regular ones from [Chapter 26](../lesson-26): no accent marks anywhere, yo ends in plain *-e* (not *-í*), and él/ella/usted ends in plain *-o* (not *-ió*). This isn't a coincidence specific to tener — every verb in this family uses this exact same ending set once you've swapped in its irregular stem.

Notice there's no boot outline drawn around this table, for the same reason [Chapter 23](../lesson-23) found no boot around *ir*'s present tense: the boot shape only appears when nosotros/vosotros form a "regular" baseline that the other four forms deviate from. Here, all six forms are built from the same irregular stem *tuv-* — nosotros and vosotros are exactly as irregular as everywhere else in the paradigm, so there's no unaffected shape left to outline. This is the pattern to expect for nearly every verb in this chapter: the irregularity in the preterite's "strong" stems is spread evenly across all six cells, not clustered into four the way a present-tense stem-change is.

<ExampleSentence es="Tuve una reunión muy larga esta mañana." en="I had a very long meeting this morning." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Tuviste tiempo de terminar el informe?" en="Did you have time to finish the report?" />

## Building Out: The Irregular Stem Families

### The u-stem group
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The largest cluster of irregular stems all insert a *u* and take exactly the ending set introduced above:

| Infinitive | Stem | yo | él/ella/usted |
|---|---|---|---|
| tener (to have) | tuv- | tuve | tuvo |
| estar (to be) | estuv- | estuve | estuvo |
| andar (to walk) | anduv- | anduve | anduvo |
| poder (to be able to) | pud- | pude | pudo |
| poner (to put) | pus- | puse | puso |
| saber (to know) | sup- | supe | supo |
| caber (to fit) | cup- | cupe | cupo |

<BootTable
  label="poder"
  caption="preterite"
  forms={{
    "yo": "pude",
    "tú": "pudiste",
    "él/ella/usted": "pudo",
    "nosotros": "pudimos",
    "vosotros": "pudisteis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "pudieron"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="No pude llegar a tiempo." en="I couldn't get there on time." />
<ExampleSentence es="Estuvimos en Barcelona la semana pasada." en="We were in Barcelona last week." />

Two close relatives use the same ending set with an *i* instead of a *u*: *querer → quise, quisiste, quiso, quisimos, quisisteis, quisieron*, and *venir → vine, viniste, vino, vinimos, vinisteis, vinieron.*

<ExampleSentence es="Quisimos ayudar, pero ya era tarde." en="We wanted to help, but it was already too late." />

### The j-stem group: decir, traer, and the -ducir verbs
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A related family swaps in a stem ending in *j* — and that single consonant forces one further tweak: where the regular ending would be *-ieron*, the *i* drops, giving *-eron* instead.

<BootTable
  label="decir"
  caption="preterite"
  forms={{
    "yo": "dije",
    "tú": "dijiste",
    "él/ella/usted": "dijo",
    "nosotros": "dijimos",
    "vosotros": "dijisteis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "dijeron"
  }}
/>

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Dijeron*, not *dijieron* — and the same for every other j-stem verb: *trajeron* (not trajieron), *condujeron* (not condujieron). This is a genuinely common spelling error, since every non-j-stem verb in this chapter does keep the *i* (tuvieron, pudieron, vinieron). The rule is narrow and specific: it's the *j* itself that swallows the following *i*, nowhere else.
</GrammarNote>

*Traer* follows the identical shape: *traje, trajiste, trajo, trajimos, trajisteis, trajeron.* The same j-stem, same -eron ending, extends to every verb ending in *-ducir*: *conducir → conduje, condujiste, condujo, condujimos, condujisteis, condujeron*, and identically for *traducir* (traduje...), *producir* (produje...), and *reducir* (reduje...).

<ExampleSentence es="Le dije la verdad." en="I told him/her the truth." />
<ExampleSentence es="Trajeron comida para todos." en="They brought food for everyone." />
<ExampleSentence es="El taxista nos condujo hasta el aeropuerto." en="The taxi driver drove us to the airport." />

### Hacer's own spelling twist: hice, hiciste, hizo
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Hacer* takes the same *-e, -iste, -o, -imos, -isteis, -ieron* ending set as the u-stem group, with its own one-letter spelling wrinkle in the él/ella/usted form:

<BootTable
  label="hacer"
  caption="preterite"
  forms={{
    "yo": "hice",
    "tú": "hiciste",
    "él/ella/usted": "hizo",
    "nosotros": "hicimos",
    "vosotros": "hicisteis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "hicieron"
  }}
/>

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Hizo*, not "hico" — the same c→z logic [Chapter 23](../lesson-23) showed for *vencer → venzo*: a plain *c* before *o* would harden into a /k/ sound, and *hacer*'s stem needs to keep its soft /θ/ sound, so the spelling swaps to *z*. Every other form (hice, hiciste, hicimos, hicisteis, hicieron) keeps the *c* because it's followed by *i*, which already keeps the sound soft.
</GrammarNote>

<ExampleSentence es="Hice la cena para toda la familia." en="I made dinner for the whole family." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Qué hizo con el dinero?" en="What did he/she do with the money?" />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Ir and ser: identical forms, told apart only by context
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Two completely unrelated verbs — *ir* (to go) and *ser* (to be) — share exactly the same preterite forms. This isn't a coincidence to untangle; it's simply a fact of Spanish you memorize once:

<BootTable
  label="ir / ser"
  caption="preterite — identical for both verbs"
  forms={{
    "yo": "fui",
    "tú": "fuiste",
    "él/ella/usted": "fue",
    "nosotros": "fuimos",
    "vosotros": "fuisteis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "fueron"
  }}
/>

As with *tuve* and *dije* above, every cell here is built from the same irregular stem, so there's no boot outline to draw — this table is simply a plain grid of six forms, all equally irregular.

<ExampleSentence es="Fui al mercado esta mañana." en="I went to the market this morning." note="ir — a completed trip." />
<ExampleSentence es="Fui profesor durante diez años." en="I was a teacher for ten years." note="Exactly the same word, fui, now meaning 'I was' (ser) — only context tells you which verb is at work." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
There's no shortcut for telling *fui* (from ir) apart from *fui* (from ser) beyond reading the rest of the sentence — a direction or destination points to ir, a description or role points to ser. In practice this is rarely ambiguous in context, but it can trip up a learner scanning a sentence word by word.
</GrammarNote>

### Dar and ver: borrowing -er/-ir endings, minus the accents
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Dar* is an -ar verb and *ver* is an -er verb, but neither uses its own regular family's endings in the preterite — both borrow the *-í/-iste/-ió/-imos/-isteis/-ieron* shape instead, and both drop every accent mark that shape would normally carry:

<BootTable
  label="dar"
  caption="preterite"
  forms={{
    "yo": "di",
    "tú": "diste",
    "él/ella/usted": "dio",
    "nosotros": "dimos",
    "vosotros": "disteis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "dieron"
  }}
/>

<BootTable
  label="ver"
  caption="preterite"
  forms={{
    "yo": "vi",
    "tú": "viste",
    "él/ella/usted": "vio",
    "nosotros": "vimos",
    "vosotros": "visteis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "vieron"
  }}
/>

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Compare *vio* here to *comió* from [Chapter 26](../lesson-26) — same ending shape, but *comió* carries a written accent and *vio* never does, because *vio* is only one syllable and a one-syllable word has nothing for an accent mark to distinguish. The same logic explains *di* and *vi*: a regular -ar or -er/-ir verb's yo form would need an accent (hablé, comí), but these are too short to need one.
</GrammarNote>

<ExampleSentence es="Le di el regalo ayer." en="I gave him/her the gift yesterday." />
<ExampleSentence es="Vimos la película el sábado." en="We watched the movie on Saturday." />

### -IR stem-changing verbs: the preterite's own vowel shift
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

[Chapter 23](../lesson-23) taught the present-tense boot pattern for stem-changing verbs, and flagged that -ir stem changers carry a second, related change into the preterite — this is that promised section. Unlike the -ar/-er stem changers from [Chapter 26](../lesson-26), which drop their stem change completely in the preterite, -ir stem changers keep a smaller version of it — but only in the él/ella/usted and ellos/ellas/ustedes forms:

<BootTable
  label="pedir"
  caption="preterite"
  forms={{
    "yo": "pedí",
    "tú": "pediste",
    "él/ella/usted": "pidió",
    "nosotros": "pedimos",
    "vosotros": "pedisteis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "pidieron"
  }}
/>

<BootTable
  label="dormir"
  caption="preterite"
  forms={{
    "yo": "dormí",
    "tú": "dormiste",
    "él/ella/usted": "durmió",
    "nosotros": "dormimos",
    "vosotros": "dormisteis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "durmieron"
  }}
/>

This is a third distinct shape, worth holding apart from the other two you've now seen: it's not the classic four-cell boot from Chapter 23's present tense (yo/tú/él/ellos), and it's not the all-six-cells irregularity of *tuve* or *fui* above — it's just two cells, both third-person, both singular and plural. The regular endings, regular accents, and regular yo/tú/nosotros/vosotros forms from Chapter 26 all still apply; only él/ella/usted and ellos/ellas/ustedes swap in the shifted vowel.

The vowel the form shifts to follows the same present-tense family it came from, one notch simplified: present-tense e→ie becomes preterite e→i, present-tense e→i stays e→i, and present-tense o→ue becomes preterite o→u.

<ExampleSentence es="El camarero nos sirvió la cena." en="The waiter served us dinner." note="servir: e→i, same family as pedir." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mi abuelo murió el año pasado." en="My grandfather died last year." note="morir: o→u, same family as dormir." />

Other common members: *sentir → sintió, sintieron; preferir → prefirió, prefirieron; seguir → siguió, siguieron; repetir → repitió, repitieron; vestir(se) → (se) vistió, vistieron; elegir → eligió, eligieron; corregir → corrigió, corrigieron.*

<GrammarNote type="tip">
The exact same vowel shift resurfaces in the gerund (*pidiendo, durmiendo, sintiendo* — [Chapter 25](../lesson-25)), for the same underlying reason: both forms build on the same shifted third-person-style stem. Learning one makes the other easier to remember.
</GrammarNote>

### A note on overcorrection: don't add what doesn't belong
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Once the regular accent rules from Chapter 26 are second nature, a predictable new mistake appears: adding an accent mark, or a regular ending, to a verb from this chapter that needs neither.

<ExampleSentence es="Tuve que salir temprano, no tuvé." en="I had to leave early." note="tuve is correct — irregular preterite stems never carry a written accent, unlike hablé or comí." />

The fix is the same one that closed this chapter's Core Pattern section: once you've recognized a verb as belonging to one of the irregular stem families above, the shared *-e/-iste/-o/-imos/-isteis/-ieron* (or *-eron* for j-stems) ending set applies exactly as shown, with no accent marks and no exceptions layered back in from the regular pattern.

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Ayer fuimos al cine. Tuve que hacer cola porque no pude comprar las entradas por internet, pero al final las conseguí a tiempo. Mi hermana pidió palomitas, yo no dije nada porque ya había comido, y vimos la película sin problemas." en="Yesterday we went to the movies. I had to wait in line because I couldn't buy the tickets online, but I got them in time in the end. My sister ordered popcorn, I didn't say anything because I'd already eaten, and we watched the movie without any problems." note="fuimos (ir/ser overlap), tuve and pude (u-stem group), pidió (the -ir stem-change vowel shift, third person), dije (j-stem, no -ieron), vimos (dar/ver family, no accent)." />

Faced with a verb you suspect is irregular in the preterite, check it against this chapter's families in order: is it ir or ser (fui...), dar or ver (di/vi...)? Does it belong to one of the u-stem, i-stem, or j-stem families with the shared strong-preterite endings? Is it hacer, with its lone c→z swap? Or is it an -ir verb that already stem-changes in the present — in which case, expect the smaller two-cell vowel shift in the third person only. Anything left over after all of that is simply regular, and [Chapter 26](../lesson-26)'s pattern covers it completely.
