A command tells someone to do something directly — sit down, wait here, don't touch that. English gets by with a single command form no matter who's listening: the bare verb, "Eat!" or "Don't wait!" Spanish can't be that economical, because a command still carries the same *tú/usted/vosotros/ustedes* information every other verb form does ([Chapter 11](../lesson-11)) — so before you can give a single instruction, Spanish makes you decide who you're talking to and how formally. That's why this chapter has five separate command sets instead of one, each both affirmative and negative: a command to a close friend (*tú*) is built differently from one to a stranger (*usted*), a group of friends in Spain (*vosotros*), a formal or plural audience (*ustedes*), or a "let's" suggestion that includes the speaker (*nosotros*). The good news is that most of the machinery is already familiar — how object pronouns attach ([Chapter 12](../lesson-12), [Chapter 14](../lesson-14)), which verb stems go irregular ([Chapter 23](../lesson-23)) — so what's genuinely new here is the command endings themselves, a short closed list of true irregulars, and one honest explanation of why negative commands look so different from affirmative ones.

## Core Pattern: Affirmative *Tú* Commands

### The regular pattern: identical to the *él/ella/usted* present-tense form
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

For any regular verb, the affirmative *tú* command is exactly the *él/ella/usted* form of the present tense you already know from [Chapter 22](../lesson-22) — no separate ending to learn at all:

| Infinitive | Affirmative *tú* command |
|---|---|
| hablar | habla |
| comer | come |
| escribir | escribe |

<ExampleSentence es="Habla más despacio, por favor." en="Speak more slowly, please." />
<ExampleSentence es="Come algo antes de salir." en="Eat something before you leave." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
If you already know a verb's regular present-tense *él/ella/usted* form, you already know its regular affirmative *tú* command — there's nothing new to memorize here. This includes stem-changing verbs: *pierde* (perder), *duerme* (dormir), *pide* (pedir) all work the same way.
</GrammarNote>

### The irregular affirmative *tú* commands: a closed list of eight
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Eight high-frequency verbs break the pattern above completely. This list doesn't grow — memorize it as a fixed set:

| Infinitive | Irregular *tú* command | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| decir | di | tell, say |
| hacer | haz | do, make |
| ir | ve | go |
| poner | pon | put |
| salir | sal | leave |
| tener | ten | have |
| venir | ven | come |
| ser | sé | be |

<ExampleSentence es="Ten paciencia, ya casi llegamos." en="Have patience, we're almost there." />
<ExampleSentence es="Sé tú mismo." en="Be yourself." note="Sé needs its accent to stay distinct from se, the reflexive/se-substitution pronoun (Chapters 14 and 15)." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Four of these are easier to remember as one group: *poner, salir, tener,* and *venir* all just keep the first syllable plus one consonant (*pon-, sal-, ten-, ven-*). The other four — *decir, hacer, ir, ser* — are genuinely one-off and worth drilling individually.
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Ir*'s command *ve* is spelled and pronounced identically to *ver*'s own regular affirmative *tú* command, also *ve* (drop the -s from *ves*). Only context tells them apart: *Ve a casa* ("Go home," from *ir*) versus *Ve la película* ("Watch the movie," from *ver*).
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Affirmative Commands for *Usted*, *Ustedes*, *Nosotros* & *Vosotros*

*Tú*'s affirmative command was its own special system. The other four affirmative persons all extend from one shared mechanism instead — a mechanism worth understanding now, because it resurfaces for every negative command in the next section too.

### *Usted* and *ustedes*: built from the yo-form, not the *él/ella* form
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

To form an affirmative *usted* command, take the verb's **yo-form** of the present tense (the irregular ones from [Chapter 23](../lesson-23) included), drop the final **-o**, and add the "opposite" vowel ending: **-ar** verbs take **-e** (**-en** for *ustedes*); **-er/-ir** verbs take **-a** (**-an** for *ustedes*):

| Infinitive | yo-form | Usted command | Ustedes command |
|---|---|---|---|
| hablar | hablo | hable | hablen |
| comer | como | coma | coman |
| escribir | escribo | escriba | escriban |

Because the stem comes from the yo-form, every irregular yo-form you already know carries straight over:

| Infinitive | yo-form | Usted command |
|---|---|---|
| tener | tengo | tenga |
| poner | pongo | ponga |
| hacer | hago | haga |
| salir | salgo | salga |
| venir | vengo | venga |
| decir | digo | **diga** |

<ExampleSentence es="Diga la verdad, por favor." en="Tell the truth, please." note="This is exactly the diga Chapter 14 built its double-pronoun command example, dígamelo, around — this is where that form comes from." />

A small handful of verbs don't follow even the yo-form-minus-o rule and have to be memorized as outright irregular: *ser → sea, estar → esté, ir → vaya, dar → dé, saber → sepa.* These same five reappear as the classic irregular subjunctive stems in [Chapter 37](../lesson-37).

### *Nosotros*: "let's" commands, and the *vamos a* shortcut
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Nosotros* commands use the identical yo-form-minus-o-plus-opposite-vowel logic, with **-emos/-amos**: *hablemos, comamos, escribamos* ("let's talk," "let's eat," "let's write"). In everyday speech, though, **vamos a + infinitive** is a far more common, more casual substitute for the same meaning:

<ExampleSentence es="¡Comamos ya!" en="Let's eat already!" note="Grammatically correct and used, especially in writing." />
<ExampleSentence es="¡Vamos a comer ya!" en="Let's eat already!" note="The far more common everyday version — the same ir a + infinitive pattern that also gives the future tense (Chapter 33) and periphrases in general (Chapter 45) a casual alternative." />

*Ir* itself has a special case here: the affirmative *nosotros* command "let's go" is almost always **vamos**, not the technically-correct *vayamos* (which sounds stiff or literary). The negative, though, only ever uses *vayamos* — *no vamos* isn't used as a command.

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Don't extend *vamos*'s special affirmative status to the negative. "Let's not go" is *no vayamos*, never *no vamos*.
</GrammarNote>

### *Vosotros*: drop the -r, add -d
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The *vosotros* affirmative command is the most mechanically regular of the five: take the infinitive, drop the final **-r**, add **-d**. This works even for verbs that are irregular in *tú*:

| Infinitive | Vosotros command |
|---|---|
| hablar | hablad |
| comer | comed |
| escribir | escribid |
| tener | tened |
| hacer | haced |
| decir | decid |
| ir | id |

<ExampleSentence es="Escuchad con atención." en="Listen carefully." note="Spain classroom/group register — vosotros commands like this are heard constantly among friends, family, and students in Spain." />
<ExampleSentence es="Tened cuidado en la calle." en="Be careful in the street." note="Tener's tú command is irregular (ten), but its vosotros command is perfectly regular (tened)." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Since Latin American Spanish doesn't use *vosotros* at all, this whole command form is a genuine Castellano marker — Latin American speakers use *ustedes* commands for this same informal-plural situation instead.
</GrammarNote>

### The full affirmative paradigm at a glance
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

| | hablar | comer | escribir |
|---|---|---|---|
| **tú** | habla | come | escribe |
| **usted** | hable | coma | escriba |
| **nosotros** | hablemos | comamos | escribamos |
| **vosotros** | hablad | comed | escribid |
| **ustedes** | hablen | coman | escriban |

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: Negative Commands

### Why negative commands switch forms: the subjunctive connection
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

You've already seen the yo-form-minus-o-plus-opposite-vowel stem in action, for *usted*, *ustedes*, and *nosotros* affirmative commands above. Here's the piece worth pausing on: **every negative command, for every one of the five persons, uses that exact same stem** — including *tú* and *vosotros*, whose affirmative forms don't.

The honest reason is that Spanish doesn't really have a dedicated grammatical form just for "don't do this." Telling someone *to* do something is direct enough that Castellano built special, dedicated forms for it (affirmative *tú*'s irregulars, *vosotros*'s -d). Telling someone *not* to do something shifts the meaning into "I want it to be the case that you don't..." — and that hypothetical, not-yet-real territory is exactly what the subjunctive mood is for elsewhere in Spanish. Negative commands simply reuse the subjunctive's own verb form rather than inventing a separate one. [Chapter 37](../lesson-37) covers exactly how that subjunctive stem is built (it's the same yo-form-minus-o-plus-opposite-vowel rule you just learned here), and [Chapter 38](../lesson-38) covers the full range of situations — including indirect commands like this one — where Spanish reaches for subjunctive over indicative.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Because *usted*, *ustedes*, and *nosotros* affirmative commands already use this subjunctive-style stem, their negative twin is the identical word — only *no* is added. It's *tú* and *vosotros* that need a real second form.
</GrammarNote>

### Negative *tú*: don't reuse the affirmative irregulars
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Because affirmative *tú* has its own special forms — including all eight irregulars — its negative twin can look completely unrelated:

| Infinitive | Affirmative *tú* | Negative *tú* |
|---|---|---|
| hablar | habla | no hables |
| comer | come | no comas |
| decir | di | no digas |
| hacer | haz | no hagas |
| ir | ve | no vayas |
| poner | pon | no pongas |
| salir | sal | no salgas |
| tener | ten | no tengas |
| venir | ven | no vengas |
| ser | sé | no seas |

<ExampleSentence es="No hables tan alto en la biblioteca." en="Don't speak so loudly in the library." />
<ExampleSentence es="No vayas solo por esa calle." en="Don't go down that street alone." note="Ve and no vayas share no resemblance at all — the single biggest trap in this whole chapter." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The most common mistake at this stage is treating the negative as "*no* + the affirmative irregular" — writing *no pon* or *no ve* instead of the correct *no pongas* and *no vayas*. Negative *tú* always comes from the subjunctive-style stem above, never from the affirmative-irregular list.
</GrammarNote>

### Spelling changes that keep the consonant sound intact
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Verbs ending in **-car, -gar,** and **-zar** need the same orthographic adjustment here that they need in the preterite yo-form ([Chapter 26](../lesson-26)), for the same reason: keeping the hard consonant sound before a vowel that would otherwise soften it.

| Ending | Change | Infinitive | Usted command | Negative *tú* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| -car | c → qu | buscar | busque | no busques |
| -gar | g → gu | llegar | llegue | no llegues |
| -zar | z → c | cruzar | cruce | no cruces |

<ExampleSentence es="Busque la salida más cercana." en="Look for the nearest exit." />
<ExampleSentence es="No cruces la calle sin mirar." en="Don't cross the street without looking." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
These two kinds of change can stack: *empezar* is both a stem-changer (*empiezo*) and a -zar verb, so its command forms are *empiece/no empieces* — stem change and spelling change applied together.
</GrammarNote>

### The full negative paradigm at a glance
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

| | hablar | comer | escribir |
|---|---|---|---|
| **tú** | no hables | no comas | no escribas |
| **usted** | no hable | no coma | no escriba |
| **nosotros** | no hablemos | no comamos | no escribamos |
| **vosotros** | no habléis | no comáis | no escribáis |
| **ustedes** | no hablen | no coman | no escriban |

## Pronoun Placement with Commands

[Chapter 12](../lesson-12) and [Chapter 14](../lesson-14) already established the placement rule that governs every verb form a pronoun can attach to: mandatory attachment on an affirmative command, mandatory return to the normal pre-verb position on a negative one, with a written accent added whenever attachment shifts the verb's natural stress. None of that changes here — what's worth working through carefully is how it plays out across all five command persons, and especially what happens with reflexive and double-pronoun commands.

### Affirmative commands: mandatory attachment, same accent rule
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

  parts={[
    { text: "Come", role: "verb" },
    { text: "lo.", role: "pronoun" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Sienta", role: "verb" },
    { text: "te.", role: "pronoun" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Diga", role: "verb" },
    { text: "me", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "lo.", role: "pronoun" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Cómelo antes de que se enfríe." en="Eat it before it gets cold." note="The same accent-preservation rule Chapter 12 introduced: cómelo needs the written accent to keep the stress on cóme-." />
<ExampleSentence es="Siéntate, por favor." en="Sit down, please." note="The reflexive te attaches exactly like a direct object pronoun does — same accent rule, same mandatory attachment." />
<ExampleSentence es="Dígamelo, por favor." en="Tell it to me, please." note="The exact dígamelo Chapter 14 built its double-pronoun-command example around: diga is this chapter's usted command of decir, with me and lo attached in the fixed indirect-before-direct order Chapter 14 taught." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
A pronoun never sits in front of an affirmative command — *Te siéntate* is wrong twice over (redundant, and the pronoun is in the wrong slot). Attachment is mandatory: *Siéntate.*
</GrammarNote>

### Negative commands: pronouns return to the pre-verb position
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "No", role: "connector" },
    { text: "lo", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "comas.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "No", role: "connector" },
    { text: "te", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "sientes.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "No", role: "connector" },
    { text: "me", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "lo", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "diga.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="No lo comas todavía." en="Don't eat it yet." />
<ExampleSentence es="No te sientes ahí, está roto." en="Don't sit there, it's broken." />
<ExampleSentence es="No me lo diga, déjeme adivinar." en="Don't tell me, let me guess." note="Both pronouns stay together, in indirect-before-direct order, right where they'd sit in front of any other conjugated verb." />

### Reflexive commands for *nosotros* and *vosotros*: a letter drops before the pronoun attaches
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Two more mechanical wrinkles, both purely about attachment. When a *nosotros* command ends in **-mos** and a reflexive *nos* attaches, the final **-s** of *-mos* drops before *nos* goes on — and a written accent is added to keep the original stress:

<ExampleSentence es="Sentémonos aquí." en="Let's sit here." note="Sentemos (nosotros command) + nos → drop the final -s → sentémonos, with an accent to preserve the original stress." />
<ExampleSentence es="¡Vámonos ya, que llegamos tarde!" en="Let's go now, we're running late!" note="Even the vamos shortcut follows the same -s-dropping rule: vamos + nos → vámonos." />

The *vosotros* equivalent drops the **-d** instead, before *os* attaches — with one well-known exception:

<ExampleSentence es="Levantaos temprano mañana." en="Get up early tomorrow." note="Levantad (vosotros command) + os → drop the -d → levantaos, no accent needed here since the stress already falls in the right place." />
<ExampleSentence es="Idos ya." en="Go now (all of you)." note="Irse is the one true exception: id keeps its -d before os, giving idos instead of the expected ios." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Despite *idos* being the standard, RAE-prescribed form, many speakers in Spain say *iros* instead in casual speech (borrowing the infinitive *ir* + *os* rather than the true command form). You'll hear both; *idos* is the one to produce yourself in writing or formal contexts.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

The same instruction changes shape depending only on who's listening — everything else about the message stays constant:

<ExampleSentence es="Niño, cómete la verdura." en="Sweetie, eat your vegetables." note="Informal tú, spoken to a child — cómete adds the reflexive te for emphasis (an emphatic use of comerse, previewed in Chapter 15), with the same mandatory attachment and accent rule as any other tú command." />
<ExampleSentence es="Señor, no se preocupe; cierre la puerta cuando salga." en="Sir, don't worry; close the door when you leave." note="Formal usted — no se preocupe negates preocuparse's command form, and cierre is the usted command of the stem-changing cerrar (cierro → cierre)." />
<ExampleSentence es="¡Vámonos ya, que llegamos tarde!" en="Let's go now, we're running late!" note="Nosotros, casual register — the vamos shortcut, reflexive-shaped attachment and all." />

Faced with any command, work through it in order: first, who's being addressed — *tú, usted, nosotros, vosotros,* or *ustedes*? Second, affirmative or negative? If it's affirmative *tú*, check the closed irregular list first, since eight common verbs won't follow the regular pattern. Every other form — every negative, plus affirmative *usted*, *ustedes*, and *nosotros* — comes from the same yo-form-minus-o-plus-opposite-vowel stem, the one [Chapter 37](../lesson-37) will show you is really the subjunctive. Finally, if a pronoun is involved: attach it only if the command is affirmative, watch for the accent, and remember that reflexive *nosotros* and *vosotros* forms shed a letter first.
