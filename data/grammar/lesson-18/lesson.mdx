English asks almost every question with a small, reusable toolkit — "what," "which," "who," "how," "when," "where," "why" — and rarely has to think hard about which one a sentence needs; "what" alone covers ground that Spanish splits across two entirely different words. Spanish's interrogative words also share a rule English speakers never have to think about at all: every single one of them takes a written accent — in a direct question, in an exclamation, and, less obviously, even buried inside a statement that never uses a question mark. This chapter builds the full interrogative and exclamative word set, the accent rule that ties them all together, and the one genuinely hard judgment call English "what" hides from you: knowing when Spanish wants *qué* and when it wants *cuál*.

## Core Pattern: The Interrogative Word Set and the Written Accent

### Meet the interrogative words
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Seven words do almost all the question-asking work in Spanish. All are invariable except for number marking on *quién/quiénes* and *cuál/cuáles* — none of them ever changes for gender:

| Word | Asks about |
|---|---|
| qué | what (definition, identification) |
| cuál(es) | which one(s), what (selection) |
| quién(es) | who |
| cómo | how |
| cuándo | when |
| dónde | where |
| por qué | why |

<ExampleSentence es="¿Qué quieres comer esta noche?" en="What do you want to eat tonight?" />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Quién llamó mientras estaba en la ducha?" en="Who called while I was in the shower?" />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Cómo hacéis vosotros el gazpacho en vuestra casa?" en="How do you all make gazpacho at your house?" note="hacéis — a vosotros example, since this book is built around Castellano." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Strip the accent off most of these words and you get a completely different word doing a completely different job: *que* and *quien* as relative pronouns ([Chapter 17](../lesson-17)), *como* and *cuando* as ordinary connectors meaning "as/like" and "when," *donde* as the relative pronoun of place, *porque* as the conjunction "because." The written accent is the single, reliable signal that a word is asking (or exclaiming) rather than connecting.
</GrammarNote>

### Interrogative words front the sentence
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Whatever an interrogative word is asking about — subject, object, time, place — it jumps all the way to the front of the sentence, ahead of the verb. Spanish frequently drops or repositions the subject to make room, exactly as pro-drop grammar ([Chapter 11](../lesson-11)) would predict:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Tú", role: "subject" },
    { text: "quieres", role: "verb" },
    { text: "una respuesta.", role: "object" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "¿Qué", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "quieres", role: "verb" },
    { text: "(tú)?", role: "subject" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Vas a la fiesta el sábado." en="You're going to the party on Saturday." note="Ordinary statement word order." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Cuándo vas a la fiesta?" en="When are you going to the party?" note="cuándo — asking about el sábado — fronts completely, while tú stays dropped just as it would in the statement version." />

## Building Out: Compounds and Exclamations

Prepositions attach to several of these words to ask more specific questions, and two of them — *qué* and *cómo* — do double duty as the machinery behind Spanish exclamations.

### Adding a preposition: de dónde, adónde, con quién, para qué
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

<ExampleSentence es="¿De dónde eres?" en="Where are you from?" />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Adónde vais este verano?" en="Where are you all headed this summer?" note="adónde — motion 'to where' — echoes the same adónde/donde relationship covered for the relative donde in Chapter 17." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Con quién vas al cine?" en="Who are you going to the movies with?" note="Spanish can't strand con at the end the way casual English strands 'with' — it fronts together with quién, exactly like the relative con quien from Chapter 17." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Para qué sirve esto?" en="What's this for?" />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
One preposition combination is a genuine trap: asking "whose?" never uses *cuyo*. *Cuyo* is a relative pronoun only ([Chapter 17](../lesson-17)) and never appears in a question — "whose is this book?" is built from *de* + *quién*, not from any form of *cuyo*.
</GrammarNote>

<ExampleSentence es="¿De quién es esta chaqueta?" en="Whose jacket is this?" note="Literally 'of whom is this jacket' — de quién, never cuyo, which only works inside a statement as a relative pronoun." />

### Exclamative structures: ¡Qué...! and ¡Cómo...!
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The same accent, and largely the same words, build exclamations instead of questions. *Qué* turns directly into "what a...!" or "how...!" in front of a noun or adjective:

<ExampleSentence es="¡Qué sorpresa!" en="What a surprise!" />
<ExampleSentence es="¡Qué bonito es este pueblo!" en="How pretty this town is!" />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
When *qué* is followed by both a noun and an adjective together, natural Spanish usually inserts *más* or *tan* directly before the adjective — ¡Qué casa más bonita! or ¡Qué casa tan bonita! ("What a beautiful house!") — rather than the bare ¡Qué casa bonita!. Neither word adds a literal "more" or "so" here; they're just part of the fixed exclamative frame.
</GrammarNote>

<ExampleSentence es="¡Qué día tan largo ha sido hoy!" en="What a long day today has been!" />

*Cómo* builds a different kind of exclamation, wrapped around a verb instead of a noun or adjective, and often carries more emotional weight than a plain description would:

<ExampleSentence es="¡Cómo llueve!" en="It's really pouring!" note="Literally 'how it rains' — cómo intensifies the verb itself, not unlike an English 'wow, look at it rain.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="¡Cómo te echo de menos!" en="How I miss you!" />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Indirect questions keep the accent
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The accent rule doesn't switch off just because a sentence loses its question marks. Any time one of these words is still functioning as a question — asking about something unknown, even tucked inside a larger statement — it keeps its accent. This is called an indirect (or embedded) question, and it's a genuine source of writing errors even for confident learners, because nothing about the sentence's punctuation signals it:

<ExampleSentence es="No sé qué quiere." en="I don't know what he wants." note="No question mark anywhere in the sentence, but qué is still functioning as an interrogative — embedding 'what does he want?' — so it keeps its accent." />
<ExampleSentence es="Dime dónde vives." en="Tell me where you live." />
<ExampleSentence es="No tengo ni idea de por qué se fue." en="I have no idea why she left." />
<ExampleSentence es="Depende de cuándo llegues." en="It depends on when you arrive." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Don't confuse an indirect question with a relative clause that also happens to translate with English "what." *Sé lo que quiere* ("I know what he wants") uses **unaccented** *que* — that's the neuter relative pronoun *lo que*, "the thing that he wants," a different construction covered in full in [Chapter 19](../lesson-19). The test isn't the English translation; it's whether the Spanish sentence is genuinely asking something (accent) or pointing back at "the thing that..." (no accent).
</GrammarNote>

Common verbs that regularly introduce an indirect question and are worth recognizing on sight: *saber, preguntar, decir, no tener ni idea de, depender de*.

### Por qué, porque, and porqué: three words that sound alike
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

"Why" (the interrogative) and "because" (the conjunction that answers it) are spelled almost identically in Spanish, which makes this one of the most common accent-and-spacing mix-ups even native speakers make:

| Form | Job |
|---|---|
| por qué | interrogative "why" — two words, accented |
| porque | conjunction "because" — one word, no accent |
| el porqué | noun "the reason" — one word, accented |

<ExampleSentence es="¿Por qué no viniste anoche? — No vine porque tenía fiebre." en="'Why didn't you come last night?' 'I didn't come because I had a fever.'" note="Both words appear in one exchange: por qué asks, porque answers." />
<ExampleSentence es="Nadie entiende el porqué de su decisión." en="Nobody understands the reason behind his decision." note="el porqué functions as an ordinary masculine noun, 'the reason' — always with its article." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A fourth, rarer form — *por que*, two words, no accent — shows up only in formal writing, where *que* is a relative pronoun governed by the preposition *por* (essentially a compressed *por el que*). You're unlikely to need to produce it yourself; recognizing it is enough.
</GrammarNote>

### Qué vs. Cuál: the choice English doesn't make
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

English "what" covers two Spanish jobs that don't overlap, and picking the wrong one is one of the most common, most persistent errors an English-speaking learner makes — often long after the rest of their Spanish has become fluent.

Reach for *qué* when you're asking for a definition, explanation, or identification of something, with no assumed set of options — "what is this thing":

<ExampleSentence es="¿Qué es esto?" en="What is this?" note="Asking to identify or define an unfamiliar object — there's no implied list of candidates to pick from." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Qué es la democracia?" en="What is democracy?" note="A genuine definition question." />

Reach for *cuál/cuáles* when you're asking to select from a set of possibilities — even an open-ended, unstated set, as long as the real question is "this one, not some other one":

<ExampleSentence es="¿Cuál es tu nombre?" en="What's your name?" note="Grammatically a selection question — of every possible name, which one is yours — even though English 'what's your name' gives no hint of that logic at all." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Cuál prefieres, el azul o el rojo?" en="Which do you prefer, the blue one or the red one?" />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A fast, reliable test: could the answer reasonably start with "It's a kind of..." or "It means..."? Reach for *qué*. Could the answer reasonably start with "This one, not that one"? Reach for *cuál*. *¿Cuál es tu nombre?* passes the second test — your name is one specific item picked out from every name that exists — even though nothing in the English sentence hints at a "selection" at all.
</GrammarNote>

### Cuál before a noun: a Spain-vs-Latin-America difference
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

The qué/cuál test above has one genuine regional wrinkle. When the word sits directly in front of a noun — *cuál* + noun, nothing in between — standard Castellano almost always reaches for *qué* instead, even in a clearly selection-type question, while several Latin American varieties accept *cuál* directly before a noun much more freely.

<ExampleSentence es="¿Qué libro prefieres, este o ese?" en="Which book do you prefer, this one or that one?" note="Selecting from a set — exactly the logic that calls for cuál above — but because cuál would sit directly in front of libro here, standard Peninsular Spanish uses qué instead. ¿Cuál libro prefieres? is heard in parts of Latin America but sounds off to most speakers in Spain." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
The rule from the previous section still holds everywhere the moment there's no noun glued directly onto *cuál* — *¿Cuál de los dos libros prefieres?* ("Which of the two books do you prefer?") is standard, natural Castellano, because *de los dos libros* comes between *cuál* and any noun.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="No sé por qué, pero cuando alguien me pregunta cuál es mi película favorita, nunca sé qué decir — ¡y no tengo ni idea de cómo elegir solo una!" en="I don't know why, but when someone asks me which is my favorite movie, I never know what to say — and I have no idea how to choose just one!" note="Stacks an indirect question (por qué, cómo), the qué/cuál contrast in the same breath, and a closing exclamation, all keeping their accents throughout." />

Faced with a question word to choose, work through it in order: decide first whether you're asking for a definition (*qué*) or a selection from a set (*cuál*, unless a noun sits directly against it, in which case Castellano still reaches for *qué*). Then remember the accent survives no matter how the word is dressed up — direct question, indirect question buried in a statement, or exclamation — and only disappears when the identically-spelled word is doing a completely different job as a relative pronoun or a conjunction.
