Ask a Spanish sentence "who did what to whom," and word order is only part of the answer — often a surprisingly small part. English leans on position to do almost all of that work: swap the order of "The dog bit the man" and you get a different, still-grammatical sentence with the opposite meaning ("The man bit the dog"), because English has little else to signal who's the subject and who's the object. Spanish starts from that same default order, subject-verb-object, but it isn't nearly as dependent on that order to stay clear — the verb itself is marked for who's doing the action, and objects often carry their own signals independent of where they sit. That slack is what lets Spanish move pieces of a sentence around for emphasis, questions, and topic-focus far more freely than English ever can. If you've already read the chapters on adjective position, direct object pronoun placement, relative-pronoun fronting, question words, or passive voice, you've already met five specific cases of the pattern this chapter is about — each gets a short recap and a pointer back to its full home below, so you can see the general principle they all share before this chapter teaches its own new material: how Spanish deliberately breaks its own default order to put focus exactly where a speaker wants it, and where that flexibility runs out completely.

## Core Pattern: Subject-Verb-Object, and Why It Bends

### The SVO default
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Spanish's neutral, unmarked word order is the same as English's: subject, then verb, then object. When nothing special is going on — no question, no contrast, no emphasis — this is where a Spanish sentence defaults to sit.

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Mi hermano", role: "subject" },
    { text: "compra", role: "verb" },
    { text: "el pan.", role: "object" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Mi hermano compra el pan todos los días." en="My brother buys bread every day." />
<ExampleSentence es="El camarero trajo la cuenta." en="The waiter brought the check." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Spanish very often drops the subject entirely once context makes it obvious (*compra el pan todos los días*, no *mi hermano* needed on the second mention) — full pro-drop logic belongs to [Chapter 11, Subject Pronouns & Pro-Drop](../lesson-11). SVO is simply where a subject sits *when one is stated at all*.
</GrammarNote>

### Why the order can move: verb agreement and marked objects carry the load
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

English can't safely reorder "the boy bought the apples" — flip subject and object and you've said something else entirely, because position is the *only* signal of who's doing what. Spanish has two other signals doing that job, which frees up position for other uses.

First, the verb is conjugated to agree with its subject in person and number, so even when a subject appears after the verb or after the object, the verb form itself points back to it:

<ExampleSentence es="Compró el niño las manzanas. — Las manzanas compró el niño." en="The boy bought the apples. — The apples, the boy bought." note="Compró is singular, so it can only agree with el niño (singular) — never with las manzanas (plural) — no matter which order the words appear in." />

Second, a direct object that's a specific person or animate being is marked with the personal *a*, regardless of where it ends up in the sentence — full treatment in [Chapter 49, Core Prepositions: A, De, En, Con](../lesson-49):

<ExampleSentence es="Vio Pedro a Juan. — A Juan vio Pedro." en="Pedro saw Juan. — Juan, Pedro saw." note="The a in front of Juan marks it as the object no matter where it sits — Pedro and Juan are both singular, so here it's the a, not verb agreement, doing the disambiguating work." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is the thread running through the rest of this chapter: because grammatical role is already marked elsewhere — by the verb, by personal *a*, by object pronoun agreement — Spanish word order is free to do other jobs. English, which relies on position for almost everything, can't move nearly as freely without becoming confusing or ungrammatical.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Word Order as a Tool for Emphasis

{/* Building Out bends its usual job here: instead of extending one new pattern into sub-variations, it both introduces the chapter's first genuinely new content (subject-verb inversion) and recaps three already-taught specific cases of this same flexibility (ch. 4, 17, 18) — each with a brief illustration and a pointer back to its full home, per this chapter's role as the synthesis chapter for word order generally. */}

Because grammatical role doesn't depend on position, Spanish can and does reorder sentence pieces on purpose — not randomly, but to control which part of the sentence carries the most informational weight. The clearest, most productive case is flipping the subject and the verb.

### Subject-verb inversion: Llegó Juan vs. Juan llegó
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Both orders below are fully grammatical, correct Spanish — the difference is entirely about emphasis, not correctness. *Juan llegó* treats Juan as already-known information and reports the new fact (that he arrived). *Llegó Juan* flips it: the verb comes first, and Juan himself is the new, focused-on information — the answer to an implicit or explicit "who?"

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Juan", role: "subject" },
    { text: "llegó.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Llegó", role: "verb" },
    { text: "Juan.", role: "subject" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Juan llegó tarde otra vez." en="Juan arrived late again." note="Neutral statement — we already know we're talking about Juan; the new information is that he was late again." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Quién llegó tarde? — Llegó Juan." en="'Who arrived late?' — 'Juan did.'" note="Subject-verb inversion puts Juan in focus position, answering exactly the question being asked — Juan llegó would sound oddly flat as an answer here." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This inversion shows up especially often with verbs of appearance, arrival, or existence — *llegar, venir, faltar, sonar, ocurrir* — where the whole point of the sentence is to introduce something new: *Sonó el teléfono* ("The phone rang"), *Ocurrió un accidente* ("An accident happened"). Presenting brand-new information is exactly the job VS order is built for.
</GrammarNote>

### Adjective position: the same flexibility one level down
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

[Chapter 4, Adjectives: Agreement & Position](../lesson-04) covers a phrase-level version of this same idea. Most descriptive adjectives follow the noun by default, but a subset can move in front of it — and for a handful of adjectives, that movement itself changes meaning, not just emphasis:

<ExampleSentence es="Es un gran hombre, no solo un hombre grande." en="He's a great man, not just a big man." note="The full mechanics — including why prenominal position tends to feel subjective and postnominal feels restrictive — belong to Chapter 4. The short version: this is the same 'position carries meaning' logic operating on a noun phrase instead of a whole sentence." />

### Question formation: obligatory fronting and inversion
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Questions are the one place in Spanish where a word-order shift isn't optional stylistic flavor — it's required. An interrogative word must front to the very start of the sentence, and the subject typically shifts after the verb or drops entirely, exactly as pro-drop grammar would predict:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "¿Qué", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "compró", role: "verb" },
    { text: "Juan?", role: "subject" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Juan compró el coche. — ¿Qué compró Juan?" en="Juan bought the car. — What did Juan buy?" note="Qué has to front, ahead of both the verb and the subject — full accent rules and the qué/cuál choice belong to Chapter 18." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Qué compráis vosotros para la fiesta?" en="What are you all buying for the party?" note="Same obligatory fronting with a vosotros subject." />

Full mechanics — the complete word set, the written accent that never disappears (even in indirect questions), and the genuinely tricky *qué* vs. *cuál* choice — live in [Chapter 18, Interrogative & Exclamative Words](../lesson-18).

### Fronting a preposition together with its relative pronoun
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

One more obligatory-fronting case, from a different corner of the grammar: when a relative clause needs a preposition, Spanish can't strand it at the end of the clause the way casual English does ("the person I spoke *with*"). The preposition and the relative pronoun move together, as one unit, to the front of the clause:

<ExampleSentence es="La persona con quien hablé es mi vecina." en="The person I spoke with is my neighbor." note="Con quien fronts as one inseparable unit — full treatment of when quien, que, or el cual is required after a preposition belongs to Chapter 17." />

Full mechanics — choosing between *que*, *quien*, and *el que/el cual* by register and preposition — live in [Chapter 17, Relative Pronouns](../lesson-17).

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: Fronting, Clefts, and the Limits of Flexibility

Subject-verb inversion moves the sentence's two core pieces around. Spanish has more marked strategies for pulling one specific piece of information into sharp focus — some with no real English equivalent — and, just as importantly, some positions in a Spanish sentence that are locked in place by grammar itself, not by style. Understanding why those two behaviors coexist in one language is the real payoff of this chapter.

### Fronting an object for contrast: A Juan no lo he visto
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

When a direct object is pulled to the front of the sentence for contrastive emphasis — "as for Juan, I haven't seen him" — Spanish requires a resumptive object pronoun back in the object's normal slot. This isn't optional stylistic doubling; the sentence is ungrammatical without it.

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "No", role: "connector" },
    { text: "he visto", role: "verb" },
    { text: "a Juan.", role: "object" }
  ]}
/>
<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "A Juan", role: "object" },
    { text: "no", role: "connector" },
    { text: "lo", role: "pronoun" },
    { text: "he visto.", role: "verb" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="No he visto a Juan. — A Juan no lo he visto." en="I haven't seen Juan. — Juan, I haven't seen." note="Once a Juan moves to the front for emphasis, lo must still appear later in the sentence, resuming the object's normal grammatical slot — dropping it (*A Juan no he visto) is simply ungrammatical." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
English can front an object for emphasis with nothing extra needed ("Juan, I haven't seen"). Spanish can't stop there — the resumptive pronoun is obligatory, not a matter of style. This is a genuine structure English doesn't build the same way.
</GrammarNote>

### Cleft-like constructions: Lo que necesito es tiempo
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

English builds a cleft sentence with a dummy "it" ("It's time that I need") or a free relative ("What I need is time"). Spanish's closest everyday equivalent leans on the neuter relative *lo que* plus *ser*, packaging everything except the one new fact into a single subject phrase, then delivering that new fact after *es*:

<SentenceStructure
  parts={[
    { text: "Lo que necesito", role: "subject" },
    { text: "es", role: "verb" },
    { text: "tiempo.", role: "object" }
  ]}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Necesito tiempo. — Lo que necesito es tiempo." en="I need time. — What I need is time." note="Lo que necesito packages 'the thing that I need' into subject position, leaving es tiempo to deliver the one piece of information the sentence exists to give." />

### Other focus-fronting: adverbials and the ser...quien cleft
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Fronting for focus isn't limited to direct objects. Time and place adverbials front constantly and don't need a resumptive pronoun at all, since Spanish doesn't track them with object pronouns the way it tracks direct objects:

<ExampleSentence es="El sábado vamos a la playa." en="On Saturday, we're going to the beach." note="El sábado fronted as the topic — ordinary and unmarked in everyday speech, unlike the object-fronting above, which specifically signals contrast." />

Spanish also has a second cleft strategy, built from *ser* + *que/quien* instead of *lo que...es*, that isolates a noun phrase — often the subject — as the direct answer to an implied question. It reads much closer to an English it-cleft:

<ExampleSentence es="Fue Juan quien llegó tarde, no María." en="It was Juan who arrived late, not María." note="Ser carries the emphasis, and quien introduces the rest as a relative clause — the same relative-pronoun system from Chapter 17, repurposed here for focus." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Negative words have their own obligatory fronting rule, structurally related to everything in this section but genuinely its own system: *nadie, nunca,* and other negative words can front before the verb, and when they do, *no* disappears entirely (*Nadie vino*, not *No nadie vino*). Full treatment in [Chapter 54, Negation](../lesson-54).
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="regional">
In literary, journalistic, and poetic registers, Spanish takes even more liberties with order — object-first (OVS) and other marked arrangements show up in writing far more than they would in relaxed conversation. Recognize this as the same flexibility principle stretched further, not a different grammar.
</GrammarNote>

### The exception: clitic pronouns and passive voice don't move
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Every pattern so far in this chapter moves whole, free-standing pieces of a sentence around: full noun phrases, prepositional phrases, adverbials. Object pronouns are different in kind, not just in degree. *Lo/la/los/las* ([Chapter 12](../lesson-12)), *le/les* ([Chapter 13](../lesson-13)), and their combined and reflexive forms ([Chapter 14](../lesson-14), [Chapter 15](../lesson-15)) aren't free constituents that discourse can place wherever it wants — they're clitics, phonologically dependent forms that have grammaticalized into exactly one of two fixed slots: immediately before a conjugated verb, or attached to an infinitive, gerund, or affirmative command. You cannot front a clitic pronoun on its own for emphasis, and you cannot strand it after the verb the way English keeps its object pronouns:

<ExampleSentence es="Lo veo. (never *Veo lo.)" en="I see it/him." note="The clitic lo has exactly one legal position here, immediately before veo — full placement rules across every verb shape live in Chapter 12." />

This is precisely why the object-fronting pattern above still needs *lo* left behind in its normal slot: fronting *a Juan* moves a free noun phrase, but the clitic pronoun resuming it is never itself free to move — it just does what it always does, sit immediately before the conjugated verb.

Passive voice ([Chapter 46](../lesson-46)) is a related but different kind of exception. It isn't that word order becomes rigid there — it's that active/passive isn't a word-order phenomenon in the sense this chapter means at all. Choosing passive changes *which noun phrase counts as the grammatical subject* and which becomes an optional agent phrase; it's a change in grammatical relations, not a reordering of the same relations:

<ExampleSentence es="Ingenieros construyeron el puente. — El puente fue construido por ingenieros." en="Engineers built the bridge. — The bridge was built by engineers." note="Both versions are internally SVO — subject-verb-object, then subject-verb-agent. Passive doesn't break the default order; it reassigns which noun phrase holds which role. Full mechanics in Chapter 46." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Put the two exceptions side by side and the underlying logic is consistent: everywhere this chapter shows flexibility, it's full constituents — nouns, noun phrases, adverbials — moving around a fixed grammatical skeleton. Clitic pronouns and passive voice both touch that skeleton itself (a grammaticalized position, a reassignment of subject/object roles), which is exactly why neither one bends the way ordinary word order does.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

Faced with a Spanish sentence to build, start from SVO and only leave it on purpose. Want the subject itself to be the new, focused-on information — especially with a verb of appearance or arrival? Invert to VS. Want to contrast one specific object against the alternatives? Front it, and don't forget the resumptive pronoun. Want to isolate one single fact as the direct answer to an implied question? Reach for *lo que...es* or *ser...quien*. And whatever else moves, remember that object pronouns and the true passive each keep their own separate rules, untouched by all of the above.

<ExampleSentence es="Todos esperaban que ganara Juan. Ganó María. A ella nadie la tomaba en serio, pero fue ella quien se llevó el premio — lo que demuestra que las apariencias engañan." en="Everyone expected Juan to win. María won. Nobody took her seriously, but she was the one who took home the prize — which shows that appearances can be deceiving." note="One short passage stacking subject-verb inversion (ganó María), object-fronting with its resumptive pronoun (a ella... la tomaba), and a ser-cleft (fue ella quien...) — all layered over the same underlying SVO skeleton, with the clitic la still obeying its own fixed placement rule throughout." />
