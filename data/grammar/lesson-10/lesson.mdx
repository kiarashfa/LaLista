Telling time and stating a date both come down to numbers doing a very specific, rule-bound job — and both trip up English speakers in ways that have nothing to do with vocabulary. Spanish clock time hides a genuine singular/plural exception that English doesn't have; Spanish dates are built in a different order than American dates and refuse to use ordinal numbers past the first of the month; and both draw on the cardinal and ordinal machinery from [Chapter 9, Numbers as Grammar](../lesson-09), which this chapter assumes rather than re-explains. By the end, you'll be able to construct a full date, a clock time, and — since the same numbers show up here too — read a decimal or a percentage the way it's actually written in Spain.

## Core Pattern: Telling Time

### Es la una vs. son las dos: the one exception to plural agreement
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Clock time is built with *ser* plus a definite feminine article standing in for *hora(s)* ("hour(s)") — and that article, unusually, has to agree in number with the hour itself. One o'clock is the sole singular case; every other hour is plural:

<ExampleSentence es="Es la una de la tarde." en="It's one o'clock in the afternoon." note="es (singular) + la (singular, standing in for 'la hora') — one o'clock is the only hour that takes the singular." />
<ExampleSentence es="Son las tres de la tarde." en="It's three o'clock in the afternoon." note="son (plural) + las (plural) — every hour from two onward uses the plural verb and article." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This trips up even confident learners because English "it's" never changes — "it's one o'clock" and "it's three o'clock" use the identical verb form. Spanish *es/son* genuinely tracks the hour.
</GrammarNote>

### Minutes: y, menos, cuarto, media
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Minutes attach to the hour with *y* (past) or *menos* (to/till), with *cuarto* (quarter) and *media* (half) as fixed shortcuts for :15 and :30:

<ExampleSentence es="Son las cuatro y cuarto." en="It's four fifteen (a quarter past four)." />
<ExampleSentence es="Son las cuatro y media." en="It's four thirty (half past four)." />
<ExampleSentence es="Son las cinco menos diez." en="It's ten to five." note="menos counts backward from the upcoming hour — the reverse of how English structures 'ten to five,' though the resulting meaning matches." />

### De la mañana, de la tarde, de la noche
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Spanish doesn't use am/pm; instead, it tags the hour with a time-of-day phrase, and the boundaries don't map exactly onto the English ones — *tarde* stretches later into the evening than an American "afternoon" would, and *noche* starts later than "night" often implies in English:

<ExampleSentence es="La reunión es a las nueve de la mañana." en="The meeting is at nine in the morning." />
<ExampleSentence es="Cenamos a las diez de la noche." en="We have dinner at ten at night." note="Ten at night for dinner is completely ordinary in Spain — de la noche covers hours an American speaker might still think of as evening." />

## Building Out: More Ways to Talk About Time

### En punto, mediodía, medianoche, and the 24-hour clock
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*En punto* ("on the dot," "exactly") pins a time down with no minutes; *mediodía* and *medianoche* replace the whole "twelve o'clock" construction outright. Beyond casual conversation, Spain also uses the 24-hour clock far more freely than American English does — on train and flight schedules, TV listings, store hours, and most official or written contexts:

<ExampleSentence es="El tren sale a las nueve en punto." en="The train leaves at nine on the dot." />
<ExampleSentence es="La farmacia abre a mediodía." en="The pharmacy opens at noon." />
<ExampleSentence es="El vuelo sale a las 18:45." en="The flight leaves at 18:45 (6:45 PM)." note="Read aloud as 'las dieciocho cuarenta y cinco' or, more formally, 'las dieciocho horas cuarenta y cinco minutos.'" />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Everyday spoken Castellano still defaults to the 12-hour clock with *de la mañana/tarde/noche* for ordinary conversation ("nos vemos a las siete") — the 24-hour clock is a register choice for formal, written, or scheduled contexts, not a replacement for casual speech.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: Dates

### Building a date: day + de + month + de + year
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

A full Spanish date is built day-month-year, each piece linked by *de* — the reverse order of standard American month-day-year:

<ExampleSentence es="Nací el 3 de mayo de 1998." en="I was born on May 3rd, 1998." note="Literally 'the 3 of May of 1998' — day first, then month, then year, each joined by de." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Because of the order swap, an all-numeral date like 3/5/1998 means May 3rd in Spain but March 5th in the US — worth flagging any time you see a numeral-only date written by a Spanish source.
</GrammarNote>

### The first of the month — but not "the second," "the third"
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Dates use cardinal numbers (*dos, tres, cuatro*...), not ordinals, for every day of the month except sometimes the first — the one day that can optionally take the ordinal *primero*, continuing the pattern from [Chapter 9](../lesson-09) where ordinals fade out past a very low ceiling:

<ExampleSentence es="Hoy es el uno de mayo." en="Today is the first of May." note="El uno, using the plain cardinal, is an everyday choice in Spain." />
<ExampleSentence es="Hoy es el primero de mayo." en="Today is the first of May." note="El primero (ordinal) is also correct and common — the only day of the month where the ordinal survives at all in everyday use." />
<ExampleSentence es="Hoy es el dos de mayo." en="Today is the second of May." note="Never el segundo de mayo — from the 2nd onward, only the cardinal is used." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Spain uses *el uno de mayo* about as often as *el primero de mayo*; some Latin American varieties favor *el primero* more strongly. Either is understood everywhere.
</GrammarNote>

### Days and months don't get capital letters
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Unlike English, Spanish writes days of the week and months of the year in lowercase, except at the start of a sentence:

<ExampleSentence es="Nos vemos el lunes 3 de mayo." en="See you on Monday, May 3rd." note="lunes and mayo both stay lowercase — a common transfer error from English capitalization habits." />

### Reading years as one whole number, not paired digits
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

English often splits a year into two two-digit chunks when reading it aloud ("nineteen ninety-eight"). Spanish never does this — a year is read as a single cardinal number, however long it is:

<ExampleSentence es="mil novecientos noventa y ocho" en="nineteen ninety-eight (1998)" note="Literally 'one thousand nine hundred ninety-eight' — read straight through as one number, never split into 'diecinueve noventa y ocho.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="dos mil veinticuatro" en="two thousand twenty-four (2024)" />

Decades and centuries draw on the same cardinal/ordinal machinery from Chapter 9: decades are named with the plural of the tens-number (*los años noventa* — "the nineties"), and centuries are written in Roman numerals but read aloud as the plain cardinal, exactly like the *siglo XXI* example from that chapter.

<ExampleSentence es="Esa canción es de los años ochenta." en="That song is from the eighties." />

## Numbers in Applied Contexts: Decimals, Percentages & Measurements

{/* This section is a deliberate extension rather than a strict "Building Out" or "Nuance" slot: decimals/percentages aren't a complication of time or date grammar, they're a related-but-separate application of the same number system, matching how the table of contents frames this as a brief closing subsection rather than its own grammatical topic. */}

This closing section isn't a new grammatical system — it's the same number grammar from Chapter 9 showing up in a few more everyday formats worth being able to read and write correctly.

### The decimal comma: 3,5 not 3.5
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Spain — like most of continental Europe — writes a decimal using a comma, and uses a period (or a thin space) to separate thousands, the exact reverse of US convention:

<ExampleSentence es="El precio es 3,50 euros." en="The price is 3.50 euros." note="A comma marks the decimal point; where American English would write '3.50,' Spanish writes '3,50.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="La población es de 1.200.000 habitantes." en="The population is 1,200,000 inhabitants." note="Periods (or thin spaces) mark the thousands groupings — the opposite role from the comma's job in American numbers." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This isn't just a stylistic quirk — reading 3.5 with the American convention on a Spanish document could genuinely be misread as "three thousand five hundred." Always check which convention a document is using before trusting a number at a glance.
</GrammarNote>

### Percentages and measurements
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Percentages are read with the cardinal number plus *por ciento* (or the % symbol), and — like other quantity expressions — the verb that follows often agrees with the noun the percentage refers to, not with the number itself:

<ExampleSentence es="El veinte por ciento de los estudiantes llega tarde." en="Twenty percent of the students arrive late." note="llega (singular) treats el veinte por ciento as the subject, though llegan (plural, agreeing with estudiantes) is also commonly heard — both are accepted in practice." />

Spain uses the metric system throughout, so measurements built with these same numbers — *kilómetros, kilos, grados, litros* — follow ordinary cardinal-number grammar with no special agreement rules beyond what this chapter and Chapter 9 already cover.

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="La reunión es el 3 de marzo de 2025, a las nueve y media de la mañana; llegó el 20,5 % de los invitados." en="The meeting is on March 3rd, 2025, at nine thirty in the morning; 20.5% of the guests arrived." note="One sentence combining date order (day-de-month-de-year), clock time with y media, and a decimal-comma percentage — the full range of this chapter's applied number grammar." />

Work through any time, date, or quantity expression the same way: identify whether you're building a clock time (check *es/son* and *la/las* agreement), a date (day-de-month-de-year, cardinal past the first), or an applied number (comma for decimals, period for thousands) — each draws on the cardinal and ordinal rules from Chapter 9, just applied to a different real-world format.
