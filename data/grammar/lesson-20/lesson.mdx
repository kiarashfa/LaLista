Every Spanish verb you'll ever conjugate follows the same two-part logic: a stable core that carries meaning, and a changeable tail that reports who's doing it, when, and how the speaker frames it. English does a little of this too — "talk" becomes "talks" for he/she/it — but Spanish pushes the idea much further, packing person, number, tense, and mood into the verb itself rather than leaning on separate words. That's why a single word like *hablamos* can stand in for a whole English sentence ("we talk," "we are talking," "we do talk") and why Spanish can drop its subject pronouns almost entirely ([Chapter 11](../lesson-11)) and still be perfectly clear about who's doing what. This chapter doesn't drill any one tense — that's the job of every chapter from here through Chapter 45 — it teaches the machinery all of them share: what a stem is, what an ending is, and how the three conjugation classes organize the whole system.

## Core Pattern: Stem + Ending

### The infinitive: where every verb starts
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Spanish verbs are cited — in dictionaries, in vocabulary lists, in this book — by their infinitive, the unconjugated "to \_\_\_" form. Every single Spanish infinitive ends in one of exactly three ways: **-ar**, **-er**, or **-ir**. *Hablar* (to talk), *comer* (to eat), *vivir* (to live) — that final two-or-three-letter chunk isn't decorative. It's the verb's class marker, and it determines which family of endings the verb will take in every tense you ever learn.

<ExampleSentence es="Hablar, comer y vivir son los tres verbos modelo de este libro — uno por cada terminación posible." en="Hablar, comer, and vivir are this book's three model verbs — one for each possible ending." />

### The stem: where the meaning lives
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Strip the **-ar/-er/-ir** off the infinitive and what's left is the stem — the fixed, meaning-carrying part of the verb that (for a regular verb) never changes shape no matter what tense or person you attach to it:

| Infinitive | Class | Stem |
|---|---|---|
| hablar | -AR | habl- |
| comer | -ER | com- |
| vivir | -IR | viv- |

The stem is doing the same job a noun's root does: *habl-* is where "talking" as a concept lives, the same way *libr-* is where "book-ness" lives in *libro*. Everything Spanish needs to say *about* that action — who's doing it, when, how many people — gets bolted onto that stem as an ending.

<ExampleSentence es="Trabajo, trabajé, trabajaré: la raíz trabaj- no cambia; solo cambia lo que viene después." en="I work, I worked, I will work: the stem trabaj- doesn't change; only what comes after it does." note="A preview of the mechanism — the full present, preterite, and future paradigms each get their own chapter." />

### The ending: what it packs in
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

An ending attached to a Spanish stem can simultaneously encode up to four pieces of grammatical information: **person** (who — yo, tú, él...), **number** (how many — singular or plural), **tense** (when — present, past, future...), and **mood** (how the speaker frames the statement — as fact, wish, or command). One small ending, four jobs at once. That's a lot more than English asks of a verb ending, and it's exactly why a bare Spanish verb form can be a complete sentence on its own — *hablamos* already tells you "we," "present," and "a plain statement of fact," with nothing else required.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Notice what's missing from that list: gender. Unlike adjectives and articles, a Spanish verb ending never changes to match whether the subject is male or female — *habla* is exactly the same word whether "he" or "she" is talking. Person and number cover everything a verb ending needs to say about its subject.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: The Three Conjugation Classes

### -AR, -ER, -IR: three families, one shared logic
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Every regular Spanish verb belongs to one of three conjugation classes, named for their infinitive ending. Once you know a verb's class and its stem, you know exactly which set of endings it will take for any tense — the same set every other verb in that class takes, with zero exceptions for a regular verb. This is also the class new verbs join when Spanish borrows or invents one: *chatear* (to chat online), *escanear* (to scan), *hackear* (to hack) are all recent arrivals, and every one of them slotted straight into the -AR class, the largest and most productive of the three by a wide margin.

| Class | Model verb | Stem | Share of Spanish verbs |
|---|---|---|---|
| -AR | hablar | habl- | the large majority — and essentially all new verbs |
| -ER | comer | com- | a smaller, closed set |
| -IR | vivir | viv- | a smaller, closed set |

### Close cousins: how -ER and -IR overlap
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

-AR verbs are the true outlier of the three. -ER and -IR verbs, by contrast, share the *same* endings for several entire tenses — not similar endings, identical ones — and differ from each other only at a couple of specific points. The table below previews the relationship without spelling out any single tense's full paradigm (that's Chapter 22 and beyond):

| Tense family | Relationship between classes |
|---|---|
| Present | -ER and -IR nearly match, differing mainly in the *nosotros/vosotros* forms |
| Preterite | -ER and -IR endings are completely identical |
| Imperfect | -ER and -IR endings are completely identical |
| Future & Conditional | All three classes — -AR, -ER, and -IR — share identical endings |

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is worth internalizing early: from the preterite onward, "-er/-ir verb" starts to function almost like a single mental category rather than two separate ones. Chapter 22 shows exactly where present tense still keeps them apart.
</GrammarNote>

### The exception: future and conditional attach to the whole infinitive
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Every tense mentioned so far builds on the *stem* — the infinitive with -ar/-er/-ir removed. The future and conditional break that pattern: their endings attach to the **entire infinitive**, ending and all, not to the bare stem. *Hablar* + *-é* gives *hablaré*, not a "stem + ending" combination in the usual sense — the *-ar* stays put and the ending glues on after it. This is exactly why future/conditional endings are identical across all three conjugation classes (per the table above): the infinitive itself already carries the class marker, so the ending doesn't need to.

<ExampleSentence es="Hablaré, comeré, viviré: el infinitivo entero se queda, y la terminación se pega detrás." en="I will speak, I will eat, I will live: the whole infinitive stays, and the ending attaches after it." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This trips learners up precisely because every other tense trains you to chop the infinitive ending first. Writing *hablré* instead of *hablaré* is the single most common mechanical error at this stage — the future and conditional are the one place where you keep the *-ar/-er/-ir* instead of removing it. Full paradigms, including a set of common irregular stems, live in [Chapter 33](../lesson-33) and [Chapter 34](../lesson-34).
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### What "irregular" actually means
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Everything above describes a regular verb — one where the stem never shifts and the endings never deviate from the class pattern. Irregularity comes in two structurally different flavors, and it's worth telling them apart from the start: a verb can have an **irregular stem** that shifts shape in a predictable pattern depending on where the stress falls (*perder* → *pierdo*, still fully regular endings), or it can have **irregular endings** that don't follow the expected class pattern at all (*ser* → *soy*, where almost nothing looks like a regular -er verb). The first kind is the subject of [Chapter 23](../lesson-23); the second kind is rarer, more disruptive, and *ser* and *estar* — covered together in [Chapter 21](../lesson-21) — are its two most important examples.

### Mood: indicative, subjunctive, and imperative
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Tense answers "when." Mood answers a different question: how does the speaker frame what they're saying — as a plain fact, as a wish or doubt, or as a command? Nearly every verb form covered in the next several chapters (present, preterite, imperfect, future, conditional) belongs to the **indicative** mood, the mood of stating things as fact, and that's the default assumption unless a chapter says otherwise. Spanish also has an **imperative** mood for direct commands ([Chapter 36](../lesson-36)) and a **subjunctive** mood for expressing wishes, doubts, emotions, and hypotheticals ([Chapters 37–41](../lesson-37)) — both built from endings that genuinely differ from their indicative counterparts, not just a different translation of the same form.

### Conjugated forms vs. non-finite forms
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Not every form built from a verb is "conjugated" in the person-and-number sense this chapter describes. The infinitive itself (*hablar*), the gerund (*hablando*), and the past participle (*hablado*) are called **non-finite** forms — they don't change for who's doing the action, because grammatically they don't have a subject the way a conjugated verb does. They still play essential roles in the sentence — as nouns, in progressive constructions, in compound tenses — and each gets its own full chapter later: the infinitive ([Chapter 42](../lesson-42)), the gerund ([Chapter 43](../lesson-43)), and the past participle ([Chapter 44](../lesson-44)).

## Bringing It Together

Given any Spanish infinitive, the same four-step process gets you ready to conjugate it in any tense you're about to learn: **identify the class** from its final two letters (-ar, -er, or -ir); **find the stem** by removing that ending; **check whether the tense you need attaches to the stem or to the whole infinitive** (everything does except future and conditional); and **confirm the verb is actually regular** before assuming the pattern applies cleanly — a quick glance at [Chapter 23](../lesson-23)'s irregular groupings will tell you if it isn't.

<ExampleSentence es="Trabajo en Madrid, trabajé en Barcelona, y algún día trabajaré en Buenos Aires." en="I work in Madrid, I worked in Barcelona, and someday I'll work in Buenos Aires." note="Same stem trabaj- for the present and preterite forms — but the future glues its ending onto the whole infinitive trabajar, giving trabajaré, not trabajré." />
