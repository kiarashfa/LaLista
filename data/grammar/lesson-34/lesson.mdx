Spanish's condicional simple is the tense behind "would": me gustaría (I would like), sería mejor (it would be better), ¿podrías ayudarme? (could/would you help me?). It shares its entire irregular-stem system with the futuro simple you just met in [Chapter 33](../lesson-33), so if that chapter is fresh, the hardest part of this one is already done. What's genuinely new here is the range of everyday jobs this tense does — softening a request, describing a hypothetical, reporting what someone said would happen, and guessing about the past — and one specific trap English speakers fall into that this chapter spends real time on.

## Core Pattern: The Same Stems, a New Ending Set

### Regular formation: infinitive + -ía endings
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Like the future, the conditional attaches directly to the whole infinitive for regular verbs — no stem to trim — and uses one identical ending set across all three conjugations: -ía, -ías, -ía, -íamos, -íais, -ían.

  caption="conditional — fully regular, no boot outline"

    "yo": "hablaría",
    "tú": "hablarías",
    "él/ella/usted": "hablaría",
    "nosotros": "hablaríamos",
    "vosotros": "hablaríais",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "hablarían"
  }}
/>

| | hablar | comer | vivir |
|---|---|---|---|
| yo | hablaría | comería | viviría |
| tú | hablarías | comerías | vivirías |
| él/ella/usted | hablaría | comería | viviría |
| nosotros | hablaríamos | comeríamos | viviríamos |
| vosotros | hablaríais | comeríais | viviríais |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | hablarían | comerían | vivirían |

<ExampleSentence es="Yo hablaría con ella antes de decidir nada." en="I would talk to her before deciding anything." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Recognize these endings? They're the exact same six endings that -er/-ir verbs use in the imperfect ([Chapter 28](../lesson-28)) — quería, comía, vivía. The difference is only what they attach to: the imperfect trims down to a bare stem (com-ía), while the conditional keeps the whole infinitive intact (comer-ía). If you can already conjugate a regular -er/-ir imperfect, you already know these endings by ear.
</GrammarNote>

Every verb that took an irregular stem in the future ([Chapter 33](../lesson-33)) takes the exact same irregular stem here — with nothing new to learn about which verbs are affected or why. Only the ending set changes, from the futuro simple's -é/-ás/-á/-emos/-éis/-án to this chapter's -ía/-ías/-ía/-íamos/-íais/-ían.

## Building Out: The Core Uses of the Conditional

### Hypotheticals: what would happen
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The most basic job of the conditional is describing something that would happen under some condition, stated or implied.

<ExampleSentence es="Me gustaría viajar por toda Sudamérica algún día." en="I would like to travel all over South America someday." />
<ExampleSentence es="Sería mejor salir temprano para evitar el tráfico." en="It would be better to leave early to avoid traffic." />

### Polite requests and softened statements
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Framing a request or a statement in the conditional pulls it back from a blunt demand toward something gentler — this is one of the conditional's most frequent everyday uses, not a rare politeness flourish reserved for formal situations.

<ExampleSentence es="¿Podrías ayudarme un momento?" en="Could you help me for a moment?" note="Far softer than the present-tense ¿Puedes ayudarme?, though both are grammatical." />
<ExampleSentence es="Querría preguntarte algo, si tienes tiempo." en="I would like to ask you something, if you have time." />
<ExampleSentence es="Deberías descansar un poco más." en="You should rest a bit more." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
¿Podríais...? and other vosotros-conditional requests are a distinctly Castellano everyday sound — most of Latin American Spanish would soften the same group request with ¿Podrían...? (ustedes), since vosotros isn't part of the everyday paradigm there. Both are equally polite; the pronoun system, not the politeness logic, is what differs.
</GrammarNote>

[Chapter 28](../lesson-28)'s imperfect softening (quería preguntarte...) does similar work and is, if anything, even more common in casual spoken requests — the conditional here sits a notch further toward formal or careful politeness, though in practice the two overlap heavily and native speakers move between them freely.

### Reported future: what someone said would happen
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

When a past-tense reporting verb (dijo, prometió, avisó...) introduces someone else's future-tense claim, that claim typically backshifts from the future into the conditional — the same kind of shift [Chapter 31](../lesson-31) showed for the present perfect backshifting into the pluperfect inside reported speech.

<ExampleSentence es="Ana dijo: 'Vendré mañana.'" en="Ana said: 'I'll come tomorrow.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="Ana dijo que vendría mañana." en="Ana said that she would come tomorrow." note="Vendré backshifts to vendría once it's folded inside dijo que — the futuro simple's own irregular stem, vendr-, carries straight over." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Conditional for conjecture in the past: the direct parallel to Chapter 33
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Just as the futuro simple guesses at something true right now ([Chapter 33](../lesson-33): estará en casa), the condicional simple guesses at something that was probably true at a past moment. This is the exact same logic, one tense-step further into the past — worth holding the two side by side rather than learning them as unrelated facts.

<ExampleSentence es="Cuando llegamos, serían las tres de la tarde." en="When we arrived, it was probably around three in the afternoon." note="Serían isn't 'they would be' here — it's a guess about a past moment, the direct past-tense counterpart to Chapter 33's estará en casa." />
<ExampleSentence es="Tendría unos veinte años cuando se mudó a Madrid." en="He was probably around twenty when he moved to Madrid." />

### The English "would" trap: hypothetical vs. habitual past
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

English "would" is genuinely ambiguous in a way Spanish isn't. Sometimes "would" marks a true hypothetical (exactly the conditional's job); other times it describes a repeated habit in the past ("when I was a kid, we would go to the beach every summer") — and for that second meaning, Spanish reaches for the imperfect ([Chapter 28](../lesson-28)), never the conditional.

<ExampleSentence es="Cuando era niño, íbamos a la playa todos los veranos." en="When I was a kid, we would go to the beach every summer." note="English 'would' here describes a repeated habit, not a hypothetical — Spanish uses the imperfect, not the conditional, for this meaning." />
<ExampleSentence es="Si tuviera tiempo, iría a la playa." en="If I had time, I would go to the beach." note="Here 'would' is a genuine hypothetical — this is exactly the conditional's job." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
"Iría a la playa todos los veranos" to mean "I used to go to the beach every summer" is a common, understandable mistake for an English speaker — but it's wrong. Spanish reserves the conditional for genuine hypotheticals and never uses it for a repeated past habit, no matter how naturally the English "would" suggests it. If you can replace "would" with "used to" without changing the meaning, you need the imperfect, not the conditional.
</GrammarNote>

### Softening isn't limited to single requests
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

The same softening instinct behind ¿podrías...? extends past individual requests into everyday hedged opinions and suggestions — deberías, podríamos, no estaría mal... — a broad, constant register choice rather than a narrow grammar rule for asking favors. [Chapter 57](../lesson-57) covers the fuller toolkit of hedging strategies this belongs to.

<ExampleSentence es="Podríamos quedar el sábado en vez del viernes." en="We could meet up on Saturday instead of Friday." />

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Me gustaría ir a la fiesta, pero le pregunté a Marta y dijo que llegaría tarde. Serían casi las nueve cuando por fin salió del trabajo. ¿Podrías avisarme si cambian de planes?" en="I would like to go to the party, but I asked Marta and she said she would arrive late. It was probably almost nine by the time she finally left work. Could you let me know if plans change?" note="Me gustaría (hypothetical), llegaría tarde (reported future, backshifted from llegaré), serían (conjecture about a past moment), podrías (polite request)." />

Facing a "would" in English, ask first whether it's describing something that never happened but could under the right condition (conditional), a habit repeated over and over in the past (imperfect — not this chapter's tense at all), someone's past claim about their own future (conditional, backshifted from what they originally said in the future tense), or a guess about how things probably stood at some earlier moment (conditional, the past-tense twin of Chapter 33's present-tense guesswork).
