Quantifiers answer the question "how much?" or "how many?" without giving an exact count — *mucho café, pocos amigos, todo el día*. Spanish groups a surprisingly varied set of words under this job: some behave like ordinary adjectives that agree with the noun they describe, some freeze into a single invariable form no matter what, and a couple shrink to a shorter form the instant they land in front of a masculine singular noun. None of this is optional detail — get the agreement or the shortened form wrong and a native speaker notices immediately, even when the meaning still comes through. This chapter organizes the quantifier system by how each word *behaves*, not by translation, since translation is exactly where English intuition starts to mislead: English "some" covers ground that Spanish splits across *alguno*, *unos*, and *varios*.

## Core Pattern: Mucho, Poco, Bastante & Demasiado

These four words form the most basic quantity scale in Spanish — from "not much" to "too much" — and they set the pattern most other quantifiers in this chapter follow: agree like an adjective when quantifying a noun, freeze into one invariable form when modifying a verb or adjective instead.

### Quantifying a noun: full adjective agreement
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*mucho/mucha/muchos/muchas*, *poco/poca/pocos/pocas*, and *demasiado/demasiada/demasiados/demasiadas* all take four forms, agreeing in gender and number with the noun they quantify. *Bastante* only has two forms — it marks number (*bastante/bastantes*) but never changes for gender.

<ExampleSentence es="Tengo mucho trabajo y poca paciencia." en="I have a lot of work and little patience." note="mucho and poca both agree with the noun they quantify — trabajo (m.) and paciencia (f.)." />
<ExampleSentence es="Bastante gente vino a la boda." en="Quite a lot of people came to the wedding." note="bastante doesn't change for gente's gender — only bastantes would mark a plural noun." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
It's tempting to write *bastanta* by analogy with *mucho/mucha* — resist it. *Bastante* only ever marks number, never gender.
</GrammarNote>

### Same word, no agreement: mucho, poco, bastante & demasiado as adverbs
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

When these words modify a verb or an adjective instead of a noun, they freeze into their base masculine-singular-looking form — because grammatically they've stopped being adjectives and become adverbs, and Spanish adverbs don't inflect.

<ExampleSentence es="Ana trabaja mucho, pero duerme poco." en="Ana works a lot, but sleeps little." note="mucho and poco here modify the verbs trabaja/duerme, not a noun — so they never shift to feminine or plural forms like *trabaja mucha." />
<ExampleSentence es="Este examen es demasiado difícil." en="This exam is too difficult." note="demasiado modifies the adjective difícil and stays in its base form, even though examen is masculine and difícil could pair with a feminine noun elsewhere." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The test isn't the gender of the nearby noun — it's what the quantifier is actually attached to. Compare *un examen demasiado difícil* (adverb, modifying *difícil*, invariable) with *demasiados exámenes* (adjective, modifying *exámenes*, agrees). Both are correct; they're just doing different jobs.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Todo, Cada, Varios & Ambos

*Todo* extends the same agreeing-adjective pattern to its fullest four-form range, while *cada* breaks the pattern entirely by refusing to change at all — and *varios/ambos* add a wrinkle of their own: they only ever appear in the plural.

### Todo: full agreement, and the article that comes with it
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*todo/toda/todos/todas* agrees like *mucho*, but it's almost always followed by a definite article (*el/la/los/las*) before the noun:

<ExampleSentence es="Trabajé todo el día." en="I worked all day." />
<ExampleSentence es="Todas las tiendas están cerradas los domingos." en="All the stores are closed on Sundays." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Drop the article and *todo* usually shifts register: *toda mujer sabe eso* ("every woman knows that," a generic, almost formal statement) reads differently from *todas las mujeres saben eso* ("all the women [in question] know that"). In everyday speech, keep the article.
</GrammarNote>

### Cada: the quantifier that never changes
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Cada* (each, every) is invariable — one single form for every gender and, more surprisingly, for number too. Even when it sets up a plural meaning, the noun right after it stays singular:

<ExampleSentence es="Cada estudiante tiene su propio libro." en="Each student has their own book." />
<ExampleSentence es="Vengo a Madrid cada dos meses." en="I come to Madrid every two months." note="cada itself never pluralizes, even paired with a number — only the noun meses carries its own ordinary plural." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
A learner's instinct is to make *cada* agree, because everything else in this chapter does. Resist it — *cada* is one of a handful of Spanish words (like the shortened *cualquier*, below) that simply doesn't inflect at all.
</GrammarNote>

### Varios and ambos: always plural, and one is more formal than it looks
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*varios/varias* (several) and *ambos/ambas* (both) agree in gender the way *todo* does, but neither has a singular form at all — the meaning itself requires more than one.

<ExampleSentence es="Compré varios libros en el mercadillo." en="I bought several books at the flea market." />
<ExampleSentence es="Ambas opciones son válidas." en="Both options are valid." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
*Ambos* is common in writing and formal speech throughout the Spanish-speaking world, but everyday spoken Castellano often reaches for *los dos / las dos* instead: *las dos opciones son válidas* sounds just as natural as *ambas opciones* and is more typical at a conversational register.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Alguno and ninguno: apocope before a masculine singular noun
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Alguno* (some/any) and *ninguno* (none/no) follow the full four-form agreement pattern you'd expect — until one lands directly in front of a masculine singular noun, where it shortens: *alguno → algún*, *ninguno → ningún*. This is the same apocope mechanism covered for *buen(o)* and *gran(de)* in [Chapter 4, Adjectives: Agreement & Position](../lesson-04) — a general rule about certain adjectives before a masculine singular noun, not a quirk unique to these two words.

<ExampleSentence es="¿Tienes algún libro sobre historia?" en="Do you have any book about history?" note="alguno shortens to algún directly before libro (m. sing.)." />
<ExampleSentence es="No tengo ningún interés en eso." en="I have no interest in that." />
<ExampleSentence es="No tengo ninguna razón para dudar de ti." en="I have no reason to doubt you." note="Before a feminine noun, no shortening happens: ninguna, not ningún." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Ningunos/ningunas* (plural) technically exists but is rarely used — Spanish tends to phrase "no X" with the singular *ningún/ninguna* even where English would say "no reasons" (plural): *no tengo ninguna razón*, not *ningunas razones*. The plural mainly surfaces with nouns that are grammatically always plural (*no tengo ningunas ganas* — "I don't feel like it at all").
</GrammarNote>

### Cualquiera: apocopates before either gender, and changes register behind the noun
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

*Cualquiera* (any, any one at all) is invariable for gender — it never splits into a *cualquiera/cualquiero* pair — but it does shorten to *cualquier* the moment it sits directly before a noun of either gender, singular:

<ExampleSentence es="Puedes elegir cualquier libro de la estantería." en="You can choose any book from the shelf." />
<ExampleSentence es="Cualquier persona puede aprender español." en="Any person can learn Spanish." note="cualquier stays cualquier before persona (f.) exactly as before libro (m.) — no gender agreement at all, only the apocope." />

Move *cualquiera* to *after* the noun instead, and two things change at once: it keeps its full form, and the meaning shifts from neutral "any" to dismissive "just any old, nothing special":

<ExampleSentence es="No es un médico cualquiera — es el mejor de la ciudad." en="He's not just any doctor — he's the best in the city." note="Post-nominal cualquiera carries a judgment (ordinary, unremarkable) that pre-nominal cualquier doesn't." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*Cualquiera* also stands alone as a pronoun with no apocope at all: *cualquiera puede hacerlo* ("anyone can do it"). The shortened form *cualquier* only ever appears glued directly in front of a noun.
</GrammarNote>

### Quantifier or pronoun? The same word doing two different jobs
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Most of the words in this chapter can drop the noun they'd normally modify and stand alone as a pronoun instead — same word, same agreement, but now referring back to something already understood from context rather than describing a noun sitting right in front of it.

<ExampleSentence es="Compré varios libros. Algunos eran en español." en="I bought several books. Some were in Spanish." note="varios modifies libros directly (quantifier); algunos stands alone, referring back to libros (pronoun)." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Queda vino? — Queda poco." en="Is there any wine left? — There's little left." note="poco here has no noun attached at all; it functions as a pronoun standing in for 'poco vino.'" />

This is exactly where English "some" and "any" become an unreliable guide: English uses one small set of words for both jobs, so a learner's instinct is to hunt for a single Spanish equivalent. There isn't one — the right choice depends on whether a noun follows (quantifier) or not (pronoun), and on which specific word the sentence needs. [Chapter 19, Neuter & Indefinite Pronouns](../lesson-19) picks up this thread for *algo/nada/alguien/nadie* — words that are pronouns *only*, with no quantifier use at all.

## Bringing It Together

Faced with a quantity to express, work through it in order: pick the word for the amount you mean (*mucho* for "a lot," *algún* for "some/any," *cada* for "each"), then ask whether it's modifying a noun right there in the sentence — if so, make it agree (unless it's *cada*, *bastante*'s gender slot, or a frozen adverb use) — and check whether it's about to apocopate in front of a masculine singular noun.

<ExampleSentence es="Cada día llegan varios turistas, pero pocos hablan español y casi ningún guía habla inglés." en="Every day several tourists arrive, but few speak Spanish and almost no guide speaks English." note="A sentence deliberately stacking cada (invariable), varios (plural agreement), pocos (full agreement), and ningún (apocope) in one breath." />
