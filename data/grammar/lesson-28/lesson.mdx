English's plain past tense doesn't ask you to specify whether an action was a one-time event or a repeated habit, a sudden happening or an ongoing backdrop — "I played piano" covers both "I played piano yesterday, once" and "I played piano every day as a kid." Spanish refuses to leave that distinction unmarked. Where [Chapters 26](../lesson-26)–[27](../lesson-27) taught you the preterite, for actions viewed as complete, this chapter teaches its counterpart: the imperfect, the tense for describing the past with no interest in when something started or stopped — the ongoing action, the repeated habit, the backdrop other things happen against. The good news is that of all the past tenses in Spanish, this is the one with almost nothing to memorize: outside of three verbs, every verb in the language conjugates by the exact same two sets of endings you're about to learn.

## Core Pattern: The Two Imperfect Endings

### -AR verbs: -aba, -abas, -aba...
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Regular -ar verbs drop the -ar and add one consistent set of endings: -aba, -abas, -aba, -ábamos, -abais, -aban.

<BootTable
  label="hablar"
  caption="imperfect"
  forms={{
    "yo": "hablaba",
    "tú": "hablabas",
    "él/ella/usted": "hablaba",
    "nosotros": "hablábamos",
    "vosotros": "hablabais",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "hablaban"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="Hablábamos por teléfono todas las noches." en="We used to talk on the phone every night." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Only one form in this set carries a written accent — nosotros (hablábamos) — because that's the only form where the stress would otherwise fall somewhere unexpected. Every other form stresses the a of -aba naturally, with no accent mark needed.
</GrammarNote>

### -ER/-IR verbs: -ía, -ías, -ía... (identical for both)
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Regular -er and -ir verbs share one identical set of endings — the first tense in this book where the -er/-ir split from [Chapter 22](../lesson-22) disappears completely: -ía, -ías, -ía, -íamos, -íais, -ían.

<BootTable
  label="vivir"
  caption="imperfect"
  forms={{
    "yo": "vivía",
    "tú": "vivías",
    "él/ella/usted": "vivía",
    "nosotros": "vivíamos",
    "vosotros": "vivíais",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "vivían"
  }}
/>

<ExampleSentence es="De pequeños, vivíamos cerca de la playa." en="As kids, we used to live near the beach." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mis abuelos comían juntos cada domingo." en="My grandparents used to eat together every Sunday." note="Comer takes the exact same endings as vivir — comía, comías, comía, comíamos, comíais, comían." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Unlike the -ar set, every single form here carries a written accent on the í — comía, comías, comía, comíamos, comíais, comían. That accent is what keeps the imperfect visually distinct from other tenses built on similar stems, so don't drop it when writing.
</GrammarNote>

### Core meaning: an action with no visible edges
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

The imperfect frames a past action without marking when it began or when it ended — it's less a single event than a stretch of time, a repeated pattern, or a state that simply held true.

<ExampleSentence es="Cuando vivía en Sevilla, caminaba a la playa cada tarde." en="When I lived in Seville, I used to walk to the beach every afternoon." note="Both verbs are imperfect — neither the living nor the walking is framed as starting or finishing; the sentence just describes how things were." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This chapter deliberately teaches the imperfect on its own terms, without lining it up side by side against the preterite. That full decision — which past tense to reach for in a given sentence — gets its own dedicated treatment in [Chapter 29](../lesson-29), once you know both tenses individually.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: The Three Irregulars, and the Imperfect's Full Range of Meaning

The same two endings above account for every verb in Spanish, with exactly three exceptions — and once you've met those three, the rest of this section just extends the "no visible edges" idea from Core Pattern into the specific jobs the imperfect does constantly: setting a scene, stating an age, telling time, and naming a habit.

### Ser, ir, and ver: the only three irregular verbs in the entire tense
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

<BootTable
  label="ser"
  caption="imperfect"
  forms={{
    "yo": "era",
    "tú": "eras",
    "él/ella/usted": "era",
    "nosotros": "éramos",
    "vosotros": "erais",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "eran"
  }}
/>

<BootTable
  label="ir"
  caption="imperfect"
  forms={{
    "yo": "iba",
    "tú": "ibas",
    "él/ella/usted": "iba",
    "nosotros": "íbamos",
    "vosotros": "ibais",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "iban"
  }}
/>

<BootTable
  label="ver"
  caption="imperfect"
  forms={{
    "yo": "veía",
    "tú": "veías",
    "él/ella/usted": "veía",
    "nosotros": "veíamos",
    "vosotros": "veíais",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "veían"
  }}
/>

None of these three trace a four-cell boot outline the way a stem-changing present-tense verb does ([Chapter 23](../lesson-23)) — every one of the six forms departs from what the regular endings would produce, including nosotros and vosotros, so there's no "unaffected" baseline left to draw a boot shape around. *Ser* and *ir* are true suppletive irregulars, built from historical roots unrelated to their infinitives — the same phenomenon behind *ir*'s present tense (*voy, vas, va...*). *Ver* is milder: it simply keeps its full stem (*ve-*) instead of shrinking to the bare *v-* that the regular ending would attach to.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A handy way to remember *veía* rather than a hypothetical shortened "vía": *vía* is already a real, unrelated Spanish word (a route or track, as in a train line). Keeping the fuller *ve-* stem conveniently keeps the verb from colliding with it.
</GrammarNote>

<ExampleSentence es="Cuando era joven, iba a la playa todos los veranos y veía el atardecer desde la arena." en="When I was young, I used to go to the beach every summer and watch the sunset from the sand." note="All three irregulars in one sentence: era (ser), iba (ir), veía (ver)." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
It's worth noticing what's *not* on this list. Verbs that are heavily irregular in the present tense — tener, hacer, querer, poder, estar, decir — are all perfectly regular in the imperfect (tenía, hacía, quería, podía, estaba, decía). The stem-changing "boot" verbs from Chapter 23 (pensar, dormir, pedir...) don't stem-change here either — pensaba, dormía, pedía, with no trace of piensa-, duerme-, or pide-. The imperfect is the one tense in Spanish where almost all of that hard-won irregularity knowledge simply doesn't apply.
</GrammarNote>

### Description in the past: setting the scene
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Because the imperfect doesn't mark a beginning or an end, it's the natural tense for describing what something was like — physical appearance, weather, mood, atmosphere.

<ExampleSentence es="La casa era pequeña pero muy acogedora." en="The house was small but very cozy." />
<ExampleSentence es="El cielo estaba gris y hacía viento." en="The sky was gray and it was windy." note="Estaba and hacía — both regular imperfect forms of present-tense irregulars, describing a scene rather than reporting an event." />

### Age and time in the past
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Two specific, very common description jobs deserve their own mention: stating someone's age, and telling the time, both always in the imperfect when set in the past.

<ExampleSentence es="Tenía diez años cuando aprendí a nadar." en="I was ten years old when I learned to swim." note="Aprendí is preterite — a single completed event — while tenía frames the ongoing background fact of being ten. Chapter 29 covers this kind of pairing in full." />
<ExampleSentence es="Eran las tres de la tarde cuando salimos." en="It was three in the afternoon when we left." note="Clock time in the past always takes the imperfect era/eran — the same logic behind ser for clock time in the present (Chapter 10)." />

### Habitual actions: the imperfect's home turf
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Repeated or habitual actions in the past — the closest match to English "used to" — are the single most common job the imperfect does. Time expressions like *todos los días, cada verano, siempre, normalmente, de niño/de pequeño,* and *mientras* signal it constantly.

<ExampleSentence es="Todos los veranos, mi familia y yo íbamos a la costa." en="Every summer, my family and I used to go to the coast." />
<ExampleSentence es="Mientras mi madre cocinaba, mi padre ponía la mesa." en="While my mother cooked, my father set the table." note="Mientras (while) is one of the imperfect's most natural companions — two ongoing actions happening at the same time, neither framed as finished." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Imperfect for politeness: softening a request by reaching into the past
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Spanish has a use of the imperfect that has nothing to do with the past at all: placing a present request one step back in time, purely to make it sound gentler and less direct.

<ExampleSentence es="Quería preguntarte algo." en="I wanted to ask you something." note="Not a statement about the past — said in the present moment, to soften the request. Quería sounds noticeably gentler than the blunter present-tense Quiero preguntarte algo." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Quería algo más?" en="Did you want anything else?" note="A very common thing to hear from a waiter or shop assistant in Spain — asked about a decision you're making right now, softened by the past tense." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
English has a narrow version of the same instinct — "I was wondering if..." uses a past-tense form to soften a present request, the same trick quería is doing here. English just doesn't extend it nearly as far: Spanish routinely reaches for quería, necesitaba, or podía in situations where English would simply stay in the present ("I need a favor," not a past-tense "I needed a favor").
</GrammarNote>

### Imperfect progressive: estaba + gerund for extra vividness
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Just as the present progressive pairs *estar* with a gerund ([Chapter 25](../lesson-25)), the imperfect of *estar* pairs with a gerund to frame a past action as vividly, visibly in progress — an optional extra layer of "you could picture it happening" on top of the plain imperfect.

<ExampleSentence es="Estaba lloviendo cuando salimos." en="It was raining when we left." note="Compare the plain imperfect Llovía cuando salimos — nearly interchangeable, but the progressive form leans harder into 'right in the middle of happening.'" />

## Bringing It Together

Faced with a past-tense sentence, reach for the imperfect whenever you're describing how things were rather than reporting what happened: a scene, an age, a time, a repeated habit, or — reaching outside the past altogether — a softened request in the present.

<ExampleSentence es="Cuando yo era pequeño, vivíamos en un pueblo donde siempre hacía buen tiempo. Todos los domingos, mis abuelos venían a comer con nosotros e íbamos todos juntos al parque. Yo tenía apenas seis años, pero me acuerdo perfectamente." en="When I was little, we lived in a town where the weather was always good. Every Sunday, my grandparents would come eat with us and we'd all go to the park together. I was barely six years old, but I remember it perfectly." note="Era, vivíamos, hacía, venían, íbamos, tenía — every verb imperfect, because the whole passage describes ongoing background and habitual routine, with no single completed event in view." />
