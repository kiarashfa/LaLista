The imperfect subjunctive is the subjunctive mood's answer to a simple question: what happens when a subjunctive trigger — a wish, a doubt, an emotional reaction, a recommendation — itself lands in the past? [Chapter 38](../lesson-38) built the machinery for triggering the subjunctive in the present tense; this chapter carries that exact same machinery one step back in time, using a new set of forms built not from the present tense at all, but from a tense you already know well: the preterite. Along the way, this chapter also hands you the single most deferential way to phrase a request in Spanish — *quisiera* — and opens the door, just a crack, onto the hypothetical *si*-clauses that [Chapter 41](../lesson-41) covers in full.

## Core Pattern: One Stem for Every Verb, No Exceptions

### Start from the preterite's ellos/ellas/ustedes form
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Every imperfect subjunctive form in Spanish — regular or irregular — is built the same way, with no separate irregular list to memorize: take the verb's preterite *ellos/ellas/ustedes* form ([Chapters 26](../lesson-26)–[27](../lesson-27)), drop the *-ron* ending, and attach a new set of personal endings to what's left. Because this stem already comes straight from the preterite, it already carries whatever irregularity the preterite has — you're not learning a second irregular pattern, you're reusing the first one.

<ExampleSentence es="hablar → hablaron → hablara" en="to speak → they spoke → (that) I/he/she/you spoke" note="Drop -ron from the preterite ellos-form, then add the imperfect subjunctive endings." />

### Two ending sets, same meaning: -ra and -se
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Spanish gives every imperfect subjunctive verb two complete, fully interchangeable sets of endings. Both mean exactly the same thing — there's no meaning difference to learn here, only a style/register one (below).

    "yo": "hablase",

    "nosotros": "hablásemos",

    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "hablasen"

/>

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Notice the written accent that appears only on the *nosotros* form — *habláramos*, *hablásemos* — never anywhere else in either paradigm. It always falls on the vowel immediately before *-ramos*/*-semos*, keeping the stress exactly where it already falls in every other form of the paradigm.
</GrammarNote>

The same two ending sets attach to -er and -ir verbs too, off their own preterite stem: *comer → comieron → comiera/comiese*; *vivir → vivieron → viviera/viviese*.

<ExampleSentence es="Si comiera más despacio, no le dolería el estómago." en="If he ate more slowly, his stomach wouldn't hurt." note="comiera — regular -er verb, -ra form." />

### -ra or -se: a real regional choice
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Both forms are used throughout the Spanish-speaking world, but not in equal measure. The *-ra* form is the everyday default nearly everywhere, including in spoken Castellano. The *-se* form, by contrast, is genuinely more common in Spain's formal and literary registers — legal writing, journalism, academic prose — than it typically is in Latin America, where *-ra* dominates almost across the board. Neither form is "more correct"; a learner aiming at natural spoken Castellano can lean on *-ra* by default and simply learn to recognize *-se* on sight.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Irregular Verbs Inherit, They Don't Reinvent

### The preterite's irregularity carries over unchanged
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Because the imperfect subjunctive stem comes directly from the preterite *ellos/ellas/ustedes* form, any verb that's irregular in the preterite is irregular here in exactly the same way — and, unlike present-tense stem-changing verbs, the irregularity spreads to *every* form, nosotros and vosotros included.

<BootTable
  label="tener"

  forms={{

    "tú": "tuvieras",
    "él/ella/usted": "tuviera",
    "nosotros": "tuviéramos",
    "vosotros": "tuvierais",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "tuvieran"
  }}
/>

As with the preterite stems themselves ([Chapter 27](../lesson-27)) and the future's irregular stems ([Chapter 33](../lesson-33)), there's no boot outline to draw here: *tuvie-* is irregular in all six forms at once, not just four of them, so the grid stays flat.

<ExampleSentence es="Si tuvieras más paciencia, esto sería más fácil." en="If you had more patience, this would be easier." />

The same shortcut extends to every irregular verb you already know from Chapter 27: *fueron → fuera* (ir and ser share this form, disambiguated by context exactly the way *fui* is); *dijeron → dijera* (the j-stem swallows the following *i*, the same spelling logic that gives you *dijeron* rather than *dijieron*); *trajeron → trajera*; *pudieron → pudiera*; *supieron → supiera*; *vinieron → viniera*; and *pidieron → pidiera*, *durmieron → durmiera* (the -ir stem-changers from Chapters 23/27, carrying their vowel change straight through).

<ExampleSentence es="Ojalá fuera tan fácil como parece." en="I wish it were as easy as it seems." note="fuera — from ser's fueron, not ir's — context makes the meaning ('were,' not 'went') clear." />

## Core Uses of the Imperfect Subjunctive

### Past-tense subjunctive triggers: the same WEIRDO categories, shifted back
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

[Chapter 38's](../lesson-38) WEIRDO triggers — wishes, emotion, impersonal expressions, recommendations, doubt, ojalá — don't stop requiring the subjunctive just because the sentence moves into the past. What changes is which subjunctive tense the subordinate clause needs: a present-tense trigger pairs with the present subjunctive, and a past-tense trigger pairs with the imperfect subjunctive instead.

<ExampleSentence es="Quiero que vengas a la fiesta." en="I want you to come to the party." note="Present trigger (quiero) → present subjunctive (vengas) — Chapter 38's territory." />
<ExampleSentence es="Quería que vinieras a la fiesta." en="I wanted you to come to the party." note="The exact same wish, now in the past (quería) → the subordinate clause shifts to the imperfect subjunctive (vinieras) too." />

The pattern holds across every WEIRDO category, not just wishes:

<ExampleSentence es="Dudaba que fuera verdad." en="I doubted it was true." note="Doubt, in the past." />
<ExampleSentence es="Me alegré de que ganaras el premio." en="I was glad you won the prize." note="Emotion, in the past." />
<ExampleSentence es="Era necesario que llegáramos a tiempo." en="It was necessary that we arrive on time." note="Impersonal expression, in the past." />
<ExampleSentence es="Nos recomendó que estudiáramos más." en="She recommended that we study more." note="Recommendation, in the past." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A quick trigger-tense test: whatever tense the WEIRDO verb in the main clause is in, that's your cue. Present, present perfect, future, or a command in the main clause generally pairs with the present subjunctive in the subordinate clause; preterite, imperfect, or conditional in the main clause generally pairs with the imperfect subjunctive instead. [Chapter 40](../lesson-40) formalizes this exact same logic for the compound subjunctive forms.
</GrammarNote>

### Quisiera: the softest way to ask for something
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

[Chapter 34](../lesson-34) introduced *querría* — the conditional of *querer* — as a softened, more polite alternative to the blunt *quiero*. *Quisiera*, the imperfect subjunctive of that same verb, pushes that softening instinct one notch further, trading the conditional for the subjunctive as its grammatical base.

<ExampleSentence es="Quisiera reservar una mesa para dos." en="I would like to reserve a table for two." note="A standard, universally polite way to open a request in a restaurant, an office, or a formal email." />
<ExampleSentence es="Quisiera preguntarle algo, si no le importa." en="I would like to ask you something, if you don't mind." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Querría*, *quisiera*, and plain *quiero* form a real politeness scale, not three interchangeable options: *quiero* is direct and neutral, *querría* softens it, and *quisiera* softens it further still — the most deferential and the safest choice in a genuinely formal context. Unlike this chapter's other imperfect subjunctive uses, *quisiera* here isn't responding to a WEIRDO trigger anywhere in the sentence — it's simply borrowed, on its own, as a fixed politeness form, the same way English "I would like" doesn't need a triggering clause either.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### A first glimpse of hypothetical si-clauses
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

The imperfect subjunctive has one more job, and it's a big one: it's the required form inside the *si*-clause of a hypothetical-present condition.

<ExampleSentence es="Si tuviera dinero, viajaría más." en="If I had money, I would travel more." note="tuviera signals the condition isn't real right now; viajaría (simple conditional, Chapter 34) is the hypothetical result." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is a preview only. The full system — all four *si*-clause types, which tense pairs with which, and why — is [Chapter 41's](../lesson-41) dedicated topic. For now, just recognize the shape: *si* + imperfect subjunctive signals "this isn't really true," and the main clause answers with a conditional.
</GrammarNote>

### -ra's other job: a literary stand-in for había
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

In formal and literary Castellano — especially journalism and legal writing — the *-ra* form (never *-se*) occasionally does indicative work, standing in for the pluperfect *había* + participle inside a relative clause, with no subjunctive meaning at all.

<ExampleSentence es="El equipo que ganara el campeonato del año anterior no pudo clasificarse esta vez." en="The team that had won the previous year's championship couldn't qualify this time." note="ganara here simply means había ganado — no wish, doubt, or hypothetical is involved. A literary usage worth recognizing, not one you need to produce yourself." />

### Common trap: the imperfect subjunctive isn't a stand-alone "past tense"
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Unlike the preterite or imperfect, the imperfect subjunctive never appears just because an action happened in the past — it appears only when something else in the sentence (a WEIRDO trigger, or a hypothetical *si*) independently demands the subjunctive mood. A past action with no such trigger stays in the preterite, imperfect, or pluperfect, exactly as [Chapters 26–31](../lesson-26) already taught.

<ExampleSentence es="Ayer hablé con mi jefe." en="Yesterday I talked with my boss." note="A plain past fact, no trigger — preterite (hablé), not the subjunctive hablara." />

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Mi madre siempre quería que yo estudiara medicina, y dudaba que fuera feliz haciendo otra cosa. Un día le dije: 'Quisiera hablar contigo de mis planes.' Si tuviera que resumirlo en una frase, le diría que prefiero ser feliz a tener un buen sueldo." en="My mother always wanted me to study medicine, and doubted I'd be happy doing anything else. One day I told her: 'I would like to talk to you about my plans.' If I had to sum it up in one sentence, I'd tell her I'd rather be happy than have a good salary." note="quería que...estudiara and dudaba que...fuera are past WEIRDO triggers; quisiera is the softened request; si tuviera...diría is the hypothetical-present si-clause previewed above." />

Whenever a subjunctive trigger sits in a past tense, or you reach for the gentlest possible way to make a request, or you build a *si*-clause about something that isn't really true right now — the imperfect subjunctive, built off nothing more exotic than the preterite stem you already know, is the form to reach for.
