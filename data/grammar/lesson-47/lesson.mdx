Some Spanish sentences have no real subject at all, and others deliberately hide a subject that could technically be named. English handles both situations with filler words and vague pronouns — "it's raining," "there are three books," "they say it's going to rain," "you never know" — and Spanish has its own dedicated toolkit for the same job, most of it built from small, unglamorous words that punch well above their size: *hay*, *hace*, *se*, and an unspoken *ellos*. This chapter gathers every strategy Spanish uses to build a sentence around an unspecified, generic, or entirely absent subject, and sorts out which tool fits which situation.

## Core Pattern: Impersonal *Hay* — Existence With No Subject at All

### Hay: there is / there are
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

*Hay* states that something exists, without ever taking a subject — it stays exactly the same shape whether one thing exists or a thousand do:

<ExampleSentence es="Hay un libro sobre la mesa." en="There is a book on the table." />
<ExampleSentence es="Hay tres libros sobre la mesa." en="There are three books on the table." note="Hay never changes to agree with libros — it has no subject to agree with." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
[Chapter 30](../lesson-30) already flagged this, and it's worth restating fully here: *hay* is never a stand-in for *he/has/ha/han*, the auxiliary forms of *haber* used to build the present perfect ([Chapter 30](../lesson-30)). *Hay tres cartas* ("there are three letters") and *He escrito tres cartas* ("I've written three letters") share a common ancestor in the verb *haber*, but they do completely different jobs and are never interchangeable.
</GrammarNote>

### Hay across every tense
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Existential *haber* isn't limited to the present — it conjugates across every tense Spanish has, always staying in this same invariant, subjectless third-person-singular shape:

| Tense | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present | hay | *Hay mucha gente aquí.* |
| Preterite | hubo | *Hubo un accidente ayer.* |
| Imperfect | había | *Había mucha gente en la fiesta.* |
| Future | habrá | *Habrá una reunión el lunes.* |
| Conditional | habría | *Habría más opciones si tuviéramos tiempo.* |
| Present subjunctive | haya | *Espero que haya suficiente comida.* |
| Present perfect | ha habido | *Ha habido varios cambios este año.* |

<ExampleSentence es="Había mucha gente en la fiesta, pero hubo un problema con la música." en="There were a lot of people at the party, but there was a problem with the music." note="había (ongoing background existence, imperfect) vs. hubo (a bounded event, preterite) — the same aspectual contrast Chapter 29 covers for every other verb, applied here to haber." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
A very common error, heard even from native speakers in casual registers: pluralizing *hay*'s past forms to match what follows it — *"Habían tres personas en la sala"* instead of *"Había tres personas en la sala."* Existential *haber* never agrees with anything, in any tense; treat *habían*, *habrán*, and similar plurals as nonstandard, however often you encounter them.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Hay Que — the Impersonal Obligation, Across Every Tense

### Hay que in every tense
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

[Chapter 45](../lesson-45) introduced *hay que* + infinitive as one of Spanish's three obligation constructions — impersonal, with no specific person named as responsible — and promised this chapter would give it the same full-tense treatment as existential *hay* above, since it's built from the exact same invariant verb form:

| Tense | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present | hay que | *Hay que reciclar más.* |
| Preterite | hubo que | *Hubo que llamar a un médico.* |
| Imperfect | había que | *Antes había que pedir cita por teléfono.* |
| Future | habrá que | *Habrá que revisar el contrato.* |
| Conditional | habría que | *Habría que avisar a los vecinos.* |
| Present subjunctive | haya que | *No creo que haya que preocuparse.* |

<ExampleSentence es="Habrá que esperar hasta mañana para saber los resultados." en="It'll be necessary to wait until tomorrow to know the results." note="habrá que — no subject at all, just like hay que in the present." />

For the full three-way contrast between *hay que* (impersonal), *tener que* (personal), and *deber* (soft advice) — the register ladder that makes these three genuinely different tools rather than synonyms — see [Chapter 45](../lesson-45); this chapter's job is just *hay que*'s own shape across every tense.

## Building Out: Weather & Time Impersonals

### Weather expressions with hacer
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Spanish describes weather with impersonal *hacer* — invariant third-person singular, with no subject standing behind it (there's no "it" doing the making, the way English's dummy "it" implies):

<ExampleSentence es="Hace frío en Madrid en enero." en="It's cold in Madrid in January." />
<ExampleSentence es="Hace mucho calor hoy." en="It's very hot today." />

A small number of weather verbs (*llover*, *nevar*) work the same subjectless way without needing *hacer* at all — they only ever exist in the third-person singular:

<ExampleSentence es="Llueve mucho en el norte de España." en="It rains a lot in northern Spain." />

### Es tarde, es temprano, and other impersonal ser expressions
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

*Ser* + adjective builds a related family of subjectless expressions about time and general conditions:

<ExampleSentence es="Es tarde — deberíamos irnos." en="It's late — we should go." />
<ExampleSentence es="Es importante llegar a tiempo." en="It's important to arrive on time." />

### Hace + time duration
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Hace* also builds time-duration expressions, another subjectless use of the same invariant verb form. Placed before a past-tense verb, it means "ago"; placed with *que* before a present-tense verb, it means an action has been going on for a stretch of time:

<ExampleSentence es="Llegué a España hace una hora." en="I arrived in Spain an hour ago." />
<ExampleSentence es="Hace dos años que vivo en Madrid." en="I've been living in Madrid for two years." note="Present tense on the Spanish side (vivo) where English needs a perfect ('have been living')." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is only enough to recognize the pattern — the fuller grammar of *hace...que* versus *desde* versus *desde hace*, including how each pairs with different tenses, belongs to [Chapter 50](../lesson-50). What matters here is simpler: *hace* + a time expression is one more member of this chapter's subjectless family, exactly like *hay* and the weather expressions above.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: Three Ways to Hide a Subject

### Two different senses of "impersonal"
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Everything covered so far — *hay*, weather *hacer*, *es tarde*, *hace* + time — shares one property: the verb has no subject slot at all. You could never attach a subject to *hay* even if you wanted to (*"Yo hay"* is simply ungrammatical, the same way [Chapter 45](../lesson-45) already flagged for *hay que*). That's one sense of "impersonal": grammatically subjectless.

The rest of this chapter covers a second, related but distinct sense: constructions Spanish uses to deliberately avoid *naming* a subject, even though the sentence structure could, in principle, have one. These constructions aren't missing a subject slot — they're filling it with something intentionally vague.

### Impersonal se: se dice que..., se vive bien aquí
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Se* plus a third-person-singular verb states something as a general truth or common belief, with no one in particular responsible for saying or doing it:

<ExampleSentence es="Se dice que va a llover este fin de semana." en="They say it's going to rain this weekend. / It's said that it's going to rain this weekend." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se vive bien en esta ciudad." en="You live well in this city. / Life is good in this city." />
<ExampleSentence es="Se puede aparcar aquí los domingos." en="You can park here on Sundays." />

This is a close cousin of [Chapter 46's](../lesson-46) passive-se (*se venden casas*), and it's worth being precise about the difference now that both are on the table. Passive-se agrees in number with the noun that follows it, because that noun is genuinely the grammatical subject (*se vende casa* / *se venden casas*). True impersonal *se* never agrees with anything, because there's no candidate for grammatical subject at all — *vivir* in *se vive bien aquí* has no object to promote, and the *que*-clause after *se dice* isn't a noun that could trigger agreement either way:

<ExampleSentence es="Se venden casas en este barrio. / Se vive bien en este barrio." en="Houses are sold in this neighborhood. / You live well in this neighborhood." note="venden agrees with casas (passive-se, Chapter 46); vive stays singular no matter what, because impersonal se has nothing to agree with (this chapter)." />

### Third-person-plural impersonal: dicen que...
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A third strategy drops the subject pronoun entirely and lets a bare third-person-plural verb imply an unspecified "they" or "people in general":

<ExampleSentence es="Dicen que va a llover este fin de semana." en="They say it's going to rain this weekend." note="Nearly interchangeable in meaning with Se dice que va a llover above — Spanish genuinely offers both routes to the same idea." />
<ExampleSentence es="Llaman a la puerta." en="Someone's knocking at the door." note="Literally 'they call at the door' — no specific ellos in mind, just an unidentified someone." />

This looks like ordinary pro-drop at first ([Chapter 11](../lesson-11) covers Spanish's general habit of omitting known subject pronouns), but the flavor is different: ordinary pro-drop omits a pronoun because the specific person is already clear from context or verb ending, while this impersonal use never had a specific person in mind to begin with — *ellos* here is genuinely nobody in particular, not merely unstated.

### Completing the impersonal-subject family: se, uno, and third-person-plural
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

[Chapter 19](../lesson-19) introduced *uno* as a third way of making a generic statement — *Uno nunca sabe qué va a pasar* — and flagged that it works differently from impersonal *se*: *uno* is itself the grammatical subject, sitting right where any other subject would, while impersonal *se* replaces the subject slot entirely rather than filling it with a placeholder word.

<ExampleSentence es="Uno se acostumbra a todo con el tiempo." en="You get used to everything, given time." note="uno is the subject; the se here belongs to the reflexive verb acostumbrarse (Chapter 15), a completely different se from this chapter's impersonal one." />

Lined up together, Spanish's three strategies for a deliberately vague human subject are:

| Strategy | Example | Full treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Impersonal *se* | *Se vive bien aquí.* | This chapter |
| Impersonal *uno* | *Uno nunca sabe.* | [Chapter 19](../lesson-19) |
| Third-person-plural impersonal | *Dicen que va a llover.* | This chapter |

All three make a statement about people in general rather than a specific individual, but they carry slightly different flavors: *se* reads as the most neutral, almost official register (recipe instructions, general rules, signs); *uno* reads more personal and reflective, often including the speaker in the generalization; the bare third-person-plural leans toward hearsay, rumor, or an event with an unnamed cause (knocking, a rumor going around).

## Bringing It Together

Faced with a sentence that has no clear specific subject, ask two questions in order. First: is there really no subject slot at all — are you talking about existence, weather, time, or duration? If so, reach for *hay*, *hace*, or *es* + adjective, and remember none of them ever conjugate to agree with anything around them. Second, if there is a subject slot but you want to leave it deliberately vague: is the sentence more like a general rule or fact (*se* + verb), a personal, reflective generalization that could include yourself (*uno*), or closer to hearsay/an unidentified someone (bare third-person plural)?

<ExampleSentence es="Hay mucha niebla hoy, así que habrá que conducir con cuidado. Dicen que va a mejorar mañana, pero uno nunca sabe con el tiempo — aquí se vive así, esperando a ver qué pasa." en="There's a lot of fog today, so it'll be necessary to drive carefully. They say it's going to improve tomorrow, but you never know with the weather — that's how life is here, waiting to see what happens." note="Five different impersonal strategies in one short passage: hay, habrá que, dicen que, uno, and se vive." />
