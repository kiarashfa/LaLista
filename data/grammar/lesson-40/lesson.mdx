The subjunctive mood has compound forms too, and they're built exactly the way every other compound tense in this book has been: a conjugated form of *haber*, plus a past participle. [Chapter 38](../lesson-38) taught you when a sentence needs the subjunctive at all; [Chapter 39](../lesson-39) gave you the subjunctive's simple past-tense form. This chapter adds the two compound members of the family — *haya* + participle, for a completed action viewed through the subjunctive in present time, and *hubiera*/*hubiese* + participle, for a completed action viewed through the subjunctive in past time — plus the sequencing logic that tells you, reliably, which one any given sentence actually needs.

## Core Pattern: Haya + Participle (Presente Perfecto de Subjuntivo)

### Haber's present subjunctive, plus a participle you already know
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

*Haber* is one of the handful of verbs with a fully irregular present subjunctive ([Chapter 37](../lesson-37)) — no flip-the-vowel shortcut here; these six forms simply have to be learned as their own small paradigm.

<BootTable

  caption="present subjunctive — auxiliary for the presente perfecto de subjuntivo"
  forms={{
    "yo": "haya",
    "tú": "hayas",
    "él/ella/usted": "haya",
    "nosotros": "hayamos",
    "vosotros": "hayáis",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "hayan"
  }}
/>

Attach any participle from [Chapter 30's](../lesson-30) list — the ten core irregulars (*escrito, hecho, dicho, visto, puesto, roto, vuelto, muerto, abierto, cubierto*), their compounds, or the regular *-ado/-ido* pattern — and you have the presente perfecto de subjuntivo: a completed action, inside a subjunctive-triggering sentence set in the present.

<ExampleSentence es="Espero que hayas terminado el informe." en="I hope you've finished the report." note="A present-tense hope (espero) about a completed action (hayas terminado)." />
<ExampleSentence es="Me alegro de que hayan llegado bien." en="I'm glad they arrived safely." />

## Building Out: Hubiera/Hubiese + Participle (Pluscuamperfecto de Subjuntivo)

### The imperfect subjunctive of haber, plus the same participle
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

*Haber's* own preterite is irregular — *hubieron* — so its imperfect subjunctive follows [Chapter 39's](../lesson-39) universal shortcut exactly like any other irregular verb: drop *-ron*, add the endings.

<BootTable
  label="haber"
  caption="imperfect subjunctive (forma -ra) — auxiliary for the pluscuamperfecto de subjuntivo"
  forms={{
    "yo": "hubiera",
    "tú": "hubieras",
    "él/ella/usted": "hubiera",
    "nosotros": "hubiéramos",
    "vosotros": "hubierais",
    "ellos/ellas/ustedes": "hubieran"
  }}
/>

<GrammarNote type="regional">
Just like every other imperfect subjunctive verb ([Chapter 39](../lesson-39)), *hubiera* has a complete *-se* counterpart — *hubiese, hubieses, hubiese, hubiésemos, hubieseis, hubiesen* — used identically, with the same Spain-leaning-formal/literary register tendency. *Si hubiese estudiado más...* is exactly as correct as *Si hubiera estudiado más...*
</GrammarNote>

Paired with a participle, this gives you the pluscuamperfecto de subjuntivo — a completed action viewed through the subjunctive, but pushed one step further into the past than *haya* + participle can reach.

<ExampleSentence es="Dudaba que hubieras terminado a tiempo." en="I doubted you had finished on time." />
<ExampleSentence es="No creía que hubiera llovido tanto." en="I didn't believe it had rained that much." />

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Sequence of tenses: which compound form does the main clause demand?
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

This is the real skill this chapter teaches: a WEIRDO trigger's own tense in the main clause determines which compound subjunctive the subordinate clause needs.

| Main clause is in... | Subordinate clause needs... | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present, present perfect, future, or a command (an indicative "now") | Presente perfecto de subjuntivo — *haya* + participle | *Espero que hayas terminado.* |
| Preterite, imperfect, or conditional (an indicative "past") | Pluscuamperfecto de subjuntivo — *hubiera*/*hubiese* + participle | *Esperaba que hubieras terminado.* |

<ExampleSentence es="Espero que hayas terminado." en="I hope you've finished." note="Main clause present (espero) → haya + participle." />
<ExampleSentence es="Esperaba que hubieras terminado." en="I was hoping you'd finished." note="Main clause imperfect (esperaba) → hubiera + participle — one full step further back than the example above." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is the exact same logic [Chapter 39](../lesson-39) introduced for the simple subjunctive tenses (present subjunctive with a present trigger, imperfect subjunctive with a past trigger) — this chapter just extends it one layer deeper, to completed actions.
</GrammarNote>

### Ojalá + hubiera/hubiese: regret about the unrealized past
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

*Ojalá* pairs with the pluscuamperfecto de subjuntivo to express a wish about something that's already, unchangeably, not the case — the grammar of "if only."

<ExampleSentence es="Ojalá hubiera salido antes." en="I wish he had left earlier." note="Ojalá is a subjunctive trigger — a wish about a past that can no longer be changed, distinct from a plain statement of fact with había." />

### Don't confuse hubiera/hubiese with había
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Three *hab-* forms look like close cousins and are genuinely easy to mix up until you've seen them side by side. *Había* ([Chapter 31](../lesson-31)) is indicative — it simply states a fact about what had already happened, with no trigger required anywhere in the sentence. *Hubiera*/*hubiese* (this chapter) is subjunctive — it only appears where something else already demands the subjunctive: a wish, a doubt, a hypothetical *si*-clause about the past. *Habría* ([Chapter 35](../lesson-35)) is a third, different animal again — the conditional perfect, the "would have" result clause of a hypothetical, never the *si*-clause itself.
</GrammarNote>

| Form | Mood & Tense | Job | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| había + participle | Indicative pluperfect | States a plain fact: something had already happened | *Sabía que ya había salido.* |
| hubiera/hubiese + participle | Subjunctive pluperfect | Needs a trigger: a wish, a doubt, or a hypothetical si-clause about the past | *Ojalá hubiera salido antes.* |
| habría + participle | Conditional perfect | The "would have" consequence of a hypothetical past condition | *Si hubiera estudiado más, habría aprobado el examen.* |

<ExampleSentence es="Sabía que ya había salido." en="I knew he had already left." note="Plain indicative fact — no trigger, so había, not hubiera." />
<ExampleSentence es="Ojalá hubiera salido antes." en="I wish he had left earlier." note="Ojalá triggers the subjunctive — hubiera, not había." />

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Espero que ya hayáis terminado los deberes; ayer esperaba que los hubierais terminado antes de cenar, pero seguíais trabajando a las nueve. Ojalá hubierais empezado más temprano." en="I hope you all have already finished your homework; yesterday I was hoping you'd finished it before dinner, but you were still working at nine. I wish you had started earlier." note="hayáis terminado (present trigger, present-perfect subjunctive), hubierais terminado (past trigger, pluperfect subjunctive), and ojalá hubierais empezado (regret about an unrealized past) — the whole chapter's sequence logic in three consecutive sentences." />

Facing a completed action inside a subjunctive-triggering sentence, look first at the main clause's own tense: present-family trigger, reach for *haya* + participle; past-family trigger, reach for *hubiera*/*hubiese* + participle. And whenever the sentence is really about regretting something that can no longer be changed, *ojalá* plus the pluscuamperfecto de subjuntivo is exactly the tool built for that job.
