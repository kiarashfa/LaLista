Coordinating conjunctions join two grammatically equal elements — two nouns, two adjectives, two verbs, two whole clauses — without turning either one into the other's dependent. That's what makes them structurally simple compared to [Chapter 52](../lesson-52)'s subordinating conjunctions, which build one complex sentence out of a main clause and a clause that depends on it. Spanish's coordinating set is short: *y, o, pero, sino, ni*. Two of them look like the easiest words in the language and hide a small, genuinely useful sound rule; a third hides a distinction that trips up English speakers more than almost any other single word choice in this book.

## Core Pattern: Y and O — Joining Equal Elements

### Y for addition, o for alternatives
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

*Y* ("and") and *o* ("or") join any two matching elements — words, phrases, or entire clauses:

<ExampleSentence es="Compro pan y leche." en="I'm buying bread and milk." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Quieres té o café?" en="Do you want tea or coffee?" />
<ExampleSentence es="Estudio por la mañana y trabajo por la tarde." en="I study in the morning and work in the afternoon." note="y here joins two full clauses, not just two words — the same conjunction does both jobs." />

Nothing about their meaning is more complicated than English "and" and "or." The only wrinkle — and it's a real one — is that both words change shape under a specific, predictable sound condition, covered next.

## Building Out: A Sound Rule, Not a Spelling Rule

### Euphony swap: y becomes e before an i-/hi- sound
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

When *y* directly precedes a word that starts with the sound /i/ — spelled *i-* or *hi-* — it swaps to *e*. The reason is purely acoustic: saying *y* (itself pronounced "ee") immediately before another word starting with the same "ee" sound produces an awkward run of identical vowel sounds, so Spanish substitutes a different-sounding word with the identical meaning.

<ExampleSentence es="Padre e hijo se parecen mucho." en="Father and son look a lot alike." note="hijo starts with the /i/ sound (hi-), so y becomes e." />
<ExampleSentence es="Fernando e Isabel unificaron España." en="Fernando and Isabel unified Spain." note="Isabel starts with a plain i-, triggering the same swap." />
<ExampleSentence es="Estudio geografía e historia." en="I study geography and history." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
The swap is about the following word's *sound*, not the following word's category — it applies whether that word is a noun, adjective, or anything else, as long as it starts with an /i/ sound. It also doesn't care what the first word is: *e* replaces *y* here no matter what comes before it.
</GrammarNote>

The rule has one clean exception, and it's worth knowing because it proves the rule is really about sound: words spelled with *hie-* at the start (a diphthong, pronounced more like "ye-") don't trigger the swap, because they don't actually start with a bare /i/ sound.

<ExampleSentence es="Compramos nieve y hielo para la fiesta de invierno." en="We bought snow and ice for the winter party." note="hielo begins with hie- (sounds like 'ye-lo'), not a plain i- sound, so y stays y — no collision to avoid." />

### The mirror rule: o becomes u before an o-/ho- sound
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*O* follows the identical logic, just with the opposite vowel: directly before a word starting with the sound /o/ — spelled *o-* or *ho-* — it swaps to *u*, again purely to avoid two matching vowel sounds landing back to back.

<ExampleSentence es="Necesito siete u ocho euros." en="I need seven or eight euros." note="ocho starts with the /o/ sound, triggering the swap." />
<ExampleSentence es="¿Prefieres un hotel u otro tipo de alojamiento?" en="Do you prefer a hotel or another type of lodging?" note="otro triggers the same swap." />
<ExampleSentence es="Necesitamos una mujer u hombre con experiencia para el puesto." en="We need a woman or man with experience for the position." />

And the same diphthong exception mirrors the *y* rule: words spelled *hue-* start with a "we-" sound, not a bare /o/, so they don't trigger the swap.

<ExampleSentence es="¿Prefieres tortilla de patata o huevos revueltos?" en="Do you prefer potato omelet or scrambled eggs?" note="huevos begins with hue- ('we-vos'), not a plain o- sound, so o stays o." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
This is the same kind of euphony rule you've already met twice in this book: *el* replacing *la* directly before *agua* ([Chapter 3](../lesson-03)) to avoid two stressed a-sounds colliding, and *le/les* becoming *se* directly before *lo/la/los/las* ([Chapter 14](../lesson-14)) to avoid two words starting with *l-* colliding. In every one of these cases, Spanish swaps in a near-identical function word purely to smooth out how the sentence sounds — the underlying grammar (the noun's gender, the pronoun's role, the conjunction's meaning) never actually changes. *Y→e* and *o→u* are that exact same phenomenon, just showing up in conjunctions this time.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: Pero, Sino, and Sino Que

### Pero: the default word for "but"
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Pero* is the ordinary, default translation of "but" — it introduces a contrast or an additional fact alongside whatever came before, including after a negative clause, as long as that negative statement isn't being directly overturned:

<ExampleSentence es="Quiero ir, pero no tengo tiempo." en="I want to go, but I don't have time." />
<ExampleSentence es="No tengo mucho dinero, pero soy feliz." en="I don't have much money, but I'm happy." note="The first clause is negative, and pero is still correct here — happiness isn't a replacement for money, just a separate, contrasting fact placed alongside it." />

### Sino: when the second clause replaces the first
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Sino* also translates as "but," yet it's grammatically off-limits except in one specific situation: the first clause must be negative, and the second element must directly contradict and replace whatever the negation just ruled out — not merely add a separate fact next to it.

<ExampleSentence es="No es rojo, sino azul." en="It's not red, but rather blue." note="The color isn't red — it's specifically, instead, blue. Blue directly replaces the rejected claim 'red.'" />
<ExampleSentence es="El problema no es el dinero, sino el tiempo." en="The problem isn't the money, but rather the time." note="Time is offered as the true replacement for the rejected claim 'money' — the same substitution pattern." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
The test that actually decides *pero* vs. *sino*: does the second clause **cancel out and replace** what the first clause just denied, or does it simply **add a contrasting fact** alongside it? Compare the two directly: *No es rojo, sino azul* (azul replaces the rejected "rojo" — sino) against *No es rojo, pero me gusta* (liking it doesn't replace or contradict "not being red," it's just an unrelated added fact — pero). Both sentences start with a negative clause; only the relationship to what follows decides the word.
</GrammarNote>

### Sino que: when the replacement has its own conjugated verb
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Plain *sino* introduces a word or phrase — a color, a noun, a preposition phrase — with no conjugated verb of its own. The moment the replacing clause needs its own finite verb, Spanish requires *sino que* instead of bare *sino*:

<ExampleSentence es="No quiero café, sino té." en="I don't want coffee, but rather tea." note="Bare sino — té is just a noun, no verb attached." />
<ExampleSentence es="No fue a la playa, sino que se quedó en casa." en="He didn't go to the beach, but rather stayed home." note="Se quedó is a fully conjugated verb, so que is grammatically required — sino alone can't introduce a clause with its own verb." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
This is a strict rule, not a stylistic preference: *No fue a la playa, sino se quedó en casa* is simply ungrammatical. As soon as a conjugated verb follows the substitution, *que* has to be there.
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A related, common idiom worth recognizing: *no solo... sino también* ("not only... but also") uses the same word *sino*, but without the replacement logic above — it adds a second true thing on top of the first rather than substituting for it. *No solo habla español, sino también francés* ("She doesn't only speak Spanish, but also French").
</GrammarNote>

### Common learner error: defaulting to pero after every negative
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

English "but" covers both jobs — plain contrast and direct replacement — so an English-speaking learner's instinct is to reach for *pero* every time a Spanish sentence happens to start negative. That instinct is wrong specifically in the replacement case:

<ExampleSentence es="No quiero café, pero té. → No quiero café, sino té." en="I don't want coffee, but tea." note="The first version is the exact error a word-for-word translation from English produces; sino is required because té directly replaces the rejected café." />

## Ni...Ni: Neither...Nor

### The paired negative conjunction
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

*Ni...ni* links two elements that are both being denied at once — "neither...nor":

<ExampleSentence es="No tengo ni tiempo ni dinero para el viaje." en="I have neither time nor money for the trip." />
<ExampleSentence es="Ni Juan ni María llegaron a tiempo." en="Neither Juan nor María arrived on time." note="When ni...ni leads the sentence, no is dropped entirely — the same rule that governs every negative word placed before the verb." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
That last point — *no* disappearing when a negative word comes before the verb, but being required when the negative word comes after it — isn't specific to *ni...ni*; it's Spanish's general double-negation rule, covered in full in [Chapter 54, Negation](../lesson-54). Treat *ni...ni* here as one more place that rule shows up, not a separate pattern to learn from scratch.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

Faced with a sentence that needs "and" or "or," reach for *y* or *o* by default, and only swap to *e* or *u* at the exact moment the very next word starts with the matching vowel sound — never based on spelling patterns elsewhere in the sentence, and never before a diphthong like *hie-* or *hue-* that only looks like a match. Faced with "but" after a negative clause, ask what the second half is doing: adding a separate, unrelated fact keeps *pero*; directly replacing what was just denied requires *sino* — or *sino que* the instant that replacement carries its own conjugated verb.

<ExampleSentence es="No quiero té ni café, sino agua; tampoco quiero quedarme, sino que prefiero salir ahora mismo, aunque llueva." en="I don't want tea or coffee, but rather water; I don't want to stay either, but rather I'd prefer to leave right now, even if it rains." note="Stacks ni...ni-style negation, sino, and sino que in one sentence — the aunque at the end is a preview of Chapter 52's territory, not this chapter's." />
