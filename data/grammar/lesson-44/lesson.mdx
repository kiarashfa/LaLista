Every irregular participle you've been building since [Chapter 30](../lesson-30) — *escrito, hecho, dicho* — has, so far, only ever done one job: sitting frozen right after a form of *haber*, refusing to agree with anything no matter what it's attached to. That's not the participle's whole story. The very same word can also step fully into an adjective's shoes, agreeing in gender and number just like any ordinary descriptive word (*la puerta abierta*, *las cartas escritas*), and it has one more job still — a more formal, more compact one — standing at the head of its own miniature clause with no conjugated verb in sight at all (*terminada la reunión, todos se fueron*). This chapter is where the participle gets treated as what it structurally always was: a verb form built to behave like an adjective, not just the fixed tail-end of a compound tense.

## Core Pattern: Building the Participle (A Quick Recap)

### Regular formation: -ado / -ido
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Drop the infinitive ending and add *-ado* for *-ar* verbs, *-ido* for *-er*/*-ir* verbs — the same rule [Chapter 30](../lesson-30) introduced for building the present perfect:

<ExampleSentence es="hablar → hablado, comer → comido, vivir → vivido" en="to speak → spoken, to eat → eaten, to live → lived" />

Nothing about this formation rule changes depending on what job the participle ends up doing — adjective, absolute construction, or compound tense. The form is always built the same way; only its behavior afterward differs, which is exactly what the rest of this chapter is about.

### The irregular participles: the same list, not re-taught
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

[Chapter 30](../lesson-30) already gave you the full irregular list, and it doesn't change here — every form below works exactly the same way whether it ends up frozen after *haber* or fully agreeing as an adjective:

*escrito, hecho, dicho, visto, puesto, roto, vuelto, muerto, abierto, cubierto* — plus their compounds (*descubierto, devuelto, deshecho, descrito, compuesto...*) and the small accented group (*leído, traído, oído, creído, caído*).

<GrammarNote type="tip">
If any of these forms feel unfamiliar, [Chapter 30's](../lesson-30) full table walks through where each one comes from, including why the compounds inherit their base verb's irregularity. This chapter assumes you have that list, not that you're meeting it for the first time.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: The Participle as Adjective

### Full agreement: -o / -a / -os / -as
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Outside of a *haber* construction, a participle behaves exactly like any other adjective ending in *-o*: it takes four forms, agreeing in gender and number with whatever it modifies. This is the single biggest contrast with everything you learned about participles in Chapter 30, so it's worth stating as directly as possible:

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Inside *haber* + participle, the participle is permanently frozen in its *-o* form — *hemos escrito las cartas*, never *hemos escritas*. The moment that same participle steps outside a *haber* construction, the freeze lifts completely, and it agrees like any adjective.
</GrammarNote>

<ExampleSentence es="Hemos escrito las cartas." en="We have written the letters." note="Invariant escrito — glued to haber, no agreement." />
<ExampleSentence es="Las cartas escritas están en el cajón." en="The written letters are in the drawer." note="escritas modifies cartas directly, like any adjective — full agreement." />
<ExampleSentence es="La puerta está abierta." en="The door is open." note="abierta agrees with the feminine puerta — a plain adjective use, not a compound tense." />

### Estar + participle: a resulting state, not a new tense
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

You met a preview of this back in [Chapter 21](../lesson-21): *estar* plus a participle describes the state something is *in* as a result of a completed action — not an action itself, and not a new compound tense. Because the participle here is functioning as an adjective, it agrees fully, exactly like the examples above:

<ExampleSentence es="Los documentos están firmados." en="The documents are signed." note="firmados agrees with the masculine plural documentos." />
<ExampleSentence es="La tienda está cerrada los domingos." en="The store is closed on Sundays." note="cerrada — a resulting state, not an action in progress." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Don't confuse this with the progressive from [Chapter 25](../lesson-25) (*estar* + gerund, e.g. *está cerrando*). The two constructions share the verb *estar* but attach a completely different non-finite form — a participle describing a finished state here, versus a gerund describing an action mid-motion there. [Chapter 43](../lesson-43) covers the gerund's own range of jobs in full.
</GrammarNote>

### Ser + participle: a first look at the passive
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

*Ser* plus a participle builds Spanish's true passive voice — the grammatical subject receives the action rather than performing it, and the agent (if named at all) follows *por*. The participle agrees here too, since it's still working as an adjective describing the subject:

<ExampleSentence es="La puerta fue abierta por el viento." en="The door was opened by the wind." note="abierta agrees with the feminine puerta; fue is ser, not estar." />

This is only a first look — the full passive system, including why Spanish often avoids it in favor of active or *se*-constructions, belongs to [Chapter 46](../lesson-46). What matters here is simply that *ser* + participle is a third context, alongside plain adjective use and *estar*, where agreement is fully active.

### After tener, dejar, and llevar: the participle as a completed result
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

A handful of other verbs pair with a participle to frame an action as already accomplished, with the participle still agreeing as an adjective would:

<ExampleSentence es="Ya tengo el informe terminado." en="I already have the report finished." note="terminado agrees with the masculine informe — tener + participle frames a completed result, distinct from haber + participle." />
<ExampleSentence es="Dejó la puerta abierta." en="He left the door open." note="abierta agrees with puerta." />
<ExampleSentence es="Llevo escritas tres páginas." en="I've got three pages written." note="escritas agrees with páginas — a result already achieved." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
That last example is easy to confuse with a completely different construction: *llevar* + **gerund** (*llevo tres horas escribiendo* — "I've been writing for three hours"), which describes ongoing duration and never agrees with anything, since a gerund is invariant. *Llevar* + **participle**, by contrast, agrees and describes a finished result, not an ongoing action. [Chapter 45](../lesson-45) gives *llevar* + gerund its own full treatment as a verbal periphrasis — worth a careful look precisely because the two *llevar* constructions look so similar on the surface.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: The Absolute Construction

### Terminada la reunión...: the participle heading its own clause
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Spanish has one more use of the participle that has no direct English equivalent in form, even though the meaning translates easily: a participle can open a compact clause of its own, with no conjugated verb at all, standing in for a full temporal or causal subordinate clause.

<ExampleSentence es="Terminada la reunión, todos se fueron." en="The meeting having ended, everyone left." note="Equivalent in meaning to Cuando terminó la reunión, todos se fueron — but built with no conjugated verb at all." />
<ExampleSentence es="Rota la promesa, ya no había nada que hacer." en="The promise broken, there was nothing left to do." />
<ExampleSentence es="Una vez firmado el contrato, empezamos a trabajar." en="Once the contract was signed, we started working." note="Una vez often introduces the construction to make the 'once X happened' sense explicit." />

The participle agrees with the noun that follows it — *terminada* agrees with *la reunión*, *rota* with *la promesa*, *firmado* with *el contrato* — the same adjective-agreement logic as everywhere else in this chapter, just applied to a noun that comes right after the participle instead of one it's tucked beside.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
Word order in this construction is fixed: the participle comes first, then the noun it agrees with. *La reunión terminada, todos se fueron* is not how this pattern works — the participle has to lead.
</GrammarNote>

### Fixed absolute-construction idioms
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

A few absolute constructions have hardened into set discourse expressions you'll meet constantly in writing and formal speech, worth recognizing even before you're ready to build new ones yourself:

<ExampleSentence es="Dicho esto, propongo que votemos." en="That said, I propose we vote." note="dicho esto — a fixed transitional phrase, extremely common at the start of a sentence that qualifies what came before." />
<ExampleSentence es="Dicho y hecho, tenían el proyecto listo en dos días." en="No sooner said than done, they had the project ready in two days." />
<ExampleSentence es="Visto lo visto, prefiero no opinar." en="Given how things have turned out, I'd rather not comment." note="visto lo visto — literally 'seen what's been seen,' an idiom for 'given the circumstances.'" />

### Absolute constructions vs. cuando/después de que clauses
<LevelBadge level="B2+" />

The absolute construction isn't a different meaning from an ordinary subordinate clause — it's the same meaning in a more compressed, more formal register. *Terminada la reunión, todos se fueron* and *Cuando terminó la reunión, todos se fueron* say the same thing; the participle version simply packs it into fewer words and reads as more literary or journalistic.

<GrammarNote type="regional">
You'll meet this construction constantly in Spanish news writing, formal reports, and literary prose — genuinely everyday in those registers, not an archaic curiosity. In casual speech, though, native speakers reach for *cuando* or *después de que* far more often; the absolute construction is a register choice, not a replacement for the ordinary clause in conversation.
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

<ExampleSentence es="Terminado el examen, los estudiantes salieron del aula. Las respuestas, ya corregidas, estaban listas al día siguiente. Dicho esto, el profesor anunció que había roto su propio récord de corrección rápida." en="The exam finished, the students left the classroom. The answers, already corrected, were ready the next day. That said, the professor announced he had broken his own record for fast grading." note="terminado (absolute construction, agrees with el examen), corregidas (adjective use, agrees with respuestas), dicho esto (fixed idiom), había roto (frozen participle inside a pluperfect, Chapter 31) — four different jobs for the same kind of word in three sentences." />

Faced with a participle, ask what it's actually doing: glued directly to a form of *haber* (frozen, never agrees), describing a noun or following *estar*/*ser* (a full adjective, always agrees), or opening its own clause with no conjugated verb at all (an absolute construction, agrees with the noun right after it). The form-building rules from Chapter 30 stay exactly the same across all three jobs — only the agreement behavior changes with the job.
