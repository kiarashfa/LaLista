Spanish marks whether a noun is singular or plural directly on the noun itself — not just on a following verb, the way English sometimes lets context do the work. Every noun, article, and adjective in a sentence has to agree in number, so once you know how a noun pluralizes, you know how everything attached to it has to change too. Most of the time this is genuinely simple — add an -s or an -es and move on — but a handful of common nouns pluralize in ways that trip up even careful learners, and a smaller set change their entire meaning between singular and plural. This chapter covers the reliable rule first, then works through every irregular corner worth knowing.

## Core Pattern: Adding -s or -es

### The default rule
<LevelBadge level="A1" />

Two simple rules cover the overwhelming majority of Spanish nouns:

- If the noun ends in a **vowel**, add **-s**: *casa → casas, coche → coches, libro → libros, estudiante → estudiantes.*
- If the noun ends in a **consonant**, add **-es**: *ciudad → ciudades, profesor → profesores, mes → meses.*

<ExampleSentence es="Los libros están en la mesa." en="The books are on the table." note="libro → libros: vowel ending, simple -s." />
<ExampleSentence es="Las ciudades españolas son muy antiguas." en="Spanish cities are very old." note="ciudad → ciudades: consonant ending, -es." />

Everything else in this chapter is a variation on one of these two rules, or a small set of nouns that don't play by them at all.

## Building Out: More Patterns

### Nouns ending in -z: z becomes c
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

Before adding -es, a final **-z** always changes to **-c** — a spelling rule, not an exception to the sound pattern:

*lápiz → lápices, luz → luces, vez → veces, pez → peces, actriz → actrices.*

<ExampleSentence es="Necesito dos lápices nuevos." en="I need two new pencils." />

### Nouns ending in a stressed vowel
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Nouns ending in a stressed **-á, -é,** or **-ó** just add -s, same as any other vowel ending: *café → cafés, sofá → sofás, dominó → dominós.*

Nouns ending in a stressed **-í** or **-ú** traditionally add **-es** (*rubí → rubíes, tabú → tabúes, hindú → hindúes*), but modern usage increasingly accepts the simpler **-s** form as well (*rubís, tabús, hindús*). Both are correct; -es is the more traditional, more formal choice.

### Stress shifts: when the plural needs a written accent
<LevelBadge level="B1+" />

Adding a syllable to a word can shift where the stress falls relative to the end of the word, which sometimes means the plural needs a written accent the singular didn't have — or loses one the singular had. The most common pattern: words ending in **-en** that are stressed on the second-to-last syllable of the *singular* keep that same spoken stress in the plural, but because the word is now longer, standard accent rules require marking it in writing:

*joven → jóvenes, examen → exámenes, imagen → imágenes, origen → orígenes, volumen → volúmenes.*

<GrammarNote type="tip">
The spoken stress doesn't actually move here — *JO-ven* and *JO-ve-nes* stress the same syllable. Only the spelling changes, because Spanish's accent rules require a written mark once that stressed syllable becomes three-from-the-end (antepenultimate) in a word ending in -s.
</GrammarNote>

Two well-known nouns go further and genuinely shift where the *spoken* stress falls: *carácter → caracteres* (stress moves from ca-RÁC-ter to ca-rac-TE-res, and the accent mark disappears because the new stress is now the regular, unmarked pattern) and *régimen → regímenes* (RÉ-gi-men → re-GÍ-me-nes). These two are worth memorizing individually — they're irregular even by the standards of this section.

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast

### Nouns invariant in number
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Nouns that already end in an **unstressed -s** don't change at all in the plural — only the article shows whether you mean one or many:

*el lunes → los lunes, el martes → los martes* (Monday–Friday; *sábado* and *domingo* pluralize normally: *sábados, domingos*), *el paraguas → los paraguas, la crisis → las crisis, el análisis → los análisis, la tesis → las tesis, la dosis → las dosis.*

<ExampleSentence es="El análisis fue rápido, pero los análisis anteriores tardaron más." en="The analysis was quick, but the previous analyses took longer." note="análisis is identical in both forms — only el/los changes." />

<GrammarNote type="warning">
Don't confuse this with words ending in a *stressed* -s, which pluralize normally: *el país → los países, el autobús → los autobuses, el inglés → los ingleses.* The rule only applies when the final syllable is unstressed.
</GrammarNote>

### Nouns that change meaning in the plural
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

A small set of nouns mean something different — not just "more than one" — in the plural:

| Noun | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| celo | *el celo* — zeal, diligence (also: heat, in animals) | *los celos* — jealousy |
| esposa | *la esposa* — wife | *las esposas* — wives, *or* handcuffs |
| deber | *el deber* — duty, obligation | *los deberes* — homework |

<ExampleSentence es="Pone mucho celo en su trabajo." en="He puts a lot of diligence into his work." />
<ExampleSentence es="Tiene celos de su hermano." en="He's jealous of his brother." note="Not the same idea as el celo — celos on its own means jealousy." />

<GrammarNote type="regional">
*Los deberes* (homework) is standard in Spain; Latin American Spanish more often uses *la tarea* instead, which doesn't have this same singular/plural meaning split.
</GrammarNote>

### Collective nouns: singular in form, plural in meaning
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Words like *la gente* (people), *la familia* (family), *el equipo* (team), and *la policía* (the police — the same word covered in [Chapter 1](../lesson-01) for its gender-change pair *el policía*) name groups but are grammatically singular, and take a singular verb:

<ExampleSentence es="La gente es muy amable aquí." en="People are very nice here." note="Not 'la gente son' — gente stays grammatically singular even though it refers to many people." />

This is a genuine trap for English speakers, since English treats "people" as plural (*people are*, not *people is*). Spanish agreement follows the noun's grammatical number, not the number of individuals it describes.

<GrammarNote type="tip">
When a collective noun is followed by "de + plural noun," everyday spoken Spanish sometimes shifts to a plural verb (*la mayoría de los estudiantes aprobaron el examen*), even though the strict, formal rule still calls for singular agreement with *mayoría* itself (*aprobó*). You'll hear both; writing formally, prefer the singular.
</GrammarNote>

### Pluralizing compound nouns
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

Compound nouns built from verb + plural noun (*el paraguas, el sacacorchos* — see [Chapter 1](../lesson-01)) are already invariant, since they already end in -s. A different compound type — two nouns placed side by side, the second describing the first — pluralizes only the *first* element:

*el coche cama → los coches cama* (sleeper car), *el coche bomba → los coches bomba* (car bomb), *el hombre rana → los hombres rana* (frogman), *el año luz → los años luz* (light-year), *el sofá cama → los sofás cama* (sofa bed).

### Pluralizing loanwords
<LevelBadge level="C1" />

Recently borrowed words often have more than one accepted plural, and usage is still settling:

- *el club* → *los clubes* (RAE-preferred) or *los clubs* (very common in practice)
- *el máster* → *los másteres*
- *el póster* → *los pósteres* (RAE) or *los pósters* (common)
- *el jersey* → *los jerséis* — the -y is treated as a vowel and Hispanicized to -éis, the same pattern as native *rey → reyes*
- *el email* → *los emails*

<GrammarNote type="tip">
A few borrowed words — *el software, el hardware* — are rarely pluralized at all in everyday use, treated more like uncountable nouns (compare "software" in English, which also resists a plural).
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

Given an unfamiliar noun, the default -s/-es rule is right often enough to lean on immediately. Check first whether it already ends in an unstressed -s (then it doesn't change at all), then apply -s for vowels or -es for consonants, remembering the z→c spelling swap and watching for an accent-mark shift on -en words. The genuine exceptions — meaning-change pairs, collective nouns, and compound/loanword plurals — are a short, memorizable list once you've seen them named.

<ExampleSentence es="El lunes, el equipo tiene que resolver varios problemas: los exámenes de los jóvenes empiezan el martes." en="On Monday, the team has to solve several problems: the young people's exams start on Tuesday." note="Stacks an invariant noun (el lunes), a collective noun with singular agreement (el equipo tiene, not tienen), a -ma plural (problemas), and two stress-shift plurals (exámenes, jóvenes) in one sentence." />
