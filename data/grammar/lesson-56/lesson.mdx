Every earlier chapter in this book has been about building one correct sentence: getting a verb ending right, choosing the right pronoun, picking *por* over *para*. This chapter is about something that sits one level up — how one sentence hands off to the next. A well-formed paragraph, in Spanish just as in English, needs small signal words that tell the reader or listener how a new sentence relates to what came before: is it a contradiction, a result, an extra point, a restatement, the next event in a story? These signal words — *sin embargo*, *por lo tanto*, *es decir*, and their relatives — are discourse connectors. Unlike the conjunctions from [Chapter 51](../lesson-51) and [Chapter 52](../lesson-52), they don't fuse two clauses into one grammatical sentence; they link two separate, complete sentences (or two independent ideas within a longer stretch of speech or writing) together. This small set of words is what makes writing and speech feel joined-up rather than choppy — which is exactly why it keeps mattering well past the beginner stage, showing up constantly in essays, news articles, and any conversation more considered than a quick exchange.

## Core Pattern: How a Connector Links Two Sentences

### The mechanics: two sentences, not one
<LevelBadge level="A2" />

A discourse connector typically opens the second sentence, followed by a comma, or sits embedded mid-sentence set off by commas on both sides. Either way, the two halves stay two separate sentences — each with its own subject and conjugated verb, each capable of standing alone — with the connector doing the work of showing how they relate:

<ExampleSentence es="No me apetece salir. Sin embargo, voy a ir porque se lo prometí a Marta." en="I don't feel like going out. However, I'm going to go because I promised Marta." note="Two complete sentences, joined by meaning rather than grammar — compare this to a conjunction, which would fuse them without a full stop: No me apetece salir, pero voy a ir..." />

That contrast with [Chapter 51](../lesson-51)'s *pero* is worth sitting with: *pero* builds one sentence out of two equal pieces, while *sin embargo* links two sentences that could each be printed with their own period. It's the same underlying idea of "connecting" applied at two different grammatical altitudes.

### Contrast: sin embargo and en cambio
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Sin embargo* ("however") sets up a contradiction — what follows goes against an expectation the first sentence created:

<ExampleSentence es="Quiero comprarme un piso en el centro. Sin embargo, los precios están por las nubes." en="I want to buy an apartment downtown. However, prices are through the roof." />

*En cambio* ("on the other hand," "whereas") is a gentler cousin: it sets two facts side by side for comparison rather than contradicting an expectation, and it reads naturally embedded mid-sentence as well as sentence-initial:

<ExampleSentence es="A mí me encanta el pescado; a mi hermana, en cambio, le da asco." en="I love fish; my sister, on the other hand, finds it disgusting." note="En cambio slots neatly between commas here, comparing two people's tastes rather than contradicting a stated expectation." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*No obstante* is a more formal, written synonym of *sin embargo* — common in essays, news writing, and official documents, less so in relaxed conversation. *Por el contrario* ("on the contrary") is stronger still: reach for it when the second statement doesn't just contrast with the first but actively reverses it.
</GrammarNote>

## Building Out: Consequence, Addition & Clarification

The same basic move — linking two sentences by naming their relationship — extends to logical relationships well beyond contrast. Three more families cover most of the rest of what a connected paragraph needs.

### Consequence: por lo tanto and its everyday cousins
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Por lo tanto* ("therefore," "consequently") states that the second sentence is a direct result of the first — a neutral-to-formal, extremely common connector in both writing and speech:

<ExampleSentence es="No estudié nada para el examen. Por lo tanto, suspendí." en="I didn't study at all for the exam. Therefore, I failed." />

The same relationship has cousins at other registers. *Por eso* is the everyday, neutral version:

<ExampleSentence es="Llovía muchísimo. Por eso no salimos de casa." en="It was pouring rain. That's why we didn't leave the house." />

*Por consiguiente* and *en consecuencia* sit at the formal, written end, often paired with a semicolon rather than a period:

<ExampleSentence es="El gobierno subió los impuestos; en consecuencia, el consumo se redujo." en="The government raised taxes; as a result, consumption fell." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Así que* behaves a little differently from the rest of this family — in relaxed speech it often glues directly onto the first clause with just a comma (*Se me ha estropeado el coche, así que hoy voy en autobús* — "My car broke down, so today I'm going by bus"), feeling almost like a coordinating conjunction. Treat it as the most colloquial, most tightly-fused end of the consequence family, with *por lo tanto* and *por consiguiente* sitting at the clearly separate, more formal end.
</GrammarNote>

### Addition: además and its family
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Además* ("furthermore," "besides," "also") adds a supporting or escalating point — stronger rhetorically than a plain *y*, which just adds items without signaling that the new point strengthens an argument:

<ExampleSentence es="El piso es luminoso y está bien comunicado. Además, el precio es razonable." en="The apartment is bright and well-connected. Furthermore, the price is reasonable." />

*Es más* escalates even harder ("what's more"), *encima* is its colloquial, often complaint-flavored cousin ("on top of that"), and *asimismo* is the formal, written register's version ("likewise"):

<ExampleSentence es="No llegó a tiempo, y encima se olvidó las llaves." en="He didn't arrive on time, and on top of that, he forgot the keys." note="Encima carries a mildly exasperated tone that además and asimismo don't." />

### Clarification: es decir and de hecho
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

*Es decir* ("that is to say," "in other words") restates or sharpens what was just said:

<ExampleSentence es="Voy a trabajar desde casa, es decir, no hace falta que me esperéis en la oficina." en="I'm going to work from home — that is, you don't need to wait for me at the office." />

*De hecho* ("in fact," "actually") does two related jobs: reinforcing a claim as even truer than it first sounded, or introducing a mild correction — a fact that turns out to be a little different, or more surprising, than expected:

<ExampleSentence es="Me encantó la película. De hecho, la vi dos veces." en="I loved the movie. In fact, I watched it twice." note="Reinforcing use — the second sentence proves the first one true." />
<ExampleSentence es="Pensaba que el examen sería fácil. De hecho, fue bastante complicado." en="I thought the exam would be easy. Actually, it was quite complicated." note="Correcting use — the second sentence overturns the expectation the first one set up." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Es decir* has a looser, more conversational cousin, *o sea*, that does much the same reformulating job in everyday speech. Because *o sea* also carries its own pragmatic flavor beyond pure clarification, it gets its full treatment in [Chapter 57, Hedging, Emphasis & Pragmatic Particles](../lesson-57) rather than being re-taught here.
</GrammarNote>

## Nuance, Exceptions & Contrast: Narrative Sequencing & Register

### A fifth job: stitching narrated events together
<LevelBadge level="B1" />

Everything so far has connected ideas by logical relationship. Connectors do the same linking job for narrated time — the words that make clear what happened, and in what order, independent of tense choice. [Chapter 32](../lesson-32) covers the tense backbone of a narrative (preterite, imperfect, pluperfect layered together); this family is the lexical glue that rides alongside that backbone:

<ExampleSentence es="Puse la mesa. Mientras tanto, mi madre terminaba de cocinar." en="I set the table. Meanwhile, my mother was finishing cooking." note="Mientras tanto marks simultaneity between two separate sentences." />
<ExampleSentence es="Todo estaba tranquilo. De repente, se apagaron las luces." en="Everything was calm. Suddenly, the lights went out." note="De repente flags an unexpected turn in the story." />
<ExampleSentence es="Llegamos tarde; entonces, decidimos coger un taxi." en="We arrived late; so, we decided to grab a taxi." note="Entonces here means 'so' — a colloquial, conversational stand-in for por lo tanto." />
<ExampleSentence es="Terminé de trabajar y luego fui al gimnasio. Después, me duché y cené." en="I finished work and then went to the gym. Afterward, I showered and had dinner." note="Luego and después are largely interchangeable 'then/afterward' markers — luego leans slightly more colloquial, después slightly more neutral." />
<ExampleSentence es="Discutimos durante horas sobre adónde ir. Al final, decidimos quedarnos en casa." en="We argued for hours about where to go. In the end, we decided to stay home." note="Al final closes out a sequence; finalmente is a more formal synonym." />

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Mientras* on its own is a subordinating conjunction that fuses two clauses into one sentence — full treatment in [Chapter 52](../lesson-52) (*Mientras yo ponía la mesa, mi madre cocinaba*). *Mientras tanto* is the connector version of the same idea: it opens its own sentence, linking two separate, complete statements instead of building one sentence out of two clauses.
</GrammarNote>

<GrammarNote type="tip">
*Entonces* does double duty: it can mean "then, next" in a strict time sequence, or "so, in that case" as a light conversational stand-in for *por lo tanto*. Context, not the word itself, tells you which job it's doing in a given sentence.
</GrammarNote>

### Choosing the right register
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Look back across the families above and a pattern emerges: every one of them has a colloquial end (*por eso*, *así que*, *encima*) and a formal end (*por consiguiente*, *en consecuencia*, *asimismo*, *no obstante*), with a neutral middle (*por lo tanto*, *además*, *sin embargo*) that works almost everywhere. Match the connector's register to the rest of your sentence — mixing *así que* into otherwise formal academic prose reads as jarring in the same way a stray slang word would in English. The full system behind register choice generally — not just for connectors — is [Chapter 55, Formality & Register](../lesson-55)'s job; this is one specific, common application of it.

### Punctuation: why a comma alone can't do the job
<LevelBadge level="B2" />

Because a discourse connector doesn't fuse two clauses the way a conjunction does, joining them with only a comma creates a comma splice — a genuine error, not a stylistic choice:

<GrammarNote type="warning">
*No estudié, por lo tanto suspendí el examen.* is exactly this mistake: a comma alone can't separate two independent sentences. You need a full stop or a semicolon before the connector, and — when the connector opens the second sentence — a comma right after it: *No estudié. Por lo tanto, suspendí el examen.*
</GrammarNote>

## Bringing It Together

Faced with two ideas you want to connect, ask what relationship the second one has to the first. A contradiction points you to *sin embargo* or *en cambio*; a direct result points to *por lo tanto* (or one of its register-matched cousins); an extra, supporting point points to *además*; a restatement or sharper truth points to *es decir* or *de hecho*; and the next moment in a story points to the narrative-sequencing family.

<ExampleSentence es="Mi hermano quería aprender a tocar la guitarra este año. Sin embargo, no tenía tiempo para practicar entre semana. Por lo tanto, se apuntó a clases los sábados por la mañana. Al principio le costó mucho. No obstante, después de unos meses, tocaba canciones enteras. Al final, decidió organizar un pequeño concierto para sus amigos." en="My brother wanted to learn guitar this year. However, he didn't have time to practice during the week. Therefore, he signed up for classes on Saturday mornings. At first it was hard for him. Nevertheless, after a few months, he was playing whole songs. In the end, he decided to put on a small concert for his friends." note="Five connectors from four different families, in a passage short enough to read as one continuous piece of narration: sin embargo and no obstante (contrast), por lo tanto (consequence), al final (narrative closure)." />
